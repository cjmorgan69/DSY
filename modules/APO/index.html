  • Coordinate time-critical transport with receiving facility notification

APO Clinical Knowledge — Tap to Expand

What is Acute Cardiogenic Pulmonary Oedema?

APO occurs when the left ventricle fails to pump blood effectively, causing fluid to back up into the pulmonary circulation. This fluid leaks into the alveoli, severely impairing gas exchange.

Classic Presentation

  • Severe dyspnoea — often sudden onset, worse lying flat
  • Orthopnoea — can only breathe sitting upright
  • Pink frothy sputum — fluid in alveoli
  • Crackles/rales — on auscultation bilaterally
  • Diaphoresis — cold, clammy, pale
  • Tachycardia & tachypnoea
  • Anxiety/distress — "sense of doom"
  • Hypoxia — SpO2 often <90%

Common Triggers

  • Acute myocardial infarction / ACS
  • Uncontrolled hypertension
  • Arrhythmias (especially AF with rapid ventricular response)
  • Medication non-compliance (heart failure patients)
  • Dietary indiscretion (high salt/fluid intake)
⚠️ PDE-5 Inhibitor Contraindication
GTN is contraindicated if patient has taken:
• Sildenafil, Vardenafil, or Avanafil — within 24 hours
• Tadalafil — within 48 hours

Concurrent use can cause life-threatening hypotension.
CPP-001-AO Protocol Summary — Tap to Expand

Core Management (AO Scope)

  • Posture: Sit patient upright — legs dependent if possible
  • Oxygen: High-flow via non-rebreather mask
  • Request clinical support
  • Consult EOC Clinician — who may advise GTN

GTN Administration (EOC Clinician Direction)

GTN 300 microg sublingual — only if ALL criteria met:
  • Systolic BP > 100 mmHg
  • Heart rate 60–120 bpm
  • No PDE-5 inhibitor use within:
    • 24 hours — Sildenafil, Vardenafil, Avanafil
    • 48 hours — Tadalafil

Ongoing Management

  • Monitor vital signs every 3–5 minutes
  • Repeat GTN as directed by EOC Clinician if vitals remain within limits
  • Consult EOC Clinician ANY time vitals fall outside limits
  • Prompt transport — notify receiving facility

BREAK POINT 5 Practical Skill / Discussion

Treatment Initiation & Medication Safety

⏸️ PAUSE — Facilitator Discussion + Practical Skills

Practical Skill — EOC Consultation:

Role-play the EOC call:

"EOC Clinician, this is [callsign] with a 72-year-old male in acute pulmonary oedema. He has known CCF, AF, and previous MI. He woke 30 minutes ago with severe dyspnoea, pink frothy sputum, bilateral crackles. Currently sitting upright on high-flow oxygen. Obs are: SpO2 89%, BP 152/94, HR 114 irregular. No PDE-5 inhibitors. Requesting authorisation for GTN. Note: We are approximately 30 minutes from nearest hospital."

EOC Clinician Response (Facilitator):

"Authorisation granted for GTN 300 microg sublingual. Monitor BP and HR closely. Repeat GTN in 5 minutes if BP remains above 100 systolic and HR 60–120. Call back if vitals fall outside those parameters. Expedite transport."

Practical Skill — GTN Administration:

  • Confirm patient identity and allergies
  • Confirm no PDE-5 inhibitor use (24/48hr rule)
  • Check BP > 100 mmHg systolic
  • Check HR 60–120 bpm
  • Administer GTN 300 microg sublingual tablet
  • Instruct: "Place under your tongue, let it dissolve"
  • Document time of administration
  • Reassess vitals in 3–5 minutes

Facilitator Prompts:

  • "Walk me through your EOC consult — what key information do you give?"
  • "What are the THREE criteria for GTN?"
  • "How do you administer sublingual GTN?"
  • "What are you monitoring for after GTN?"

Expected AO Reasoning:

  • GTN is a vasodilator — reduces preload and afterload
  • In APO, this reduces the workload on the failing heart
  • Must have adequate BP (>100 systolic) — GTN will drop it further
  • Monitor for hypotension, headache, dizziness

Key Teaching Points:

  • GTN in APO requires EOC Clinician authorisation — not autonomous
  • Three-point safety check: BP >100, HR 60–120, no PDE-5 inhibitors
  • GTN reduces preload/afterload — takes pressure off the failing heart
  • Repeat doses only with EOC direction and vitals within limits

Real-Job Translation:

"Your EOC consult needs to be crisp and complete. They can't see the patient — paint the picture quickly. And always confirm the three GTN criteria out loud before you give it."

BREAK POINT 6 Decision-Making / Discussion

Reassessment & Escalation Decision

⏸️ PAUSE — Facilitator Discussion

Facilitator Prompts:

  • "What do these observations tell you?"
  • "Is the patient improving or deteriorating?"
  • "Would you give more GTN? What do you need to do first?"
  • "What are your escalation triggers?"

Expected AO Reasoning:

  • Improving: SpO2 up, RR down, HR down, subjective improvement
  • BP drop is expected and appropriate with GTN
  • Still symptomatic — consider repeat GTN with EOC direction
  • Vitals still within GTN criteria (BP >100, HR 60–120)

Escalation Triggers (call EOC immediately if):

  • SBP drops ≤100 mmHg
  • HR drops <60 or rises >120 bpm
  • Deteriorating conscious state
  • SpO2 not improving or dropping
  • Increasing respiratory distress

Key Teaching Points:

  • Reassess every 3–5 minutes — APO can change rapidly
  • Improving trend is what you want — but don't relax
  • BP drop with GTN is expected — concern is if it drops too far
  • Always re-consult EOC before repeat GTN doses

Real-Job Translation:

"Improvement doesn't mean you're done. Keep monitoring, keep reassessing. APO patients can crash quickly if something changes. Stay vigilant."

BREAK POINT 7 Practical Skill / Discussion

Extrication & Transport Planning

⏸️ PAUSE — Facilitator Discussion + Practical

Facilitator Prompts:

  • "How are you getting Kevin to the ambulance?"
  • "What position must you maintain?"
  • "What's your transport priority?"
  • "Who needs to be notified?"
  • "How will you manage the 30-minute transport?"

Practical Skill — APO Patient Extrication:

  • Green carry chair — patient remains upright
  • DO NOT lay patient flat on stretcher
  • Stretcher positioned with head elevated (45-90°)
  • Maintain oxygen therapy throughout move
  • Move smoothly — minimise exertion for patient
  • Portable oxygen ready for transit
⚠️ Critical: APO patients MUST remain upright. Laying flat can cause rapid deterioration and potential cardiac arrest. Use carry chair → stretcher with head elevated.

Transport Considerations (30-minute journey):

  • Continue high-flow oxygen
  • Vitals every 3–5 minutes during transport
  • Repeat GTN as directed by EOC if required
  • Pre-notify receiving facility early
  • Have suction ready — pink frothy sputum may increase
  • Plan for potential deterioration en route
  • Ensure adequate oxygen supply for 30+ minute transport

Expected AO Reasoning:

  • Green carry chair maintains upright position
  • Patient should NOT walk — exertion increases myocardial oxygen demand
  • Transport is time-critical — don't delay
  • Pre-notify allows ED to prepare
  • 30-minute transport = continue active treatment, may need 2-3 GTN doses total

Key Teaching Points:

  • Position is therapy — never compromise it during extrication
  • Green carry chair is your friend for APO
  • Stretcher head UP before transferring patient
  • Rural transport time means you ARE the treatment for 30 minutes

Real-Job Translation:

"The move from house to ambulance is where things can go wrong. Plan it, communicate it, and keep that patient sitting up the whole way. That 30-minute drive means you're providing definitive care — this isn't just a taxi ride to hospital."

R — "Treated with upright positioning, high-flow oxygen, GTN 300mcg x2 with EOC authorisation. No PDE-5 inhibitors. Allergies: Nil. Needs ongoing monitoring and likely diuretics."

Facilitator Prompts:

  • "Deliver your ISBAR handover"

Key Teaching Points:

  • ISBAR keeps handover structured and complete
  • Include what you found, what you did, how they responded
  • Mention GTN given and PDE-5 status confirmed
  • Trend is important — improving vs deteriorating

Real-Job Translation:

"Your handover sets up the ED to continue care seamlessly. Be clear, be concise, and make sure they know the trajectory — this patient was critical but is improving with treatment."

Take-Home Messages

  • Position is treatment — sit them up, keep them up
  • Pink frothy sputum = APO until proven otherwise
  • High-flow oxygen early — don't delay
  • GTN requires EOC authorisation and three-point safety check
  • PDE-5 inhibitor question is mandatory — ask it every time
  • Reassess every 3–5 minutes — APO changes quickly
  • Never lay an APO patient flat — green carry chair to elevated stretcher
  • Rural transport = extended treatment time — you ARE the care

Optional Scenario Variations

Variation 1: PDE-5 Inhibitor Positive

Patient admits to taking Viagra 18 hours ago. GTN is contraindicated. Management: Position, oxygen, request clinical support, expedite transport. Discuss alternative management with EOC.

Variation 2: Hypotensive APO

Patient presents with BP 88/60 — cardiogenic shock. GTN contraindicated. Position, oxygen, urgent transport. Discuss escalation pathway and potential MedSTAR involvement.

Variation 3: Deterioration Post-GTN

After first GTN, BP drops to 94/58. Patient becomes more drowsy. Discuss withholding further GTN, EOC escalation, and urgent transport priorities.

Variation 4: Extended Transport (60+ minutes)

Same presentation but 60-90 minutes from hospital. Discuss extended transport management, GTN dosing strategy, oxygen supply management, and MedSTAR/retrieval considerations.
