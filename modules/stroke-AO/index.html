Stroke — AO Walk-Through Scenario

CPP-016-AO | ROSIER Assessment & Time-Critical Transport

Title & Context

Scenario: 78-year-old male — acute onset facial droop, right-sided weakness, and speech difficulty while having morning tea. Wife witnessed onset approximately 45 minutes ago.

Protocol: CPP-016-AO Stroke — AO (SAAS)

Setting: Residential property, Coffin Bay, Lower Eyre Peninsula. Mid-morning. Nearest STS facility is Port Lincoln Hospital (approximately 35 minutes transport).

Crew: 2-person Volunteer AO crew

Time-Critical Principle: This scenario is built around the thrombolysis eligibility window. Every minute on scene matters. Rapid ROSIER assessment, BGL exclusion, EOC consultation, and direct transport to Port Lincoln Hospital STS are the priorities. "Time is brain."

Learning Objectives

  • Recognise clinical features of acute stroke and differentiate from stroke mimics
  • Perform a ROSIER assessment accurately and interpret the score
  • Measure BGL to exclude hypoglycaemia as a stroke mimic
  • Apply CPP-016-AO thrombolysis eligibility criteria correctly
  • Consult EOC Clinician for authorisation to transport to CSU/STS
  • Obtain required patient identification and contact details per protocol
  • Notify Port Lincoln Hospital STS stroke coordinator using ISBAR
  • Manage a time-critical, low-intervention patient requiring rapid extrication and transport

ROSIER Scale — Practical Application

The ROSIER (Recognition of Stroke in the Emergency Room) scale is scored as follows. Apply each item to this patient:

ROSIER ItemFindingScore
Loss of consciousness or syncope?No — patient was conscious throughout0

ROSIER Interpretation: A total score > 0 suggests stroke is likely. Frank's score of +4 is a strong positive. Combined with acute onset and no loss of consciousness or seizure activity, this presentation is highly consistent with acute stroke.

Thrombolysis Eligibility Checklist (CPP-016-AO)

All four criteria must be met simultaneously:

  • Positive ROSIER score — Yes, score +4
  • Independent pre-morbid functioning — Yes, Margaret confirms fully independent
  • <4 hours from symptom onset to CSU/STS arrival — Yes, onset ~45 mins ago + ~15 mins on scene + ~35 mins transport = ~95 mins total (well under 4 hours)
  • <60 minutes travel time to CSU/STS — Yes, Port Lincoln Hospital STS is ~35 minutes
Decision: All four criteria are met. This patient is eligible for thrombolysis consideration. The next step per CPP-016-AO is to consult EOC Clinician for authorisation to transport directly to Port Lincoln Hospital STS.

Key Teaching Points

  • ROSIER is the SAAS-preferred tool — not FAST. Know it and be able to apply it on scene
  • The negative scoring items (LOC, seizure) are important — they reduce the score and may point to non-stroke causes
  • "NEW ACUTE" means these are new symptoms, not pre-existing deficits from a previous stroke
  • Pre-morbid independence is a clinical judgement — ask the family directly: "Was he fully independent before today?"
  • You must calculate the time from onset to expected arrival at STS, not just time on scene
Real-Job Translation: This is where the scenario lives or dies. Getting the ROSIER right and identifying thrombolysis eligibility is the highest-value clinical decision you will make on this job. Everything after this is execution — moving the patient efficiently to Port Lincoln.

Basic Care & Life Support (per CPP-016-AO)

  • BGL measured — 6.2 mmol/L — hypoglycaemia excluded
  • ROSIER completed — score +4 — positive
  • Airway maintained — patient managing own airway, no suction required
  • Position patient comfortably — 30° head elevation if tolerated
  • Protect affected (right) limbs — support arm, ensure leg is secure
  • Keep patient nil by mouth — aspiration risk with dysarthria
  • Maintain normothermia — blanket if needed
  • Reassure patient and Margaret — explain what you are doing
Do NOT Administer:
  • Aspirin — contraindicated until CT scan at hospital excludes haemorrhagic stroke. Even though Frank takes Warfarin for AF, stroke type is unknown pre-hospital
  • GTN — do not lower blood pressure in acute stroke
  • Oral fluids or medications — nil by mouth (aspiration risk)
  • Supplemental oxygen — SpO2 96% (only if <94%)

EOC Clinician Consultation — Skill Practice

Per CPP-016-AO: Consult EOC Clinician for authorisation to transport patient directly to Port Lincoln Hospital STS for consideration for thrombolysis.

Structure your call:

  • "This is [name], Volunteer AO, Coffin Bay. I have a 78-year-old male with acute onset stroke symptoms approximately 50 minutes ago."
  • "ROSIER score is positive at +4 — facial weakness, arm and leg weakness, speech disturbance."
  • "BGL 6.2, no hypoglycaemia. Patient was independently functioning pre-morbid."
  • "Port Lincoln Hospital STS is approximately 35 minutes transport. Estimated arrival at STS well within 4 hours of onset."
  • "Requesting authorisation to transport directly to Port Lincoln Hospital STS for thrombolysis consideration."
  • "PMHx includes AF on Warfarin — hospital will need to consider this."

Required Information to Obtain (CPP-016-AO)

When transporting an eligible patient to CSU/STS, you must obtain and confirm:

Real-Job Translation: This is a low-intervention, high-decision job. You are not giving medications or performing procedures. Your value is in getting the assessment right, making the correct transport decision, and communicating effectively with EOC and the receiving hospital. That is the clinical skill here.

Clinical Support Trigger (CPP-016-AO)

Per protocol: "Request clinical support if patient unstable or transport time >20 mins."

  • Patient is currently stable — observations unchanged, GCS maintained
  • Transport time to Port Lincoln Hospital is approximately 35 minutes — this exceeds 20 minutes
  • Therefore: clinical support should be requested even though the patient appears stable
Important: Clinical support does not necessarily mean waiting for backup. Deterioration Red Flags

  • Decreasing GCS — may indicate evolving stroke or raised intracranial pressure
  • Vomiting — aspiration risk, lateral positioning required
  • Seizure activity — changes ROSIER score, manage airway
  • Loss of airway or respiratory compromise
  • Rapid rise in blood pressure (e.g., >220 systolic)
Real-Job Translation: On a rural job with 35 minutes of road ahead, you need to know what you are watching for. Frank is stable now, but stroke can evolve. Reassess every 5 minutes en route. If GCS drops or he vomits, you need to manage the airway and call EOC immediately.

Reference — ROSIER Scale

The ROSIER (Recognition of Stroke in the Emergency Room) scale is the SA state-wide stroke clinical network's preferred stroke recognition tool.

ItemYesNo
Loss of consciousness or syncope-10
Seizure activity-10
NEW ACUTE asymmetric facial weakness+10
NEW ACUTE asymmetric arm weakness+10
NEW ACUTE asymmetric leg weakness+10
NEW ACUTE speech disturbance+10
NEW ACUTE visual field defect+10

Score range: -2 to +5

Interpretation: Total score > 0 suggests stroke is likely.

Key points:

  • Seizure and LOC score negatively — they may suggest non-stroke causes (e.g., epilepsy, vasovagal)
  • "NEW ACUTE" means the deficits are new — not residual from a previous event
  • ROSIER is used alongside a thorough history and clinical assessment, not as a standalone tool
  • BGL must be measured to exclude hypoglycaemia before interpreting the ROSIER score

Eligible for Thrombolysis — All Four Must Be Met:

  1. Positive ROSIER score (total > 0)
  2. Independent pre-morbid functioning
  3. <4 hours from symptom onset to CSU/STS arrival
  4. <60 minutes travel time to CSU/STS

Action When Eligible:

  • Consult EOC Clinician for authorisation to transport directly to CSU/STS
  • Request clinical support if patient unstable or transport time >20 mins
  • Obtain patient ID and family/carer contact details
  • Notify stroke coordinator via mobile phone using ISBAR

Not Eligible for Thrombolysis If:

  • Negative ROSIER score (≤ 0); or
  • ≥ 4 hours since symptom onset; or
  • Diminished pre-morbid independent living

If not eligible: transport to closest CSU/STS, or closest hospital if travel time is excessive, or consult EOC Clinician for advice.

SA Stroke Thrombolysis Services

  • CSUs: Royal Adelaide Hospital (24hr), Flinders Medical Centre (0800–2000), Lyell McEwin Hospital (0800–2000)
  • STS: Mount Gambier, Naracoorte (0800–1700 Mon–Wed & Fri), Port Augusta, Port Lincoln, Port Pirie, Riverland General (Berri), Wallaroo (0830–1530 Mon–Fri), Whyalla
Note: Some STS facilities have limited operating hours. Port Lincoln Hospital STS does not have published restricted hours in the current protocol, but always confirm availability via EOC or direct contact if attending outside business hours.

Not every presentation with neurological symptoms is a stroke. Common mimics to consider:

  • Hypoglycaemia — BGL <3.5 mmol/L can cause focal neurological deficits identical to stroke. Always check BGL first (CPP-016-AO mandate)
  • Seizure with post-ictal deficit (Todd's paresis) — unilateral weakness after a seizure that resolves over hours. ROSIER score reduces with seizure history (-1)
  • Migraine with aura — can cause transient speech difficulty, visual changes, weakness. Usually younger patients with migraine history
  • Syncope / presyncope — brief LOC with rapid recovery. ROSIER reduces with LOC (-1)
  • Bell's palsy — isolated facial weakness (forehead affected in Bell's, spared in stroke). No arm/leg weakness, no speech disturbance
  • Space-occupying lesion — tumour causing progressive symptoms over days/weeks, not acute onset

BGL measurement and the ROSIER scale's negative scoring items (LOC, seizure) are built into the protocol specifically to help differentiate stroke from mimics.

Take-Home Messages

1. Time is brain. Every minute of delay in a thrombolysis-eligible stroke reduces the chance of a good outcome. Your scene time should be as short as safely possible.
2. ROSIER is your tool — know it cold. The ROSIER scale is the SAAS-required stroke screening tool. Practise it until you can apply it without looking at a reference card.
3. BGL first, always. Hypoglycaemia is the most common and most treatable stroke mimic. A 10-second finger prick can change the entire management plan.
4. No aspirin for stroke. Unlike chest pain, aspirin is contraindicated pre-hospital in stroke until a CT scan excludes haemorrhagic stroke. Giving aspirin to a bleeding brain is catastrophic.
5. Your clinical value is in the decision, not the procedure. Stroke is a low-intervention, high-decision job. Getting the ROSIER right, identifying eligibility, consulting EOC, and notifying the stroke coordinator is the skill.
6. Collect the details on scene. Patient ID, family contact number, medication list — the protocol specifically requires these. Do it before you leave the house, not in the ambulance.
7. Port Lincoln Hospital is your STS. Know your local stroke-capable facility, know the travel time, and know how to contact the stroke coordinator. This should be automatic, not something you look up on scene.

Margaret woke at 0630 and found Frank on the bathroom floor with right-sided weakness and speech difficulty. He was normal when he went to bed at 2200 the night before. Onset time is unknown.

Key change: Cannot confirm <4 hours from onset. Patient is not eligible for thrombolysis per CPP-016-AO. Transport to closest hospital or consult EOC for advice. Still assess with ROSIER and provide basic care.

Variation 2 — BGL 2.8 mmol/L (Hypoglycaemia Mimic)

Same presentation, but BGL returns 2.8 mmol/L. Frank takes Metformin and missed dinner last night.

Key change: Treat hypoglycaemia per CPP-009-AO before proceeding with ROSIER. If symptoms resolve with BGL correction, this was a stroke mimic, not a stroke. If symptoms persist despite normal BGL, proceed with ROSIER assessment.

Variation 3 — Deterioration En Route

Ten minutes into transport, Frank vomits and his GCS drops to 10 (E3 V2 M5). SpO2 drops to 91%.

Key change: Manage airway (lateral position, suction), apply oxygen to target SpO2 ≥94%, contact EOC Clinician immediately, continue transport. This patient is now unstable — clinical support is critical. Update stroke coordinator with new clinical status.

Variation 4 — After-Hours Presentation (2200hrs)

Same patient, same symptoms, but it is 2200hrs. Port Lincoln Hospital STS availability after hours is uncertain.

Key change: Consult EOC Clinician to confirm STS availability at Port Lincoln at this time. If STS is not available after hours, EOC may direct transport to closest ED with telemedicine stroke assessment capability, or arrange MedSTAR consultation. The protocol still applies — follow the eligibility criteria and let EOC guide the transport decision.
