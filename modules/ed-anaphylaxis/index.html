Anaphylaxis in the Rural ED

Simulation Scenario — Walk-Through & Skills Practice

The Sting That Won't Settle

Refractory Anaphylaxis — Bee Sting in a Beta-Blocked Patient

A 52-year-old male farmer presents to a small rural hospital ED on the lower Eyre Peninsula after a bee sting. He is becoming progressively unwell. The GP is on call from home, 20 minutes away. You are it.

Setting
Small rural hospital ED, lower Eyre Peninsula, SA. Level 5 facility.
Day / Time
Saturday, 1430 hrs — January (mid-summer)
Staffing
1 RN, 1 EN on shift. GP/VMO on call from home (20 min ETA).
ED Status
Department clear. No other patients. EN restocking; RN at triage desk.
Nearest Regional Centre
60 minutes by road once ambulance arrives. Port Lincoln paramedics: 45 min response.
Retrieval
MedSTAR via RFDS fixed-wing from Adelaide. Estimated 1.5 hr response to bedside.

Learning Objectives

  1. Recognise anaphylaxis using ASCIA diagnostic criteria, including presentations without typical skin features.
  2. Assign the correct ATS triage category and initiate time-critical interventions, including nurse-initiated IM adrenaline.
  3. Apply the HIRAID framework to structure assessment, identify red flags, and prioritise interventions in a 2-person nursing team.
  4. Correctly prepare and administer IM adrenaline 1:1000 (ampoule and syringe), repeat dosing at 5-minute intervals, and escalate to an IV adrenaline infusion using the pre-hospital protocol.
  5. Recognise refractory anaphylaxis in a beta-blocked patient and identify glucagon as a rescue agent.
  6. Demonstrate structured communication using ISBAR: phone call to on-call GP, MedSTAR retrieval referral, and clinical handover to retrieval team.
  7. Coordinate roles between RN, EN, and GP within their respective scopes of practice in a resource-limited, time-critical scenario.
  8. Prepare a patient for retrieval, including transfer documentation, ongoing monitoring, and contingency planning for delayed retrieval.

Pre-Brief

  • Walk participants through the sim environment. Identify: resus bay, resuscitation trolley, cardiac monitor, oxygen outlets, phone location, medication imprest.
  • Confirm the resus trolley has been checked and adrenaline 1:1000 ampoules are present and in-date.
  • Identify the ASCIA anaphylaxis management poster on the wall — this is your protocol reference during the scenario.
  • Explain that the manikin (or simulated patient) will present with progressive symptoms. Facilitator will voice the patient and provide vital signs when requested.
  • Note: the GP is not on site at scenario start. The GP role-player will be available by phone and will physically arrive at the 15–20 minute mark.

RN — Triage / Primary Nurse / Clinical Lead (until GP arrives)

  • First contact with the patient at triage
  • Conducts ATS triage and initiates HIRAID assessment
  • Initiates nurse-initiated IM adrenaline under SA Health clinical directive / nursing clinical guideline
  • Directs the EN and allocates tasks
  • Communicates with GP by phone (ISBAR)
  • Leads clinical care until GP arrives, then supports GP as primary nurse

EN — Secondary / Assisting Nurse

  • Works under direct supervision of the RN at all times
  • Sets up monitoring (cardiac monitor, SpO2, NIBP)
  • Records observations and documents interventions with times
  • Prepares equipment: draws up adrenaline for RN to check and administer; prepares IV access equipment; spikes fluid bags
  • Assists with IV cannulation if competent and directed by RN
  • Makes phone calls as directed (GP, ambulance, MedSTAR) — uses hands-free phone where possible
  • Positions and reassures the patient
  • Does not independently administer S4/S8 medications without RN direction and supervision

GP / VMO — On-Call Doctor

  • Available by phone from scenario start; physically arrives at ~15–20 minutes
  • Provides phone orders for initial management (confirms adrenaline, orders IV fluids)
  • On arrival: assumes clinical lead role — assessment, prescribing, escalation decisions
  • Initiates MedSTAR referral and specialist consultation
  • Decides on adrenaline infusion, glucagon, further interventions
  • Leads clinical handover to retrieval team

Presentation & Triage Phase

Mode of Arrival

Self-presentation. Patient arrives in his wife's ute, driven to the front entrance. Wife runs in: “My husband's been stung by a bee — he's swelling up and he can't breathe properly. Please help.”

Keith is walking in, leaning on his wife. He is flushed, visibly swollen around the face, and you can hear audible wheeze from the doorway. He is talking in short sentences.

No Ambulance Handover

There is no paramedic ISBAR. History must be obtained from the patient and his wife. This is typical of a rural self-presentation.

What the Wife Tells You

  • “He was working in the top paddock, got stung on the forearm about 25 minutes ago.”
  • “He started getting hives and his face puffed up. Then he said his throat felt funny.”
  • “He's allergic to bees — he had a reaction a few years back but it wasn't this bad.”
  • “He doesn't have an EpiPen. The doctor told him to get one but he never did.”
  • “He takes a blood pressure tablet — metoprolol, I think — and a puffer for his asthma sometimes.”
Break Point 1 Decision-Making

Triage Decision

Facilitator: PAUSE the scenario. Ask the RN participant:

  • “What ATS category are you assigning and why?”
  • “Where is this patient going — waiting room, acute bay, or resus?”
  • “What are your immediate actions before you even get him onto a bed?”

Expected Clinical Reasoning

ATS Category 2 — Imminently Life-Threatening. The patient has features of anaphylaxis (urticaria + angioedema + respiratory compromise + haemodynamic compromise). ATS 2 requires clinical assessment and treatment within 10 minutes. However, in practice this presentation demands immediate action — treat as a functional ATS 1.

If hypotension, stridor, or loss of consciousness develop at any point, formally escalate to ATS Category 1.

Immediate Actions

  • Do NOT allow patient to walk further. Assist onto trolley immediately at triage. Use a wheelchair if needed to get to resus — do not let him walk.
  • Call EN to resus bay immediately: “I need you in resus now — anaphylaxis.”
  • Lay patient flat. If breathing is difficult, allow to sit with legs outstretched. Do NOT sit him in a chair.
  • RN: locate adrenaline from resus trolley. Prepare 0.5mL of 1:1000 (0.5mg) IM.
  • EN: begin setting up monitoring (cardiac monitor, SpO2, NIBP).
  • Call GP on call: “I have an anaphylaxis in resus. I'm giving adrenaline. Please come in now.”

Key Teaching Points

  • Positioning is critical and kills people. Fatality can occur within minutes if an anaphylaxis patient stands or walks. This includes not walking them to a bed, not sitting them in a chair, and not standing them up to transfer. The ASCIA guidelines are explicit on this.
  • Triage is a 30-second decision here. Do not delay to complete paperwork. Triage category, bed allocation, and first intervention happen almost simultaneously.
  • The RN can and should initiate IM adrenaline under SA Health nursing clinical guidelines for anaphylaxis. Do not wait for the GP.

Real-Job Translation

At 2:30 on a Saturday afternoon, your ED is empty but you might be mid-way through restocking or on a break. The arrival is unannounced — no ambulance pre-alert, no warning. The wife is distressed and Keith is walking in under his own steam. The instinct is to walk him to a bed. Stop him at triage. Get him flat. Get the adrenaline. Everything else is secondary. You won't have the GP for 20 minutes. You are the clinical decision-maker right now.

Break Point 2 Discussion / Practical Skill

Doorway Assessment & ABCDE

Facilitator: PAUSE. Ask all participants:

  • “What do you see from the doorway? What are you worried about?”
  • “Walk me through your ABCDE — what are you checking first?”
  • “Does this patient meet ASCIA criteria for anaphylaxis? Which criteria?”

Doorway Assessment

  • General: Flushed, anxious, visibly swollen face and lips, widespread urticaria on arms and trunk
  • Audible: Wheeze heard without stethoscope, speaking in short sentences, voice slightly hoarse
  • Posture: Wants to sit up (breathing difficulty) — allow to sit with legs outstretched

ABCDE Assessment

SystemFinding
A — AirwayTongue mildly swollen. Lips oedematous. Voice slightly hoarse. No stridor at this point.
B — BreathingRR 28. Bilateral wheeze. SpO2 93% on room air. Using accessory muscles. Persistent cough.
C — CirculationHR 110 (note: on metoprolol — this HR is significant). BP 95/60. Peripherally warm but mildly diaphoretic. Cap refill 3 sec.
D — DisabilityGCS 15. Alert but anxious. “I feel really weird.” BGL 7.2.
E — ExposureGeneralised urticaria. Bee sting site visible on right forearm (erythematous, swollen). No stinger visible. Temp 36.8°C.

Does This Meet ASCIA Criteria?

Yes — Criteria 1. Acute onset illness with skin features (urticaria, angioedema) PLUS respiratory compromise (wheeze, cough, dyspnoea, SpO2 93%) PLUS cardiovascular compromise (hypotension, tachycardia). This is unequivocal anaphylaxis.

Individual signs of anaphylaxis present: difficult/noisy breathing, wheeze, persistent cough, hoarse voice, persistent dizziness. Any ONE of these signs indicates anaphylaxis.

Key Teaching Points

  • Anaphylaxis can occur without skin features. If a patient has acute onset hypotension, bronchospasm, or upper airway obstruction after a known allergen exposure, treat as anaphylaxis even without urticaria.
  • The HR of 110 on a beta-blocker is significant. Without metoprolol, this patient's heart rate would likely be 130–150+. The beta-blocker is masking the true degree of cardiovascular compromise.
  • Do not delay adrenaline to complete the full assessment. If the doorway assessment shows anaphylaxis, give adrenaline and then complete the detailed assessment.

Real-Job Translation

You know this man. He's Keith from out on the Brennan property. You went to school with his wife, Jenny. That recognition can either help (you know his history, you know he's “allergic to bees”) or hinder (emotional response, disbelief: “not Keith”). Use the knowledge. Manage the emotion. Do the job.

Primary Survey & Initial Assessment (HIRAID)

H — History

Presenting Complaint

“Got stung by a bee about 25–30 minutes ago. Started getting itchy, then my face swelled up, now I can't breathe properly and my throat feels tight.”

History of Presenting Complaint

  • Bee sting to right forearm, single sting, approximately 25–30 minutes prior to arrival
  • Symptom progression: localised sting reaction → generalised itch → urticaria → facial swelling → throat tightness → wheeze → feeling “funny” (light-headed)
  • Progressive worsening over the drive to hospital
  • No adrenaline given prior to arrival. No EpiPen at home.

Past Medical History

  • Bee venom allergy: Previous reaction ~5 years ago — widespread urticaria only, no respiratory/cardiovascular compromise. Seen by GP. Advised to obtain EpiPen but did not follow through. Never referred to an allergist.
  • Mild asthma: Uses salbutamol MDI PRN. Rarely needed. No preventer. No hospital admissions for asthma.
  • Hypertension: On metoprolol 50mg daily (beta-blocker).

Medications

  • Metoprolol 50mg mane
  • Salbutamol 100mcg MDI PRN

Allergies

  • Bee venom (documented — previous urticarial reaction)
  • NKDA

Social History (Rural Context)

  • Farmer. Lives 15km from hospital on a grazing property.
  • Wife (Jenny) present — drove him in. She is in the waiting area, distressed.
  • Two adult children — one in Adelaide, one in Port Lincoln.
  • Known to nursing staff. Community member.
  • Weight: approximately 85kg.

I — Identify Red Flags

Break Point 3 Discussion

History & Red Flag Screening

Facilitator: PAUSE. Ask the RN:

  • “What are the red flags in this history that increase the risk of a severe or fatal outcome?”
  • “What questions must you not miss asking?”
  • “What is the significance of his metoprolol?”

Red Flags Checklist

  • Known bee venom allergy with previous systemic reaction — escalating severity pattern
  • Concurrent asthma — risk factor for fatal anaphylaxis (even mild asthma)
  • Beta-blocker therapy (metoprolol) — may cause refractory anaphylaxis; adrenaline may be less effective; masks tachycardia as a clinical marker
  • No adrenaline autoinjector — delayed first dose of adrenaline
  • Rapid progression of symptoms — 25 minutes from sting to multi-system involvement
  • Hypotension on presentation — cardiovascular compromise already present
  • Hoarse voice + tongue swelling — airway compromise developing
  • Remote from definitive care — retrieval 1.5+ hours away

Questions You Must Not Miss

  • “Have you had any adrenaline already?” (No — no EpiPen available)
  • “What medications are you on?” (Metoprolol — critical information)
  • “Do you have asthma?” (Yes — risk factor for severe bronchospasm)
  • “How quickly did the symptoms come on?” (Rapid — suggests high-risk reaction)
  • “Any chest pain or palpitations?” (Differentials; also adrenaline safety)

Key Teaching Points

  • Beta-blockers and anaphylaxis: Patients on beta-blockers may have a more severe and prolonged anaphylaxis because adrenaline's beta-adrenergic effects (bronchodilation, inotropy, chronotropy) are blocked. These patients may be refractory to standard adrenaline dosing. Glucagon is the specific rescue agent — it works via a non-adrenergic pathway.
  • Asthma + anaphylaxis = high risk of fatal outcome. Most fatalities from food or venom anaphylaxis occur in patients with coexisting asthma.
  • The delay from sting to first adrenaline dose (now ~30+ minutes) increases the risk of a severe and protracted reaction. Every minute of delay matters.

Real-Job Translation

At 3am you might not get the medication history so easily. The patient might be alone, confused, or unable to speak. Medication lists might be at home 40km away. In this scenario, the wife is a good historian — use her. In real life, your local pharmacy might know Keith's medications. Community knowledge is a rural asset. Use it.

I — Interventions (Nursing-Initiated)

  • Position flat with legs elevated (or sitting with legs outstretched if breathing difficulty is predominant)
  • IM Adrenaline 0.5mg (0.5mL of 1:1000) into outer mid-thigh — RN-initiated, EN assists with preparation and checking
  • Oxygen via Hudson mask at 8L/min (escalate to non-rebreather 15L/min if SpO2 not improving)
  • IV access — 16G or 18G cannula, left antecubital fossa. Flush and secure. Spike 1L NaCl 0.9%.
  • Continuous monitoring — cardiac monitor, SpO2, NIBP cycling q3 min initially
  • Remove allergen — inspect sting site, flick out any remaining stinger (do not squeeze)
  • Call GP — ISBAR by phone (EN can make the call on hands-free while RN gives adrenaline)

D — Diagnostics

What can be done in this setting?

  • 12-lead ECG — yes (baseline, monitor for arrhythmia with adrenaline)
  • BGL — yes (point-of-care)
  • Urinalysis — yes (not indicated acutely)
  • Bloods — no (pathology closed on Saturday). Mast cell tryptase cannot be done here.

Note: Serum mast cell tryptase is useful to confirm anaphylaxis retrospectively but requires pathology laboratory processing. Note the time of the reaction for future tryptase timing if bloods can be collected at the receiving facility (ideally 1–2 hours post-onset, and a baseline at 24+ hours).

Clinical Decision Point

Break Point 4 Decision-Making

Working Diagnosis & Escalation Decision

Facilitator: PAUSE. This break point is for all participants — RN, EN, and GP (by phone or in person if arrived).

  • “What is your working diagnosis?”
  • “What are your differentials?”
  • “Can this patient be managed here, or do you need retrieval?”
  • “Who are you calling, in what order, and what information do you need before you call?”

Working Diagnosis

Anaphylaxis — bee venom (Hymenoptera) — with cardiovascular and respiratory compromise in a beta-blocked patient.

Differential Diagnoses to Consider

  • Severe allergic reaction (non-anaphylaxis) — excluded by presence of multi-system involvement
  • Acute asthma exacerbation — possible concomitant, but the urticaria, angioedema, and hypotension point to anaphylaxis
  • Vasovagal episode — does not explain wheeze, urticaria, angioedema
  • Panic attack — does not explain hypotension, wheeze, urticaria
  • Acute coronary syndrome — consider if chest pain develops, especially post-adrenaline (Kounis syndrome)

Escalation Pathway

  1. GP on-call — already contacted. Confirm ETA. Provide ISBAR update.
  2. MedSTAR (1800 MedSTAR / 1800 633 782) — call early. Do not wait for the patient to deteriorate further. MedSTAR provides real-time clinical advice AND coordinates retrieval.
  3. SA Ambulance (000) — request ambulance for potential road transfer. Volunteer crew may arrive sooner than Port Lincoln paramedics.

This patient cannot be managed locally to discharge. He has multi-system anaphylaxis with risk factors for refractory and biphasic reactions (beta-blocker, asthma, severity). He requires at minimum 12–24 hours of monitored observation in a facility with ICU capability. Retrieval is needed.

Key Teaching Points

  • Call MedSTAR early. You do not need to be in crisis to call. MedSTAR provides clinical advice from the first call. They can guide you through adrenaline infusion setup, help you decide on glucagon, and get retrieval wheels turning while you stabilise.
  • Know your numbers: MedSTAR — 1800 633 782. SA Ambulance — 000. Have these on the wall in resus.
  • The GP may ask: “Have you given adrenaline?” The answer should be “yes” before you even call. Do not wait for permission. Nurse-initiated adrenaline for anaphylaxis is within scope and supported by SA Health clinical guidelines.

Real-Job Translation

The GP might be 20 minutes away, or they might be stuck at a farm accident 40 minutes in the other direction. You cannot count on them arriving quickly. Call them, give the ISBAR, get what orders you can, and continue managing. If the GP is significantly delayed, escalate to MedSTAR directly for phone clinical support. You are authorised and expected to do this.

Treatment & Stabilisation Phase

Break Point 5 Practical Skill / Medication Safety

Treatment Initiation & Skills Practice

Facilitator: PAUSE for hands-on skills practice. Walk through each intervention in real time.

Skill 1: IM Adrenaline Administration

Adrenaline — First-Line Treatment

Drug: Adrenaline (epinephrine) 1:1000 (1mg/mL ampoule)

Dose: 0.5mg (0.5mL) for adults >50kg — maximum single dose

Route: Intramuscular (IM) injection — outer mid-thigh

Needle: 22–25G, 25mm length

Syringe: 1mL syringe (allows accurate measurement)

Repeat: Every 5 minutes if no response or deterioration

CAUTION: IV bolus adrenaline is NOT recommended unless the patient is in cardiac arrest. IM only for anaphylaxis.

Facilitator Prompts — Medication Safety (NSQHS Standard 4):

  • “Talk me through your 5 rights: right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, right time.”
  • “What concentration are you using? Show me the ampoule.” (1:1000 = 1mg/mL. Do NOT use 1:10,000 — that is for cardiac arrest IV bolus only.)
  • “How are you drawing it up? Show me.” (Draw 0.5mL into a 1mL syringe.)
  • “Where are you injecting? Show me the site.” (Outer mid-thigh, through clothing if necessary.)
  • “EN — what is your role in this medication administration?” (Preparing the ampoule, checking the drug/dose/expiry with the RN, documenting the time of administration, monitoring the patient's response.)

Skill 2: IV Access & Fluid Preparation

  • Insert 16G or 18G IV cannula — antecubital fossa preferred (large, accessible)
  • Flush with 10mL NaCl to confirm patency
  • Spike 1L bag NaCl 0.9%. Connect giving set. Ready to run if hypotension worsens.
  • If hypotensive (BP <90 systolic): 20mL/kg NaCl rapidly under pressure. For 85kg = 1700mL stat. Use pressure bag if available.
  • Consider second IV line (opposite arm) if significant fluid resuscitation anticipated

Skill 3: Oxygen Therapy

  • Start with Hudson mask at 8L/min
  • If SpO2 not improving or <94%: escalate to non-rebreather mask at 15L/min
  • Supplemental O2 is indicated for all patients with respiratory distress, reduced consciousness, or requiring repeated adrenaline

GP Arrives — Clinical Handover at Bedside

The RN provides a rapid bedside ISBAR to the GP. The GP assumes clinical lead.

GP Decision: Adrenaline Infusion

After 2 doses of IM adrenaline with inadequate response, the GP (in liaison with MedSTAR if available by phone) decides to commence an IV adrenaline infusion.

IV Adrenaline Infusion — Pre-Hospital Protocol

Preparation: Mix 1mL of 1:1000 adrenaline (1mg) in 1000mL NaCl 0.9%

Concentration: 1 microgram/mL

Starting rate: ~5mL/kg/hr (~0.1 microgram/kg/min)

For 85kg patient: 5 × 85 = 425mL/hr initially

If no infusion pump: Standard giving set = ~20 drops/mL. Start at ~2 drops/second for an adult.

Titrate: Up or down according to response and side effects.

Monitor: Continuous ECG, SpO2, frequent NIBP (minimum q3 min).

Use dedicated IV line. Use infusion pump and anti-reflux valves wherever possible.

CAUTION: IV boluses of adrenaline are NOT recommended due to risk of cardiac arrhythmia — UNLESS the patient is in cardiac arrest.

Glucagon — Consider for Beta-Blocker Refractory Anaphylaxis

Glucagon (if adrenaline infusion ineffective)

Indication: Refractory anaphylaxis in patients on beta-blockers where adrenaline is failing to achieve adequate haemodynamic response.

Dose (adult): 1–2mg IV bolus

May repeat or follow with infusion at 1mg/hr

Mechanism: Acts via a non-adrenergic pathway (increases cAMP independent of beta receptors), supporting cardiac output and bronchodilation.

Note: Check imprest for glucagon availability. If not stocked, flag this as a system improvement in debrief.

Additional Measures

  • Nebulised salbutamol 5mg for persistent wheeze (via nebuliser mask with O2 at 8L/min)
  • IV hydrocortisone 200mg — may be given but is NOT first-line and should NOT delay adrenaline. Evidence for preventing biphasic reactions is unproven but commonly administered.
  • Do NOT give injectable promethazine — it can worsen hypotension and cause muscle necrosis. Antihistamines have no role in treating cardiovascular or respiratory symptoms of anaphylaxis.
  • Continue IV NaCl bolus to target SBP >100. Maximum 50mL/kg in first 30 minutes if needed.

Key Teaching Points

  • Adrenaline is safe and you cannot withhold it. There are no absolute contraindications to adrenaline in anaphylaxis. The risk of not giving it far exceeds any risk of giving it. If in doubt, give adrenaline.
  • The IV infusion protocol for a rural ED / pre-hospital setting uses a dilute solution (1mg in 1000mL) because it is safer and more forgiving of rate errors than the ED/tertiary protocol (1mg in 100mL).
  • Watch for adrenaline toxicity: if the patient becomes nauseous, shaky, vomiting, or hypertensive with a normal or elevated SBP, this may be overtreatment, not worsening anaphylaxis. Measure palpable SBP to guide ongoing dosing.
  • Fluid resuscitation is critical — massive vasodilatation and fluid extravasation occur in severe anaphylaxis. Do not forget the fluids while focusing on the adrenaline.

Real-Job Translation

You have now given two doses of adrenaline and your patient is still hypotensive. The GP has just walked through the door. You need to hand over clearly and concisely so they can hit the ground running. The infusion setup takes time — the EN should be preparing it while the RN does the handover. Every person has a job. No one is standing idle. This is a 3-person resuscitation and every set of hands counts.

Escalation Triggers — Call MedSTAR If:

  • Inadequate response after 2–3 IM adrenaline doses (THIS PATIENT — already met)
  • Requirement for adrenaline infusion (THIS PATIENT — already met)
  • Refractory hypotension despite fluids and adrenaline
  • Airway compromise (stridor, inability to speak, worsening tongue/throat swelling)
  • Altered consciousness / GCS drop
  • Cardiac arrest
  • Any patient you are concerned about and want clinical advice — you're worried, they're listening

ISBAR — MedSTAR Retrieval Referral

GP → MedSTAR (1800 633 782)

I“This is Dr [name], GP at [hospital] on the lower Eyre Peninsula. I'm calling for retrieval consultation.”
S“I have a 52-year-old male with refractory anaphylaxis following a bee sting. He has required 2 doses of IM adrenaline with inadequate response and is now on an IV adrenaline infusion.”
B“Background: known bee venom allergy. On metoprolol 50mg daily — beta-blocked. Mild asthma. No EpiPen. Stung approximately 50 minutes ago. Rapid progression to multi-system anaphylaxis with hypotension, bronchospasm, and angioedema.”
A“Current observations on adrenaline infusion at 425mL/hr with 1500mL NaCl given: HR 88, BP 108/68, SpO2 96% on 8L O2, RR 20, GCS 15. Wheeze resolving. Angioedema present but not worsening. He's had 2 × 0.5mg IM adrenaline, 1500mL NaCl, nebulised salbutamol 5mg, and is on IV adrenaline infusion 1mg in 1000mL at 5mL/kg/hr. I am considering glucagon given the beta-blockade.”
R“I'm requesting retrieval to a facility with ICU-level monitoring. He's at high risk of biphasic reaction given the severity, repeated adrenaline requirement, asthma, and beta-blockade. We're 60 minutes by road from the nearest regional centre. Can you advise on retrieval options and any further management you'd recommend?”

Key Teaching Points

  • MedSTAR will provide real-time clinical advice alongside retrieval coordination. They may suggest adjustments to the infusion rate, recommend glucagon, or advise on additional interventions. Listen carefully and implement their guidance.
  • Retrieval options: RFDS fixed-wing from Adelaide (~1.5 hr), SA Ambulance road transfer (~60 min once on scene), or a combination. MedSTAR will determine the most appropriate platform based on patient acuity and logistics.
  • While waiting for retrieval, you are still managing the patient. Do not “hand over mentally” once the retrieval call is made. The patient can still deteriorate.
  • Biphasic reactions can occur hours after initial improvement. The minimum observation period is 4 hours after the last adrenaline dose, but this patient warrants overnight observation given the severity, beta-blockade, and asthma history.

Real-Job Translation

MedSTAR are pragmatic and solution-focused. They will ask you for specific information: what drugs have been given, current infusion rate, vital signs, airway status. Have the EN keep a clear, legible medication/observation chart so you can read off the numbers. When MedSTAR say “we'll send the RFDS,” that clock starts ticking — 1.5 hours until a retrieval team is at your bedside. You need to keep this patient stable until then. That is your job.

Preparation for Transfer / Retrieval

Break Point 7 Discussion / Practical Skill

Transfer Preparation & Packaging

Facilitator: PAUSE. The RFDS retrieval is confirmed. ETA 1 hour 20 minutes. The patient is currently stable on the infusion.

Facilitator Prompts:

  • “What monitoring continues while we wait?”
  • “What documentation do you need to prepare?”
  • “What if retrieval is delayed by 2 hours — weather, aircraft availability? What's your contingency?”
  • “The patient's wife is in the waiting area. Who is speaking to her? What are you telling her?”

Ongoing Monitoring

  • Continuous cardiac monitoring (ECG) — watch for arrhythmias (adrenaline-related or anaphylaxis-related)
  • Continuous SpO2
  • NIBP every 5 minutes (can extend to q10 min if stable)
  • Respiratory assessment q15 min (wheeze, RR, work of breathing, voice quality)
  • Ongoing GCS / AVPU assessment
  • Monitor adrenaline infusion rate and IV site patency
  • Monitor fluid balance — intake (NaCl volume) and output (urine if catheterised)
  • Watch for signs of adrenaline toxicity: tremor, nausea, hypertension, chest pain, arrhythmias
  • Watch for signs of biphasic reaction: recurrence of urticaria, wheeze, hypotension, angioedema after initial improvement

Transfer Documentation Checklist

  • Complete set of timed observations from arrival to present
  • All medications given with exact times, doses, routes
  • Adrenaline infusion: concentration, rate, start time, rate changes
  • IV fluid volumes: type, volume, rate
  • Clinical findings: presenting complaint, history, examination findings, working diagnosis
  • GP clinical notes
  • Copy of ECG tracing
  • Patient demographics, NOK details, Medicare number
  • Allergy status (documented and labelled on patient and notes)
  • Medication list (regular medications)
  • MedSTAR advice received and implemented

Contingency Planning — Retrieval Delayed

  • If RFDS delayed 2+ hours: Can you road-transfer? Volunteer ambulance crew may be available but may not have the skill set for an adrenaline infusion. Port Lincoln paramedics (45 min response) would be more appropriate for a monitored road transfer. Discuss with MedSTAR.
  • If patient deteriorates while waiting: Increase infusion rate. Give further IM adrenaline. Consider glucagon. Fluid resuscitation. Prepare for advanced airway if airway compromise. Call MedSTAR back immediately.
  • If cardiac arrest: Standard ALS. IV adrenaline 1mg q2–3 min (cardiac arrest dosing). Aggressive fluid resuscitation 20mL/kg stat. Prolonged CPR is appropriate — this is a reversible cause. Call MedSTAR.
  • Consumables: Check remaining adrenaline ampoules, NaCl bags, oxygen supply. Is there enough for a prolonged wait?

Family Communication

  • Assign one person (ideally the EN, when not performing clinical tasks) to update Jenny.
  • Be honest, clear, and compassionate: “Keith has had a severe allergic reaction to the bee sting. We've given him medication and he's responding, but he needs to go to a bigger hospital for closer monitoring. We've arranged a retrieval team from Adelaide. They'll be here in about an hour and a half.”
  • Ask if she wants to come and sit with him (if safe and appropriate).
  • Ask about contacting family: their adult children. Does she need someone to drive her to Adelaide?
  • Acknowledge: you know this family. This is not a stranger. Maintain professional boundaries but do not pretend you don't know them. Empathy is not weakness.

Real-Job Translation

The wait for retrieval is the hardest part of rural emergency nursing. You have done everything right. The patient is responding. But you have to maintain vigilance for 90+ minutes with a 3-person team. Fatigue, adrenaline (yours, not the drug), and the emotional weight of knowing this patient will all be factors. Take turns for a 5-minute break if possible. Drink water. This is a marathon, not a sprint.

I“I'm Dr [name], GP here at [hospital]. This is [RN name] our RN and [EN name] our EN. The patient is Keith Brennan, 52 years old, DOB [date], UR number [X].”
S“Keith presented approximately 2 hours ago with anaphylaxis following a bee sting. He required 2 doses of IM adrenaline without adequate response and has been on an IV adrenaline infusion for the past 90 minutes. He is currently haemodynamically stable on the infusion.”
B“Background: known bee venom allergy — previous urticarial reaction 5 years ago. Mild asthma, salbutamol PRN. Hypertension on metoprolol 50mg daily — he's beta-blocked, which we believe contributed to the refractory response. No other significant history. No known drug allergies. Weight approximately 85kg.”
A“Current observations: HR 82, BP 112/70, RR 18, SpO2 97% on 6L O2 via Hudson, GCS 15, temp 36.9. Wheeze has resolved. Angioedema has improved significantly. Urticaria mostly resolved. He is comfortable, alert, and oriented.

Treatment given: IM adrenaline 0.5mg at [time], repeated at [time +5]. IV adrenaline infusion commenced at [time +15] — 1mg in 1000mL NaCl at 5mL/kg/hr, currently weaned to 200mL/hr. Total IV NaCl: 2500mL. Nebulised salbutamol 5mg ×2. IV hydrocortisone 200mg. ECG: sinus rhythm, no acute changes. No bloods — pathology closed today. Glucagon was considered but not required as he responded to the infusion.”
R“He needs ongoing monitored observation for at least 24 hours given the severity, beta-blockade, and asthma. High risk for biphasic reaction. The infusion is still running and will need to be weaned further during transport. He'll need bloods at the receiving facility — including mast cell tryptase if within the window. He'll also need an allergist referral, an EpiPen prescription, and an ASCIA Action Plan before eventual discharge. His wife Jenny is here — she'll need to make arrangements to get to Adelaide. All documentation is ready for you.”

Key Teaching Points

  • Good handover saves lives. Poor handover loses information. Practise ISBAR until it is second nature.
  • Include exact times of all medication administration. The retrieval team needs to know when the last adrenaline was given to plan ongoing management.
  • Hand over the paper documentation (observation chart, medication chart, nursing notes, GP notes, ECG) as well as the verbal handover.
  • The RN should actively contribute to handover — they were the first clinician to see the patient and have the most continuous observations.
  • Don't forget the discharge planning elements that the receiving facility will need to action: allergist referral, EpiPen prescription, ASCIA Action Plan.

Real-Job Translation

When the retrieval team arrives, you might feel an overwhelming sense of relief. That's normal. But do not rush the handover. Take 5 minutes to do it properly. The retrieval team will want to examine the patient themselves and will take over the infusion. Your job is to give them every piece of information they need to continue seamless care. Once Keith is in the aircraft, your work on this patient is done — but the emotional processing may just be starting. That's what the debrief is for.

Debrief (10–15 Minutes)

Structured Debrief: Advocacy-Inquiry Model

Sit down together. Remove sim equipment from hands. Take a breath. This is protected time.

Phase 1: Reactions (2 minutes)

  • “How are you feeling right now?”
  • “What was going through your mind during the scenario?”
  • Allow all participants to express initial reactions without analysis.

Phase 2: What Went Well (Plus) (3–4 minutes)

  • “What did the team do well?”
  • Specifically explore: early adrenaline administration, team communication, role clarity, escalation decisions, ISBAR quality.
  • Facilitator should reinforce good practice with specific observations.

Phase 3: What Would We Do Differently (Delta) (3–4 minutes)

  • “If this happened again tomorrow, what would you change?”
  • Use Advocacy-Inquiry: “I noticed [observation]. I'm curious about what you were thinking at that point?”
  • Explore: Was there any hesitation giving adrenaline? Was the GP contacted early enough? Was MedSTAR called at the right time? Did the EN feel clear about their role?

Phase 4: Systems Issues (2 minutes)

  • Was the resus trolley stocked and ready?
  • Is glucagon on the imprest at this site? If not, who do we escalate this to?
  • Is the ASCIA poster current and visible in resus?
  • Do we have a nurse-initiated adrenaline protocol accessible to all staff?
  • Are the phone numbers for MedSTAR and SA Ambulance posted in resus?
  • Do staff know the EN scope regarding medication administration in emergencies?

Phase 5: Emotional Check-In (2 minutes)

  • “In a real scenario, Keith is someone you know. His wife is someone you went to school with. How does that change things for you?”
  • “What support would you need after a case like this on a real shift?”
  • Remind participants of EAP, peer support, and the importance of debriefing after real critical events.
  • Normalise the emotional impact of rural emergency care. It is harder when you know the patient. That doesn't make you less professional — it makes you human.

Key Take-Home Points

  1. Adrenaline is the drug. IM is the route. Do not delay. Do not wait for the doctor.
  2. Position flat. Do not allow the patient to stand or walk. This single action prevents deaths.
  3. Beta-blockers change the game. Know about glucagon. Check your imprest.
  4. Call MedSTAR early. They give advice, not just retrieval.
  5. ISBAR is your framework. Use it every time — phone, bedside, handover.
  6. The EN is essential. Clear role allocation and communication make a 2-nurse + GP team function like a resuscitation team.
  7. Document as you go. Times matter. Doses matter. The retrieval team needs this.
  8. You are enough. With the right knowledge, the right protocol, and the right communication, a small rural team can stabilise this patient and get them to definitive care.

Scenario Variations

Use these to increase complexity or run the scenario a second time with a different focus.

Variation 1: Cardiac Arrest Before Retrieval

Keith deteriorates 45 minutes into the wait for retrieval. The adrenaline infusion line occludes unnoticed. He becomes unresponsive — PEA arrest. Tests: ALS in anaphylaxis, IV adrenaline bolus dosing (1mg q2–3 min in cardiac arrest), aggressive fluid resuscitation (20mL/kg stat under pressure), recognition that prolonged CPR is appropriate for a reversible cause.

Variation 2: GP Unavailable — RN Leads Alone

The GP is at a farm accident 50 minutes away and cannot come. The RN must manage the entire case with the EN, supported by MedSTAR phone advice. Tests: RN clinical leadership, nurse-initiated protocols, ISBAR to MedSTAR without a doctor intermediary, EN scope maximisation, emotional resilience.

Variation 3: Paediatric Presentation

A 6-year-old (22kg) attending the local sports day has a peanut anaphylaxis. Parents are present and distressed. Tests: paediatric adrenaline dosing (0.22mL of 1:1000 IM or 300mcg autoinjector), weight-based fluid calculations (20mL/kg = 440mL), paediatric infusion rates, communication with distressed parents, SAAS MedSTAR Kids retrieval pathway.

Variation 4: Patient Refuses Transfer

Keith stabilises after the first adrenaline dose and says: “I'm fine now. I'm not going to Adelaide. The harvest is on.” His wife supports his decision. Tests: capacity assessment, risk communication, documentation of informed refusal, safety netting (what to watch for, when to return), prescription of an EpiPen, follow-up planning, the ethical and medicolegal complexities of refusal in a rural context.

Variation 5: Second Patient Arrives

Thirty minutes into the scenario, a second patient arrives by private car: a 70-year-old female with chest pain. The ED now has two patients and three staff. Tests: prioritisation, resource allocation, communication under pressure, EN managing the stable-but-monitored patient while RN and GP focus on the deteriorating one (or vice versa), calling for backup staff.
