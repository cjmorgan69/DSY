Multi-Trauma: Fall From Height

Paramedic Walk-Through Scenario — Severe Haemorrhage • TBI • Spinal Injury

Context

Crew: 2 Paramedics Setting: Rural property, Flinders Hwy near Koppio Backup: Nil available MedSTAR: ~90 min by air (Adelaide) PLHS: ~30 min by road Time: Saturday 1540 hrs, mid-June Weather: 12°C, overcast, light S/W wind Daylight remaining: ~90 min

Learning Objectives

After this session, participants will be able to:

  1. Prioritise and manage competing clinical priorities in multi-system trauma
  2. Apply damage control resuscitation: haemorrhage control, thermoregulation, movement minimisation, targeted fluids, rapid transport
  3. Recognise when permissive hypotension is not appropriate (concurrent TBI) and apply the correct fluid target
  4. Administer TXA within protocol criteria and time constraints
  5. Make evidence-based spinal immobilisation decisions — including collar contraindications when raised ICP is suspected
  6. Coordinate a two-person crew to deliver time-critical multi-trauma care in a rural setting
  7. Deliver a structured ISBAR notification to the receiving facility

  • This scenario combines three SAAS CPGs — Severe Haemorrhage, TBI, and Spinal Injury
  • The focus is on clinical decision-making with competing priorities
  • No backup crew — manage with your resources
  • MedSTAR retrieval: ~90 min air from Adelaide
  • PLHS: ~30 min by road
  • All treatment decisions must be verbalised with rationale
  • Skills will be practiced hands-on during the treatment phase

Severe Haemorrhage — Damage Control Resuscitation

The lethal triad: coagulopathy + hypothermia + acidosis. Each component independently and collectively potentiates bleeding.

  • Haemorrhage control is the primary intervention
  • Thermoregulation — warm ambulance, remove wet clothing, insulate
  • Movement minimisation — promote haemostasis, prevent clot disruption
  • TXA — reduces mortality, must be within 3 hrs of injury, slow push over 2–3 min
  • Targeted fluid resuscitation in 250mL aliquots (max 10mL/kg aliquots in paeds)
  • Rapid transport & early ISBAR notification
KEY: Permissive hypotension is NOT appropriate for patients with confirmed or suspected TBI. Where severe haemorrhage and TBI coexist, the fluid strategy should aim at treating the TBI.

Traumatic Brain Injury — Preventing Secondary Injury

  • Hypoxia and hypotension are the strongest predictors of poor outcome
  • Target SpO2 >94%
  • Target MAP ≥90 mmHg or SBP ≥110 mmHg (for ≥14 yrs)
  • IV saline 250mL aliquots up to 20mL/kg
  • EtCO2 via nasal sampling line — detect hypoventilation early
  • Optimise cerebral venous return: 30° head-up if condition permits, avoid constrictive neck clothing, avoid tight collars
  • Monitor GCS, pupils, BGL
  • Exclude or treat: hypovolaemia, hypoglycaemia, alcohol/drug OD, psychosis

Spinal Injury — Precautions & Extrication

  • C-spine is the most vulnerable region (>50% of spinal injuries)
  • Apply the most appropriate precaution technique: manual stabilisation, self-splinting, modified towel roll, vacuum mattress, semi-rigid collar
  • Semi-rigid collar contraindicated if: compromised airway, spinal deformities, concerns about raised ICP
  • Neurogenic shock (isolated cord injury): target MAP 80 / SBP 100 — but exclude haemorrhagic shock first
  • Time-critical patients: rapid extrication — spinal precautions must not delay

CRITICAL DECISION — Fluid Resuscitation Strategy

The Severe Haemorrhage protocol states permissive hypotension targets a patient who “responds appropriately to verbal commands” or has a palpable radial pulse.

However, the same protocol explicitly states:
“Permissive hypotension is NOT an appropriate treatment strategy for patients with confirmed or suspected TBI. Where severe haemorrhage and TBI are suspected in a patient, the fluid resuscitation strategy should be aimed at treating the TBI.”

TBI fluid target: MAP ≥90 mmHg or SBP ≥110 mmHg
This patient’s MAP is 65. He requires aggressive fluid resuscitation to a higher target than permissive hypotension would allow.
CERVICAL COLLAR DECISION

The Spinal Injury protocol lists “concerns about raised intracranial pressure” as a contraindication for semi-rigid collar application.

The TBI protocol notes that factors restricting venous drainage from the head — including tight collars — are preventable contributors to raised ICP.

This patient has unilateral pupil changes suggesting developing intracranial pathology.

Decision: Do NOT apply a semi-rigid collar.
Alternative: manual in-line stabilisation → modified towel roll → vacuum mattress.

TXA Criteria Check

  • ≥16 years: YES (58 years old)
  • <3 hours of injury: YES (~37 min ago)
  • HR >120: YES (128)
  • SBP <90 mmHg: YES (86)

Decision: Administer TXA 1g IV bolus, slow push over 2–3 minutes.

Major Trauma Triage

  • GCS <14: YES
  • SBP <90: YES
  • Fall >3 metres: YES

This is a major trauma — time-critical transport required.

Key Teaching Points

  • The TBI fluid target overrides permissive hypotension — this is explicitly stated in the Severe Haemorrhage CPG
  • When protocols overlap, read them together — they deliberately cross-reference each other
  • Cervical collar is NOT always the default — it can worsen ICP in TBI
  • The collar decision requires clinical reasoning, not just mechanism-based application
  • TXA has a 3-hour window from injury — document time of administration for handover
  • Clinical decision framework: What kills first? Address that first.

Real-Job Translation

These competing priorities are exactly what makes multi-trauma challenging on the road. On a real job, you will not have time to read protocols in the field — you need to know them. Practise recognising where they intersect and conflict.

Treatment Phase

Break Point 5

Treatment Initiation & Medication Safety

Type: Practical Skill

Work through each treatment step in order. Verbalise rationale for every intervention. Practise hands-on where marked.

Haemorrhage Control — Scalp Laceration

Priority: FIRST. Minimising blood loss is the primary intervention aimed at reducing acidosis.

  1. Don PPE. Remove Margaret’s towel carefully.
  2. Briefly inspect wound — identify bone visibility, foreign bodies, skull depression.
  3. If no depressed skull fracture: pack wound firmly with folded gauze.
  4. Apply direct pressure bandage over the packing.
  5. Ensure bandage is firm but does not circumferentially compress — risk of restricting venous drainage and raising ICP if too tight around the head.
  6. Monitor for continued bleeding through the dressing.
Clinical Note: Scalp wounds can lose significant blood volume due to rich vascular supply. Direct wound packing and pressure is highly effective. Haemostatic dressings may be used if available.
Haemorrhage Control — Femoral Traction Splint (CT-6) per CPPRO-028

Closed femur fractures can sequester 1–2 litres of blood into the thigh compartment. Traction splinting reduces compartment volume and promotes tamponade.

  1. Assess and document distal neurovascular status before application: dorsalis pedis pulse, sensation, movement in left foot.
  2. Measure the splint against the uninjured (right) leg.
  3. Clinician 2 applies the ankle hitch to the left foot/ankle.
  4. Clinician 1 may need to briefly hand off C-spine stabilisation to assist — communicate clearly.
  5. One clinician applies manual longitudinal traction while the other extends and secures the splint.
  6. Apply mechanical traction per device instructions — patient should report pain relief when adequate traction is achieved.
  7. Reassess and document distal neurovascular status after application.
Two-Person Challenge: Applying a traction splint while maintaining C-spine stabilisation requires clear verbal communication and brief task-switching. The person at the head must verbalise before releasing: “I’m releasing C-spine — taking over traction. You take head.”
Oxygen Therapy
  1. Apply non-rebreather mask at 15 L/min.
  2. Target: SpO2 >94% (per TBI protocol).
  3. Suction unit assembled and positioned at patient’s head — ready for immediate use.
  4. Apply EtCO2 nasal sampling line under the NRB mask.
IV Access — Two Large-Bore Lines
  1. Establish two large-bore IV cannulae (16G or larger).
  2. Sites: bilateral antecubital fossae or large forearm veins.
  3. Flush each with normal saline and confirm patency.
  4. Connect primed giving sets.
  5. Secure with tape and transparent dressings.
Two-Person Crew Note: One line is often established first by Clinician 2 while Clinician 1 manages airway/head. The second line can be established once the first fluid bolus is running, or en route.
TXA 1g IV — Preparation & Administration
  1. Draw up TXA 1g (typically 1g/10mL ampoule) into a 10mL syringe.
  2. Perform 5 Rights check with your partner: Right patient, Right drug, Right dose, Right route, Right time.
  3. Administer IV slow push over 2–3 minutes.
  4. Monitor for transient hypotension during administration — slow down if BP drops.
  5. Document the exact time of administration — this is critical for handover (hospital needs it for the second dose and ongoing management).
Medication Safety:
  • TXA must NOT be prioritised over haemorrhage control or transport
  • First dose must be within 3 hours of injury
  • Rapid bolus can cause hypotension — slow push over 2–3 min
  • The time of administration MUST be clearly handed over to the receiving facility
Targeted Fluid Resuscitation
  1. Normal saline 0.9% in 250 mL aliquots.
  2. Target: MAP ≥90 mmHg or SBP ≥110 mmHg (TBI target — NOT permissive hypotension).
  3. Reassess BP after each 250 mL bolus.
  4. Up to 20 mL/kg before EOC clinician consultation is required.
  5. Patient weight estimate ~78 kg → 20 mL/kg = 1,560 mL ceiling.
  6. If target not reached after 20 mL/kg: consult EOC clinician for SAAS Medical Practitioner advice.
Critical Rationale: The Severe Haemorrhage CPG explicitly states that where severe haemorrhage and TBI coexist, the fluid strategy should aim at treating the TBI. This means targeting MAP ≥90 / SBP ≥110 — a higher target requiring more aggressive fluid than permissive hypotension would allow.
Awareness: Crystalloid fluids can worsen the lethal triad (haemodilution, hypothermia, acidosis). Give the minimum amount required to reach target. This patient needs a surgeon and blood products — crystalloid is a bridge, not a solution.
Temperature Management
  1. Cut away wet and blood-soaked clothing with trauma shears (maintain spinal alignment during any rolling).
  2. Cover with dry cloth blankets.
  3. Apply foil/space blanket over the top.
  4. Prepare ambulance: turn heating to maximum before loading.
  5. Minimise skin exposure during interventions.

Hypothermia impairs coagulation, reduces platelet function, and worsens acidosis. Patient is already 35.6°C, lying on cold concrete, in winter conditions.

Spinal Precautions — Without Collar
  1. Maintain manual in-line stabilisation throughout all interventions.
  2. Do NOT apply a semi-rigid cervical collar — contraindicated due to raised ICP concerns (unilateral pupil changes).
  3. Prepare modified towel roll for packaging: roll two towels, position on either side of the head/neck, tape across forehead to the vacuum mattress.
  4. Vacuum mattress will provide whole-body immobilisation for transport.
  5. Document reasoning for collar non-application on ePCR.
Documentation: Choosing NOT to collar is a defensible, protocol-supported clinical decision when raised ICP is suspected. Document: “Semi-rigid collar withheld due to unilateral pupil changes and concern for raised ICP. Spinal precautions maintained with manual stabilisation, modified towel roll, and vacuum mattress.”
Head Positioning (TBI)
  • TBI protocol: posture 30° head-up if patient condition permits
  • Currently hypotensive (MAP 65) — do NOT elevate head yet
  • Head elevation could reduce cerebral perfusion in a hypotensive patient
  • Reassess once BP improving toward target — consider 15–30° head-up during transport
  • Ensure no constrictive clothing or ties around the neck

Facilitator Prompts for Break Point 5

  • Walk through each treatment step — verbalise what you are doing and why
  • What are the contraindications and precautions for TXA?
  • Why is the fluid target different in this patient compared to isolated haemorrhage?
  • Why are you NOT applying a collar? Where does the protocol support this?
  • How do you manage spinal precautions with only two crew?
  • What would you do if the patient started seizing right now?
  • When do you decide to move to the ambulance? What is the trigger?

Key Teaching Points

  • Haemorrhage control FIRST — then TXA — then fluids. In that order.
  • TXA slow push — rapid bolus can cause transient hypotension in an already shocked patient
  • Document TXA time precisely — the hospital needs it for second dose timing
  • Collar contraindication in raised ICP is evidence-based and protocol-supported
  • Do not delay transport for perfection — minimum interventions on scene, continue en route
  • Calculate your fluid ceiling: 20 mL/kg → then consult EOC

Real-Job Translation

With two people, you cannot do everything simultaneously. Prioritise what you can control (external bleeding, temperature, oxygen) before what requires IV access (TXA, fluids). Target scene time: <15 minutes from arrival to departure where possible. If you are spending >20 minutes on scene with a time-critical patient, reassess your priorities.

Escalation Contacts — Order of Priority

    • Blood products / massive transfusion protocol standby
    • CT readiness (head, C-spine, chest, abdo, pelvis)
  2. MedSTAR — notification for secondary retrieval

    • MedSTAR may meet at PLHS or arrange retrieval to RAH/FMC

  3. EOC Clinician — if >20 mL/kg fluid required or additional clinical advice needed

Key Teaching Points

  • Reassess after every intervention — did it work?
  • The TBI fluid target is hard to reach with crystalloid alone — this is realistic. The patient needs blood products at hospital.
  • Do not wait for perfection — move to hospital
  • MedSTAR notification is not the same as requesting retrieval — notify early, they will advise
  • PLHS notification allows them to activate resources before you arrive
  • Concurrent treatment and transport = damage control resuscitation in practice

Real-Job Translation

On a real rural job, the transport decision is often: “Go now and treat on the way.” MedSTAR can advise on management en route and may redirect you. PLHS can provide a lot — but only if you give them time to prepare. Do not sit on scene chasing numbers when the patient needs a surgeon and blood.

Break Point 7

Extrication & Transport Planning

Type: Practical Skill / Discussion

Situation

Decision made to transport. Barry is supine on the concrete pad, ~15m from the ambulance. Traction splint in situ. Manual C-spine stabilisation maintained. Only two crew members. No backup.

Facilitator Prompts

  • What extrication method are you using and why?
  • How do you get the vacuum mattress under the patient with two people?
  • What is the minimum number of log rolls and why?
  • Can Margaret assist? If so, what can she safely do?
  • Is this a rapid or controlled extrication?

Expected Paramedic Reasoning

This is a time-critical patient — rapid extrication is appropriate per the Spinal Injury protocol.

  1. Bring the stretcher and vacuum mattress to the patient (not the other way around).
  2. Pre-shape the vacuum mattress on the ground alongside Barry.
  3. Two-person log roll:
    • Clinician 1 at head — maintains alignment, calls the roll
    • Clinician 2 at hips/legs — manages the roll and slides mattress
    • Margaret can assist: hold the edge of the vacuum mattress in position
    • Roll to one side — minimum angle necessary
    • Slide vacuum mattress underneath
    • Roll back onto mattress
    • One roll only — minimise movement
  4. Position patient centrally. Mould the mattress around the patient and splinted limb.
  5. Apply modified towel roll: rolled towels on each side of head/neck, tape across forehead to mattress.
  6. Evacuate air from vacuum mattress — confirm rigidity.
  7. Secure straps.
  8. Lift vacuum mattress onto stretcher — correct manual handling technique, communicate on count.
  9. Load into ambulance.

Once in the Ambulance

  • Warm ambulance — heating already on max
  • Continue IV fluid resuscitation — 250 mL boluses targeting MAP ≥90 / SBP ≥110
  • Continue monitoring: GCS, pupils, vitals q5 min
  • Consider 15–30° head-up tilt once BP improving (tilt entire vacuum mattress/stretcher)
  • Prepare ISBAR phone notification for PLHS
  • One drives, one monitors — driver stops if the clinician needs assistance
  • Estimated travel time to PLHS: ~30 minutes

Key Teaching Points

  • Vacuum mattress provides: immobilisation, thermal insulation, and padding
  • Minimise log rolls — every roll is a potential secondary injury event and clot disruption
  • Time-critical patients get rapid extrication — do not delay for perfect packaging
  • Use bystanders for simple, well-instructed tasks
  • The ambulance is your treatment room — get there quickly and continue care en route

Real-Job Translation

Vacuum mattress application with two people and a traction splint in situ is challenging but practised regularly. The modified towel roll is the correct C-spine technique when the collar is contraindicated. Rehearse the log roll sequence before you do it: “I’m going to count to three. On three, we roll toward you. Ready?”

Sample ISBAR

I — Identify:
“Port Lincoln Paramedic crew. I’m [Name], paramedic, handing over to [receiving clinician].”
S — Situation:
“This is Barry, 58-year-old male. Major trauma — fall from approximately 4 metres off a shed roof onto concrete. Time of injury approximately 1535 today. He has a significant head injury, a closed left femur fracture, and suspected thoracic spinal injury.”
B — Background:
“Found by his wife on the concrete. GCS was 10 on our arrival at 1612. No significant past medical history known — wife reports he is usually well, takes no regular medications. No allergies known.”
A — Assessment:
“Current GCS 11 — E3 V3 M5 — eyes opening to voice, confused speech, localising pain. Left pupil 4 mm and sluggish, right 3 mm and reactive — unchanged since first assessment. HR 108, BP 104 on 68, MAP 80, RR 20, SpO2 98% on non-rebreather, EtCO2 36, temperature 36.0. BGL 5.8 on scene. ECG sinus tachycardia throughout.”
R — Recommendation:
“He has had TXA 1 gram IV at 1625 — that is 35 minutes ago — within protocol criteria. Total IV normal saline is 1,000 mL in 250 mL boluses. MAP was 65 on arrival, now 80 — target is 90 or above for suspected TBI. Scalp laceration packed and pressure-bandaged, femoral traction splint applied to the left leg, in vacuum mattress with modified towel roll — no cervical collar applied due to ICP concern. MedSTAR have been notified for potential secondary retrieval — GCS and pupil signs suggest he will need a neurosurgical centre. He will likely need ongoing fluid resuscitation, CT imaging head and C-spine minimum, blood products, and consideration for a second dose of TXA. Are you happy to take over?”

Key Teaching Points

  • ISBAR is structured — do not ramble. Practise until it is second nature.
  • TXA time must be explicitly handed over — the hospital needs it for second dose timing
  • Fluid volumes AND targets must be communicated clearly
  • Explain why the collar was not applied — hand over the clinical reasoning
  • MedSTAR notification status ensures continuity of the retrieval pathway
  • Include what you think the patient needs — it guides their preparation

Real-Job Translation

The phone pre-notification en route is often more important than the bedside handover — it activates trauma teams, blood products, and CT. At bedside, deliver your ISBAR clearly, hand over the ePCR, confirm they have everything, and then step back. Stay available for questions.

Debrief

Reflective Questions (15 minutes)

  1. What was the most challenging clinical decision in this scenario and why?
  2. How did you prioritise the competing protocol requirements — haemorrhage control vs TBI fluid targets vs spinal precautions?
  3. Where specifically in the Severe Haemorrhage protocol does it address the TBI fluid conflict? Can you recall the wording?
  4. Why did you choose not to apply a cervical collar? What clinical evidence and protocol reference supports that decision?
  5. If the patient’s GCS had dropped to 7 during transport, what would you have done differently?
  6. How effective was your crew communication and task allocation with only two people?
  7. What would you do differently on a real job based on what you practised today?

Take-Home Messages

  1. When TBI and severe haemorrhage coexist, the TBI fluid target overrides permissive hypotension — target MAP ≥90 / SBP ≥110. This is explicitly stated in the Severe Haemorrhage CPG.
  2. Cervical collars are contraindicated when raised ICP is suspected — manual stabilisation, modified towel rolls, and vacuum mattress are the alternatives. Document your reasoning.
  3. TXA must be given within 3 hours of injury — and the exact time of administration must be clearly handed over to the receiving facility.
  4. Haemorrhage control is the first priority — before TXA, before fluids. Control the bleeding, then augment with medications and fluids.
  5. Damage control resuscitation = haemorrhage control + thermoregulation + movement minimisation + targeted fluids + rapid transport to definitive care.
  6. Two-person crew management of multi-trauma requires constant verbal communication, flexible role-sharing, and ruthless prioritisation. You cannot do everything — do what matters most, first.
  7. In rural settings, the transport decision is often “go now, treat en route.” Do not sit on scene chasing perfect numbers when the patient needs a surgeon and blood products.

Optional Scenario Variations

Variation 1 — Deterioration En Route

GCS drops to 7 (E1 V2 M4) fifteen minutes into transport. Left pupil fixed and dilated (6 mm).

  • What changes in your management? (Airway intervention, assisted ventilation, MedSTAR escalation)
  • Do you divert? Continue to PLHS? Pull over and manage?
  • How does a GCS ≤8 change your airway approach?
  • What are the ventilation targets if you are bagging? (EtCO2 guidance)

Variation 2 — Pelvic Instability Found

During the log roll for vacuum mattress, significant pelvic instability is identified.

  • How does this change your haemorrhage management? (Pelvic binder application)
  • What is the additional expected blood loss from a pelvic fracture?
  • Does this change your fluid strategy or escalation timeline?
  • How does a pelvic binder interact with the traction splint and vacuum mattress?

Variation 3 — Severe Agitation Post-TBI

After initial fluid, Barry becomes severely agitated — pulling at lines, thrashing, resisting care. GCS motor component drops to M4 (flexion withdrawal).

  • What is causing the agitation? (TBI, pain, hypoxia, hypovolaemia)
  • How do you manage this safely with two crew?
  • The TBI protocol notes early clinical support for potential sedation — who do you call?
  • How does agitation impact your spinal precautions and monitoring?

Variation 4 — Isolated TBI (No Significant Haemorrhage)

Same mechanism, but only a head injury identified. No femur fracture, no significant external bleeding. BP is 148/92.

  • How does your fluid strategy change? (Patient is hypertensive, not hypotensive)
  • Does TXA apply? (Check: HR >120 and/or SBP <90 criteria not met)
  • What is your focus now? (Oxygenation, ventilation, ICP management, transport)
  • Is a collar appropriate in this variation? (ICP concern still exists if pupil signs present)
