Fluid Resuscitation in Medical Emergencies

P-Level | Single Responder Scenario

Scenario Context

Hypotension Secondary to Dehydration — Rural Home Setting

Time: 10:30 hours (mid-morning)

Location: Isolated rural property, 35 minutes from nearest hospital

Crew Configuration: Paramedic single responder (fast response vehicle) with full equipment. Volunteer AO crew backup en route (ETA 12 minutes)

Duration: 90–120 minutes (walk-through with break points)

Reference: CPG — Fluid Resuscitation in Medical Emergencies — P

Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

  • Apply the general approach to fluid resuscitation per SAAS protocol
  • Differentiate dehydration-related hypotension from other shock states
  • Establish IV access and administer appropriate fluid therapy
  • Recognise acute vs subacute dehydration presentations
  • Integrate single responder workflow with incoming backup crew
  • Apply appropriate postural management for hypotensive patients
  • Determine transport priority and ongoing monitoring requirements

Clinical Signs of Dehydration:

  • Dry mucous membranes
  • Reduced skin turgor (tenting)
  • Sunken eyes
  • Concentrated urine (wife reports minimal output)
  • Tachycardia with hypotension
  • Delayed capillary refill (>3 seconds)

Key Teaching Points:

Dehydration Assessment Triad

1. History: Reduced intake, increased losses (vomiting, diarrhoea, sweating), duration

2. Clinical Signs: Skin turgor, mucous membranes, urine output, mental state

3. Vital Signs: Tachycardia, hypotension, narrow pulse pressure

Real-Job Translation:

In elderly patients, dehydration can develop insidiously over days. The compensatory tachycardia may be blunted by beta-blockers. Always check medication lists — this patient may not mount the expected heart rate response.

Clinical Decision Point

BREAK POINT 4 Decision-Making

Screening & Working Diagnosis

Facilitator Prompts:

  • What's your working diagnosis?
  • How do you differentiate this from a cardiac cause of hypotension?
  • Does this patient fit the "symptomatic dehydration with hypotension" criteria?
  • What other shock states should you consider?

Expected Paramedic Reasoning:

Working Diagnosis: Hypotension secondary to dehydration (subacute, 3-day history)

Likely Cause: Gastroenteritis leading to volume depletion

Ruling OUT other causes:

  • Primary Cardiac: No chest pain, no acute ECG changes (just sinus tachycardia from known AF patient), no acute pulmonary oedema — UNLIKELY
  • Sepsis: Temp 37.8°C borderline, no clear source other than likely viral gastro, no rigors — LESS LIKELY but monitor
  • Haemorrhage: No melaena, no haematemesis, no trauma — RULED OUT
  • Obstructive: No respiratory distress, no JVP elevation, equal breath sounds — RULED OUT

Protocol Application

From CPG — Fluid Resuscitation in Medical Emergencies:

"Symptomatic dehydration with hypotension → Adopt general approach to fluid resuscitation"

"Consider if acute or subacute dehydration"

This is SUBACUTE — 3-day history with progressive deterioration.

Key Teaching Points:

  • Always exclude cardiac cause before attributing hypotension to dehydration
  • The protocol explicitly states it "does not apply to hypotension from primary cardiac cause"
  • Subacute dehydration (days) vs acute (hours) affects both clinical picture and response to treatment
  • Beta-blockers mask compensatory tachycardia — don't rely on heart rate alone to assess volume status

Real-Job Translation:

Elderly patients on multiple cardiac medications are complex. Don't let the "simple" presentation of dehydration lull you into complacency. Get a 12-lead ECG before committing to your diagnosis if there's any doubt.

BREAK POINT 5 Practical Skill

IV Access & Fluid Administration

Facilitator Prompts:

  • Talk me through your IV cannulation approach in a dehydrated patient
  • What gauge cannula and why?
  • What fluid, what volume, what rate?
  • What are you monitoring during the infusion?

Skill Practice: IV Cannulation (Dehydrated Patient)

  1. Assess venous access: Dehydrated patients have collapsed veins. Use a tourniquet, hang the arm down, palpate for deeper veins. Consider antecubital fossa for reliable access.
  2. Select cannula: 18G preferred for rapid fluid administration. 20G acceptable if veins are poor.
  3. Prepare equipment: Cannula, tegaderm, saline lock, giving set already primed with NaCl 0.9%
  4. Aseptic technique: Alcohol swab, allow to dry, anchor vein, insert cannula at 15-30° angle
  5. Confirm placement: Blood flashback, advance cannula, remove needle, connect giving set
  6. Secure: Tegaderm over site, loop and secure tubing
  7. Document: Time, gauge, site, number of attempts

Fluid Resuscitation — General Approach (Per Protocol)

  • Fluid: Sodium Chloride 0.9% (Normal Saline)
  • Initial Bolus: 250–500mL
  • Rate: Rapid infusion (consider pressure bag if severely hypotensive)
  • Reassess: After each bolus — BP, HR, mental state
  • Repeat: If still hypotensive, repeat bolus
  • Caution: Cardiac history — monitor for signs of fluid overload
Fluid Overload Warning Signs:
  • New or worsening dyspnoea
  • Increased respiratory rate
  • New crackles on auscultation
  • Falling SpO2
  • JVP elevation

This patient has cardiac history — fluid resuscitate judiciously and reassess frequently.

Additional Interventions:

  • Position patient supine with legs elevated (if tolerated)
  • Remove excessive bedding if warm
  • Small sips of water if conscious and able to swallow (oral rehydration adjunct)
  • Keep patient warm if peripherally shut down

Real-Job Translation:

As a single responder, getting IV access established before backup arrives is gold. When the crew arrives, you can hand over with IV running and move straight to transport planning. Efficiency matters in rural settings with long transport times.

BREAK POINT 6 Decision-Making

Reassessment & Escalation Decision

Facilitator Prompts:

  • How do you interpret this response to fluid?
  • Do you give more fluid? How much?
  • Does this patient need EOC consultation?
  • What's your transport priority?

Expected Paramedic Reasoning:

  • Positive response to initial bolus — supports dehydration as primary cause
  • Still hypotensive — warrants second bolus
  • Cardiac history requires cautious approach — smaller boluses with frequent reassessment
  • Transport is indicated — this patient needs hospital-based rehydration and investigation

Decision: Second Bolus

Plan: Administer second 250mL NaCl 0.9% bolus during transport preparation

Rationale: Continuing response, no signs of fluid overload, patient remains symptomatic

EOC: Consider notification if BP fails to respond to second bolus or if concerns arise

Key Teaching Points:

  • "Assess, treat, reassess" — the cornerstone of fluid resuscitation
  • Improvement confirms your working diagnosis
  • Deterioration should prompt reconsideration of differentials
  • Document response to treatment — this guides hospital management

Real-Job Translation:

Subacute dehydration won't be fully corrected in the field. Your role is to stabilise, prevent deterioration, and transport. The hospital will complete the rehydration with lab-guided therapy.

  • Transfer to stretcher — position supine with legs slightly elevated

  • Paramedic: In rear with patient, managing ongoing care
  • AO Clinician: Can assist in rear or drive (Paramedic's discretion)

Hospital Handover

BREAK POINT 8 Practical Skill

ISBAR Handover Practice

Facilitator: "You've arrived at the ED. The triage nurse is ready to receive handover. Deliver your ISBAR."

Sample ISBAR Handover:

I – Identify: "This is Harold Patterson, 76 years old, from a rural property on Boundary Road."

S – Situation: "Harold presented with hypotension secondary to dehydration. He's been unwell with gastro symptoms for 3 days with minimal oral intake and vomiting."

B – Background: "Past history of atrial fibrillation, hypertension, and previous TIA. He takes Metoprolol, Perindopril, and Aspirin. Allergic to Penicillin — causes a rash."

A – Assessment: "On our arrival, BP was 82/50 with a heart rate of 112. He was pale, lethargic, with dry mucous membranes and reduced skin turgor. GCS was 14. ECG showed sinus tachycardia only. We established IV access and administered 500mL of normal saline in two boluses. BP improved to 102/64, heart rate down to 96, and GCS improved to 15. He's responded well to fluids with no signs of overload."

R – Recommendation: "He'll likely need ongoing IV rehydration and bloods to check his electrolytes and renal function. The wife is following behind and can provide more history."

Key Handover Points:

  • Quantify the fluid given and the response
  • Highlight cardiac history and medication (Penicillin allergy)
  • Note the clinical improvement — confirms your working diagnosis
  • Clear recommendation supports continuity of care

Real-Job Translation:

A good handover saves time and prevents errors. The ED staff need to know what you found, what you did, and how the patient responded. Be concise but complete.

Debrief (10–15 minutes)

Reflective Questions:

1. Clinical Reasoning: How did you differentiate dehydration from cardiac causes of hypotension in this patient?
2. Protocol Application: What are the key principles of the Fluid Resuscitation guideline that guided your treatment?
3. Single Responder Challenges: What did you prioritise while waiting for backup? How did this affect patient outcomes?
4. Medication Considerations: How did the patient's beta-blocker and ACE inhibitor influence your assessment and treatment?
5. Fluid Therapy: Why did we use smaller boluses (250mL) rather than larger volumes? When would you escalate to EOC?
6. Team Integration: How effectively did you brief and utilise the volunteer backup crew?
7. Transport Decision: At what point was transport "locked in" as the priority? Could anything have changed this?

Take-Home Messages:

1. Always exclude cardiac causes before attributing hypotension to dehydration — the protocol explicitly states this.
2. Subacute dehydration (days) presents differently to acute — expect a more insidious picture with greater total volume deficit.
3. Beta-blockers mask compensatory tachycardia — don't rely on heart rate alone to assess volume status.
4. Fluid resuscitate judiciously in patients with cardiac history — smaller boluses, frequent reassessment, watch for overload.
5. "Assess, treat, reassess" — response to fluid confirms or challenges your diagnosis.
6. Transport to hospital is the goal — prehospital care is stabilisation, not definitive treatment.
7. Single responder efficiency: prioritise assessment and IV access before backup arrives — this accelerates the overall care timeline.

Optional Scenario Variations

▼

Variation 1: No Response to Fluids

After 500mL, BP remains 84/52. Patient becomes more confused. What differentials do you now consider? When do you escalate to EOC? Consider: underlying sepsis, occult bleeding, cardiac cause missed on initial assessment.

Variation 2: Fluid Overload

After second bolus, patient develops dyspnoea and you hear bilateral crackles. SpO2 drops to 92%. How do you manage this? What does this tell you about the underlying condition?

Variation 3: Hyperthermia Component

Same scenario, but temperature is 39.8°C and the house has no air conditioning. Patient was working outside yesterday. Apply the protocol's hyperthermia-specific interventions: remove clothing, moisten skin, cold packs to groin/axillae.

Variation 4: Extended Transport

Nearest hospital is 90 minutes away. Retrieval services unavailable. How does this change your fluid strategy? What ongoing care is required? Consider: larger total fluid volumes, more frequent reassessment, EOC consultation for guidance.

Protocol Quick Reference

▼

CPG — Fluid Resuscitation in Medical Emergencies — P

Principle: Fluid resuscitation should be used to treat hypovolaemia in conjunction with other treatments such as universal care, posture and where possible, treatment of the cause.

Exclusion: This guideline does not apply to hypotension resulting from a primary cardiac cause.

Goal: Transport to hospital is the desired outcome in all cases.

General Approach to Fluid Resuscitation:

  • Establish IV access
  • Administer NaCl 0.9% boluses (250–500mL)
  • Reassess after each bolus
  • Repeat as needed
  • Prompt transport

Symptomatic Dehydration with Hypotension:

  • Adopt general approach as above
  • Consider if acute or subacute dehydration
  • If secondary to hyperthermia: remove excessive clothing, moisten skin, enhance evaporation, apply cold packs to groin and axillae

Related Protocols:

  • CPG-133-P Severe Haemorrhage (for haemorrhagic hypovolaemia)
  • CPG-143-P Sepsis Adult (if sepsis suspected)
  • CPG-173-P Sepsis Paediatric (paediatric cases)
