Narcotic Overdose - AO

Source Protocol: CPP-011-AO | SAAS Clinical Practice Protocols

⏱ 60-75 minutes

Title & Context

This walk-through scenario prepares Volunteer AO crews to assess and manage a patient with suspected narcotic overdose. The scenario emphasises airway assessment, ventilation support, EOC consultation for naloxone administration, and transport to definitive care.

Protocol Scope

Narcotic overdose may present from recreational and prescribed medication use. The aim is to re-establish airway control and effective ventilation. IM Naloxone requires EOC Clinician consultation and direction.

Learning Objectives

  • Recognise signs and symptoms of narcotic overdose
  • Differentiate between effective and ineffective breathing with airway compromise
  • Demonstrate safe airway management and IPPV techniques
  • Understand indications and process for EOC consultation regarding naloxone
  • Administer IM Naloxone 400 microg as directed by EOC Clinician
  • Manage patient who regains consciousness post-naloxone
  • Provide appropriate transport and handover

Pre-Brief (≈10 minutes)

Gather your crew and review the following before commencing the scenario:

  • Review CPP-011-AO Narcotic Overdose protocol
  • Confirm roles: Clinician and Driver
  • Check equipment readiness
  • Discuss airway assessment and IPPV technique
  • Review IM injection technique (deltoid/vastus lateralis)
  • Discuss potential scene safety considerations
Scene Safety Reminder

Narcotic overdose scenes may involve drug paraphernalia, sharps, unknown substances, or agitated bystanders. Prioritise crew safety. Do not enter until scene is assessed as safe.

📍 Scenario: Port Lincoln Foreshore

Location: Public toilet block near the marina carpark, Port Lincoln foreshore

Time: 2230 hours, Thursday evening

Weather: Cool, 12°C, clear night

Caller: Security guard doing routine patrol

Break Point 1 Discussion / Planning

En-Route Planning

📟 MDT DISPATCH INFORMATION
CASE: P2 - OVERDOSE
TIME: 22:31
LOCATION: Public toilets, Marina Carpark, Port Lincoln Foreshore
CALLER: Security guard - not with patient
INFO: Male found unresponsive in toilet cubicle. Possible drug use. Security has called out - no response. Unknown if breathing.
BACKUP: MedSTAR retrieval 45+ mins if required
NOTES: Security will meet crew at carpark entrance. Police notified - ETA unknown.

Facilitator Prompts

  • What does "P2 Overdose" tell you about expected acuity?
  • What are your scene safety concerns?
  • What equipment will you take in first?
  • What differential diagnoses come to mind?

Expected AO Reasoning

  • Scene safety priority - public toilet, unknown substances, sharps risk
  • Take response bag, Corpuls3, suction, BVM, oxygen
  • Consider: narcotic OD, other drug OD, hypoglycaemia, head injury, stroke, cardiac event
  • Wait for security escort - don't enter alone

Key Teaching Points

  • P2 overdose can rapidly become time-critical if airway/breathing compromised
  • Bring airway equipment - anticipate the worst
  • Security escort provides safety and information

Real-Job Translation

On a real job, you'd be planning access, considering lighting (torches), confirming police attendance, and mentally preparing for airway intervention. The MDT info shapes your approach before you arrive.

On Arrival – Scene Management

Security guard meets you at the carpark entrance. He's anxious and provides the following information:

Security Guard Report

"I found him about 10 minutes ago. He's in the second cubicle - door's not locked. I called out but he didn't respond. I could see his legs through the gap - he's on the floor. I didn't touch him. There's some stuff on the floor near him - looks like drug gear."

  • Confirm security has not seen anyone else in the area
  • Request security remains at entrance to direct police
  • Don PPE before entering (gloves, eyewear)
  • Driver collects additional equipment from vehicle
Break Point 2 Discussion / Observation

Doorway / First Impression

What You See

Male patient, appears 30-40 years old, lying supine on the toilet floor. Head tilted back against the wall. Skin appears pale/grey under the fluorescent light. You can see very slow, shallow chest movement - maybe 4-6 breaths per minute. Lips appear dusky. A used syringe and tourniquet visible on the floor beside him. No obvious injuries or blood.

Facilitator Prompts

  • What's your immediate impression?
  • Is the scene safe to approach?
  • What's your first action?
  • Which pathway does this patient fit - "effective breathing" or "ineffective breathing"?

Expected AO Reasoning

  • Classic narcotic overdose presentation - pinpoint pupils, slow/shallow breathing, drug paraphernalia
  • Scene relatively safe - note sharps location, don't kneel on them
  • This is INEFFECTIVE breathing - RR 4-6, cyanosis = airway/breathing compromise
  • Immediate priority: Danger check, then airway and breathing support

Key Teaching Points

  • Respiratory depression is the killer in narcotic overdose
  • RR less than 10 with cyanosis = ineffective breathing pathway
  • Naloxone is NOT your first action - airway and ventilation come first

Real-Job Translation

That doorway pause takes 3-5 seconds but gives you critical information. You've already identified the pathway (ineffective breathing) and your immediate priorities before touching the patient.

Patient Contact & Primary Survey

DRSABCD Approach

  • Danger: Sharps identified - move carefully, dispose safely when possible
  • Response: Verbal stimulus - no response. Painful stimulus (trapezius squeeze) - slight groan only
  • Send for help: Driver to bring stretcher, oxygen, suction closer
  • Airway: Partial obstruction - gurgling sounds, tongue fallen back
  • Breathing: Present but inadequate - RR 4, shallow, SpO2 78% on room air
  • Circulation: Pulse present - weak, regular, rate 52
  • Defibrillation: Not required - has pulse
Critical Finding

Patient has a pulse but breathing is INEFFECTIVE. This is NOT a cardiac arrest - but without intervention, it will become one. Start IPPV immediately.

Break Point 3 Practical Skill / Decision-Making

Initial Observations & Corpuls3 Setup

Initial Observations

Time: 22:38

  • GCS: E1 V1 M2 = 4
  • RR: 4 breaths/min (shallow)
  • SpO2: 78% (room air)
  • HR: 52 bpm (regular)
  • BP: 88/52 mmHg
  • Pupils: Pinpoint bilaterally
  • Skin: Pale, cool, cyanotic lips
  • BGL: 5.8 mmol/L
  • Temp: 35.8°C

Facilitator Prompts

  • What do these observations tell you?
  • What's the classic narcotic overdose triad?
  • What's your immediate airway intervention?
  • Who manages the airway while observations continue?

Expected AO Reasoning

  • Classic opioid toxidrome: CNS depression, respiratory depression, miosis (pinpoint pupils)
  • SpO2 78% is critically low - brain damage occurring
  • BGL normal - rules out hypoglycaemia as cause
  • Clinician: Open airway (head tilt/chin lift), insert OPA, commence IPPV with BVM + high-flow O2
  • Driver: Continue observations, prepare equipment, document

Key Teaching Points

  • Opioid triad: Decreased LOC + respiratory depression + pinpoint pupils
  • IPPV before naloxone - oxygenation is the priority
  • Target 10-12 breaths/min with BVM, visible chest rise
  • Don't hyperventilate - squeeze, release, count "one-thousand-one"

Real-Job Translation

On a real job, you're multitasking immediately. One crew member controls the airway while the other gets monitoring on. BGL is a quick win to rule out hypo. Every minute of low SpO2 causes harm.

Break Point 4 Discussion / Decision-Making

Screening Tools, Protocols & Working Diagnosis

Facilitator Prompts

  • What's your working diagnosis?
  • What protocol applies?
  • Does this patient have "effective breathing" or "ineffective breathing" per the protocol?
  • What does the protocol direct you to do now?

Expected AO Reasoning

  • Working diagnosis: Narcotic (opioid) overdose
  • Protocol: CPP-011-AO Narcotic Overdose
  • Pathway: INEFFECTIVE breathing, airway compromised (absent gag/cough reflexes)
  • Protocol directs: Basic care including IPPV → Consult EOC Clinician → IM Naloxone as directed

Effective Breathing, Airway Patent

  • Basic care
  • Consider clinical support
  • Provide transport and notify receiving facility

Ineffective Breathing, Airway Compromised

  • If unresponsive and pulseless → CPR per cardiac arrest protocol
  • Basic care including IPPV
  • Consult EOC Clinician who may advise:
  • IM Naloxone 400 microg - repeat doses as directed
  • Request clinical support
  • Provide transport and notify receiving facility

Key Teaching Points

  • The protocol has TWO pathways - you must assess which one applies
  • Naloxone requires EOC consultation - it's not automatic
  • IPPV must be established BEFORE calling EOC

Clinical Decision Point

After 2 minutes of IPPV with high-flow oxygen:

Reassessment

  • SpO2: Improving to 89%
  • HR: 58 bpm
  • Still unresponsive (GCS 4)
  • No gag reflex when OPA checked
  • Pupils remain pinpoint

Decision: Patient requires ongoing IPPV. Time to consult EOC Clinician regarding naloxone administration.

Break Point 5 Practical Skill / Communication

Treatment Initiation & Medication Safety

EOC Consultation Structure

Clinician calls EOC while Driver maintains IPPV:

📞 EOC CALL SCRIPT
"EOC this is [Unit] at Port Lincoln foreshore.

We have a male approximately 35 years, found unresponsive in public toilets with drug paraphernalia present.

He has ineffective breathing - RR was 4, SpO2 78% on arrival. Pinpoint pupils. GCS 4.

We've established IPPV with OPA and high-flow oxygen. SpO2 now 89% and improving. He has a pulse - rate 58.

Requesting advice regarding IM Naloxone per CPP-011-AO."
EOC Clinician Response (Simulated)

"Copy that. Sounds like opioid overdose with respiratory depression. Continue IPPV. I'm authorising IM Naloxone 400 micrograms. Administer now and reassess in 2-3 minutes. Call back if no response - may need repeat dose. Are you able to transport? Notify Port Lincoln Hospital."

IM Naloxone Administration - Step by Step

  • Confirm order: IM Naloxone 400 microg (0.4mg)
  • Check ampoule: Naloxone 400 microg/mL (or 1mg/mL - adjust volume)
  • Check expiry date
  • Draw up correct volume (1mL if 400microg/mL)
  • Select injection site: Deltoid (upper arm) or Vastus Lateralis (lateral thigh)
  • Clean site with alcohol swab
  • Insert needle at 90° angle
  • Aspirate briefly (check not in vessel)
  • Inject medication steadily
  • Withdraw needle, apply pressure
  • Dispose of sharps safely
  • Document time and dose given

Facilitator Prompts

  • Walk through the IM injection technique
  • What concentration is your Naloxone? How much do you draw up?
  • Why do we aspirate before injecting IM?
  • What response are you looking for?
  • What's the risk if we give too much naloxone too quickly?

Expected AO Reasoning

  • 400 microg = 0.4 mg = 1mL of standard ampoule
  • Aspiration checks needle isn't in a blood vessel
  • Looking for: Increased RR, improved LOC, pupil dilation
  • Risk of rapid reversal: Acute withdrawal - agitation, aggression, vomiting, risk of aspiration
Naloxone Considerations

Goal: Restore adequate breathing - NOT full consciousness. We want the patient breathing adequately, not wide awake and combative.

Duration: Naloxone lasts 20-90 minutes. Many opioids last longer. Patient may re-sedate → continuous monitoring essential.

Key Teaching Points

  • Naloxone is NOT a magic fix - it's an adjunct to ventilation
  • EOC authorisation is mandatory for AO administration
  • Aim for breathing improvement, not full reversal
  • Prepare for agitation if reversal is rapid
  • Short acting - patient may re-sedate

Real-Job Translation

On a real job, you're maintaining IPPV while preparing the naloxone - this requires coordination. Driver holds the mask seal while Clinician draws up and administers. Communication is constant: "Keep bagging, I'm giving the naloxone now."

Reassessment Post-Naloxone

Time: 3 minutes post-naloxone administration

Patient Response

  • Patient begins to stir
  • RR increases to 12/min spontaneously
  • SpO2: 96% on high-flow O2
  • HR: 72 bpm
  • BP: 102/68 mmHg
  • GCS improving: E2 V2 M4 = 8
  • Pupils: Dilating slightly
  • Begins to move arms purposefully
Positive Response

This is a good response. Continue monitoring closely. Maintain oxygen via mask. Be prepared for the patient to become more alert and potentially confused/agitated.

Break Point 6 Discussion / Decision-Making

Reassessment & Escalation Decision

Facilitator Prompts

  • Has the naloxone worked?
  • Do you need a repeat dose?
  • What's your ongoing plan?
  • What if the patient wants to refuse transport?
  • What are you monitoring for during transport?

Expected AO Reasoning

  • Good response - adequate spontaneous breathing restored
  • No repeat dose needed currently
  • Must transport - naloxone may wear off before opioid
  • If patient refuses: Explain risks clearly, involve EOC, document thoroughly
  • Monitor for: Re-sedation, respiratory depression, vomiting/aspiration

Key Teaching Points

  • Response to naloxone confirms opioid as cause
  • Transport is essential - re-sedation risk is real
  • Patient capacity to refuse may be impaired
  • Continuous monitoring throughout transport
Break Point 7 Practical / Planning

Extrication & Transport Planning

Current Patient Status

Patient is now semi-conscious, GCS 11 (E3 V3 M5). He's confused, asking "What happened? Where am I?" He's cooperative but unsteady. He states his name is "Dave" and says he "used a bit too much."

Facilitator Prompts

  • How do you get this patient out of the toilet cubicle safely?
  • What position for transport?
  • What equipment stays with the patient?
  • What's your transport priority?

Expected AO Reasoning

  • Assist patient to stand if able, support to carry chair or stretcher
  • Don't let patient walk unassisted - fall risk, may re-sedate
  • Transport on stretcher with rails up
  • Position: Semi-recumbent or recovery position if vomiting risk
  • Keep suction immediately available
  • Continuous SpO2 and observation throughout
  • Priority 2 transport - stable but requires monitoring

Key Teaching Points

  • These patients can deteriorate rapidly - stay vigilant
  • Suction must be ready - vomiting common post-naloxone
  • Recovery position if any concern about airway protection

Real-Job Translation

Extrication from confined spaces is always challenging. The patient might seem better but they're not stable. Use the carry chair to get them to the stretcher. Keep talking to them - their responsiveness is your early warning system.

Break Point 8 Practical Skill / Communication

ISBAR Handover

Practice the Handover

📋 ISBAR HANDOVER
I - Identify:
"I'm [Name] from SAAS. This is Dave, approximately 35-year-old male."

S - Situation:
"Dave was found unresponsive in public toilets with evidence of IV drug use. Suspected opioid overdose."

B - Background:
"Found at 2238 with ineffective breathing - RR 4, SpO2 78%, GCS 4, pinpoint pupils. Drug paraphernalia on scene. Unknown medical history - unable to obtain collateral. No medications or allergies known."

A - Assessment:
"On arrival, patient had respiratory depression requiring IPPV for approximately 5 minutes. Responded to IM Naloxone 400 microg given at 2245 following EOC authorisation. Currently GCS 11, RR 14, SpO2 97% on 8L O2, BP 108/70, HR 76. Alert but confused."

R - Recommendation:
"He'll need ongoing monitoring for re-sedation - the naloxone may wear off. He's at risk of aspiration. May benefit from social work or drug and alcohol services referral."

Facilitator Prompts

  • Practise delivering this handover clearly and concisely
  • What's the most important information the ED needs?
  • What are you recommending and why?

Key Teaching Points

  • Time of naloxone administration is critical for ED planning
  • Mention risk of re-sedation explicitly
  • Honest handover - "IV drug use" not judgmental but accurate

Real-Job Translation

A clear ISBAR ensures the ED team understands why this patient needs close monitoring. The timing of naloxone tells them when to expect potential re-sedation. Your recommendation prompts them to consider the whole patient - not just the acute overdose.

Debrief (10-15 minutes)

Reflective Questions

  1. What clinical signs confirmed this was an opioid overdose rather than another cause of unconsciousness?
  2. Why is IPPV the priority intervention before naloxone?
  3. What information did you need to provide EOC to get naloxone authorisation?
  4. How did crew roles work during this scenario? What would you do differently?
  5. What's the risk of naloxone wearing off before the opioid? How does this affect your transport planning?
  6. How would you handle this patient if they became agitated or aggressive post-naloxone?
  7. What scene safety considerations did you address?

Take-Home Messages

  • Oxygenation first, naloxone second - IPPV saves lives while you prepare the antidote
  • Naloxone requires EOC authorisation - establish IPPV, then call
  • Aim for breathing, not consciousness - partial reversal is safer than full reversal
  • Naloxone is short-acting - patients can re-sedate, transport and monitor continuously
  • Scene safety is paramount - sharps, unknown substances, unpredictable people
  • These patients need hospital - even if they feel "fine" after naloxone
  • Non-judgmental care matters - professional, compassionate care regardless of circumstances

Reference Information

Opioids bind to receptors in the brainstem that control respiratory drive. This causes:

  • Decreased respiratory rate
  • Decreased tidal volume
  • Hypoxia → hypercapnia → respiratory acidosis
  • CNS depression (sedation, unconsciousness)
  • Miosis (pinpoint pupils) - parasympathetic effect

Death occurs from respiratory arrest leading to hypoxic cardiac arrest.

  • Mechanism: Competitive opioid receptor antagonist
  • Onset: IM 2-5 minutes (IV faster but not AO scope)
  • Duration: 20-90 minutes (shorter than most opioids)
  • AO Dose: 400 microg IM, repeat as directed by EOC
  • Side effects: Acute withdrawal syndrome - agitation, nausea, vomiting, tachycardia, hypertension

Not all "found unconscious" patients are opioid overdoses. Consider:

  • Hypoglycaemia (check BGL early)
  • Head injury (look for signs of trauma)
  • Stroke (focal neurological signs)
  • Post-ictal state (evidence of seizure)
  • Other drug overdose (stimulants, sedatives, alcohol)
  • Sepsis (fever, infection source)
  • Cardiac event

The opioid triad (CNS depression + respiratory depression + miosis) + drug paraphernalia points strongly to opioid overdose.

Optional Scenario Variations

Variation 1: No Response to Naloxone

Patient shows no improvement after 400 microg naloxone. EOC directs repeat dose. Still no improvement. Consider: mixed overdose (benzodiazepines + opioids), non-opioid cause, very high opioid load. Continue IPPV and transport urgently.

Variation 2: Aggressive Patient Post-Naloxone

Patient wakes abruptly, becomes agitated and combative. Attempts to remove monitoring, refuses care, wants to leave. Practise de-escalation, safety positioning, EOC consultation regarding capacity. Police may be required.

Variation 3: Paediatric Overdose

18-month-old child found with grandmother's oxycodone tablets nearby. Modify approach: smaller BVM, paediatric OPA sizing, weight-based naloxone dosing via EOC, parental communication, mandatory transport.

Variation 4: Cardiac Arrest on Arrival

Patient found pulseless and apnoeic. Switch to CPP-002-AO Cardiac Arrest protocol. CPR, AED, request backup. Naloxone is NOT indicated in cardiac arrest - focus on high-quality CPR and defibrillation if shockable.
