Care Plan Interview — Type 2 Diabetes

GP Clinic Practice Module

Welcome to the Module

This module walks you through a GP Chronic Condition Management Plan (GPCCMP) care plan interview for a patient with Type 2 Diabetes.

You will work through a simulated patient scenario — step by step — practising the structured interview, clinical reasoning, and documentation skills that practice nurses use every day.

Setting: General practice clinic. Scheduled 45-minute care plan appointment. You are the practice nurse preparing the GPCCMP for the GP to review and finalise.
🎯 Learning Objectives
  1. Understand the GPCCMP framework and the practice nurse's role in care plan preparation
  2. Conduct a structured care plan interview for a patient with Type 2 Diabetes
  3. Review and interpret key clinical indicators — HbA1c, eGFR, Urine ACR, lipids
  4. Screen for diabetes-related complications — feet, eyes, kidneys, cardiovascular risk
  5. Identify appropriate allied health referrals and prioritise within the Medicare allocation
  6. Document findings and recommendations for the GP to review and finalise
📚 Understanding GPCCMPs

From 1 July 2025, the GP Chronic Condition Management Plan (GPCCMP) replaced the previous GP Management Plan (GPMP) and Team Care Arrangements (TCA) as a single, streamlined plan.

  • Eligibility: Patient has at least one chronic condition present (or likely) for 6+ months, or a terminal condition
  • No specific condition list — it is the GP's clinical judgement
  • Preparation: Once per 12 months (if necessary)
  • Reviews: Every 3 months
  • MBS Items: 965 (prepare, face-to-face), 967 (review, face-to-face)
  • Fee: $156.55 for preparation or review

Practice nurses can assist the GP in preparing or reviewing a GPCCMP. This typically means:

  • Conducting the structured care plan interview with the patient
  • Reviewing recent pathology results and screening status
  • Assessing current symptoms, medication adherence, and lifestyle
  • Identifying complication risks and screening gaps
  • Recommending allied health referrals for GP consideration
  • Documenting findings so the GP can review and finalise the plan
Scope note: The GP makes the final clinical decisions, signs the plan, and writes referrals. Your role is to gather comprehensive, structured information to support that process.

Patients with a GPCCMP can access Medicare-subsidised allied health services:

  • Up to 5 individual visits per calendar year (10 for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander patients)
  • Services can be one type or a mix — e.g. 2 podiatry + 2 dietitian + 1 diabetes educator
  • T2DM patients can also access up to 8 group sessions per year (diabetes education, dietetics, exercise physiology) — these are additional to the 5 individual visits
  • GP refers directly via letter (no more EPC referral form)
  • Referrals valid for 18 months
  • Providers available: podiatrist, dietitian, diabetes educator, exercise physiologist, physiotherapist, psychologist, occupational therapist, and others
Key point: With only 5 individual visits per year, prioritisation matters. Group sessions for T2DM patients are a valuable way to extend the reach of allied health support.

💬 The Care Plan Interview

1

Opening & Building Rapport

Margaret arrives on time. She's carrying her handbag and a water bottle. She sits down and says, "Here I am again — annual check-up time!" She seems relaxed but slightly apprehensive.

Suggested Approach

  • Greet Margaret warmly — she's a familiar patient, use that rapport
  • Explain the purpose of today's appointment: "We're going to go through your diabetes care plan together — check how things are going, look at your recent blood results, and make sure we've got the right support in place for you."
  • Set expectations for time: "It'll take about 30–40 minutes. The GP will then review everything and finalise the plan."
  • Ask if she has any particular concerns she wants to discuss today

Margaret Says

"That sounds good. I have to be honest, I've been a bit slack with things lately. But I'm here, so that's something, isn't it?"

Key Point

Acknowledge her honesty without judgement. A care plan interview works best as a collaborative conversation, not an interrogation. She's already signalling that her self-management has slipped — that's useful information delivered early because she feels safe enough to say it.

2

General Wellbeing Check

Before diving into the diabetes-specific questions, you want to get a sense of how Margaret is going overall. This helps frame the rest of the interview.

Suggested Questions

  • "How have you been feeling generally over the past few months?"
  • "How's your energy levels? Sleep?"
  • "Any new aches or pains, or anything that's been worrying you?"
  • "How are things at home? Getting out and about okay?"
  • "How's your mood been?"

Margaret Says

"Look, I've been alright. A bit tired, maybe more than usual. My knees are slowing me down — I used to walk around the block but I've stopped doing that. I've put on a few kilos. My daughter worries about me being on my own but I manage fine. I just don't cook proper meals as much when it's just me."

Key Point

Several important themes here: fatigue (could be poor glycaemic control), reduced mobility (OA knees limiting exercise), weight gain, poor diet (living alone), and social isolation. These are all modifiable factors that will inform the care plan. Note them all.

Document

Increased fatigue. Reduced mobility due to OA knees — ceased daily walks. Weight gain reported. Living alone — reduced motivation to prepare meals. Mood appears stable. Daughter nearby but not daily contact.

3

Reviewing Blood Sugar Control

Time to review Margaret's HbA1c and blood glucose results with her. Her HbA1c has risen from 7.6% to 8.2% — above target and worsening. This is a key conversation.

Suggested Approach

  • Share the result in plain language: "Your HbA1c — that's the 3-month average blood sugar reading — has gone up from 7.6 to 8.2. We'd ideally like it under 7."
  • Ask about home monitoring: "Are you checking your blood sugars at home? What sort of readings are you getting?"
  • Explore what might have changed: "What do you think might have contributed to it going up?"
  • Ask about symptoms: any increased thirst, urination, blurred vision?
  • Avoid blame — focus on understanding and problem-solving together

Margaret Says

"Oh, that's gone up, has it? I haven't been checking at home much — I ran out of strips a while back and never got more. I know my eating hasn't been great. Lots of toast and biscuits. I do feel thirstier than I used to, now you mention it. I thought it was just the warm weather."

Red Flag

Increased thirst (polydipsia) is a classic symptom of hyperglycaemia. Combined with worsening HbA1c and reduced self-monitoring, this patient's diabetes control has deteriorated significantly. Flag for GP review — medication adjustment likely needed.

Document

HbA1c 8.2% (up from 7.6%). Not doing home BGL monitoring — ran out of strips. Reports increased thirst. Diet has deteriorated — high carbohydrate intake (toast, biscuits). For GP review re: medication optimisation and BGL monitor supplies.

4

Medication Review & Adherence

You need to understand how Margaret is taking her medications, whether she understands them, and if she's experiencing any side effects.

Suggested Questions

  • "Can you talk me through the medications you take each day?"
  • "Do you ever miss doses? What usually causes that?"
  • "Any side effects — stomach upsets, dizziness, anything like that?"
  • "Do you know what each medication is for?"
  • "Do you use a Webster pack or pill organiser?"
  • "Have you had any hypos — shaky, sweaty, feeling faint?"

Margaret Says

"I take them in the morning — the diabetes ones and the blood pressure one. The cholesterol one at night. I'm pretty good with that, I use a little pill box my daughter got me. Sometimes I forget the night one. No real side effects that I notice. I had a funny turn a couple of months ago — went a bit shaky and sweaty before lunch. Had a glass of juice and felt better. Only happened once."

Flag for GP

The "funny turn" sounds like it may have been a hypoglycaemic episode — Gliclazide (sulfonylurea) can cause hypos, especially if meals are irregular. Margaret lives alone, which increases the risk if a severe hypo occurs. Flag for GP: review Gliclazide in context of irregular eating patterns and solo living.

Document

Reports good adherence to morning medications. Occasional missed evening Atorvastatin dose. Uses pill organiser. One possible hypoglycaemic episode ~2 months ago — tremor, diaphoresis, resolved with juice. Lives alone — hypo risk. For GP review: Gliclazide appropriateness, consider hypo education.

5

Complication Screening — Feet

Margaret hasn't seen a podiatrist in over 2 years. Diabetic foot disease is a major complication. You need to ask about symptoms and flag the screening gap.

Suggested Questions & Assessment

  • "Have you noticed any numbness, tingling, or pins and needles in your feet?"
  • "Any sores, cuts, or blisters that have been slow to heal?"
  • "Do you check your feet regularly?"
  • "What sort of footwear do you usually wear?"
  • "When did you last see a podiatrist?"
  • If within your scope and practice protocols — visual inspection of feet for skin integrity, colour, deformity, calluses, nail condition

Margaret Says

"My feet are alright, I think. Actually, I do get a bit of tingling at night sometimes — like pins and needles in my toes. I don't really look at them, to be honest. I wear slip-on shoes mostly. I haven't been to the podiatrist in ages — I meant to book but never got around to it."

Red Flags

  • Tingling/pins and needles — possible early peripheral neuropathy
  • Not checking feet — risk of undetected injury
  • Slip-on shoes — may not provide adequate protection
  • 2+ years since podiatry — overdue for annual diabetic foot assessment

Document

Reports intermittent tingling/paraesthesia in toes — nocturnal. Not performing self-foot checks. Wears slip-on shoes. Last podiatry >2 years ago. Recommend: urgent podiatry referral, foot care education, visual foot check today if able.

6

Complication Screening — Eyes

Margaret's last eye review was 14 months ago. Annual diabetic retinopathy screening is recommended.

Suggested Questions

  • "When did you last have your eyes checked by the optometrist?"
  • "Any changes to your vision — blurring, floaters, difficulty reading?"
  • "Did the optometrist mention anything at your last visit?"

Margaret Says

"I went about a year ago, maybe a bit more. Everything was fine then. My reading glasses are okay. I haven't noticed anything different with my eyes."

Key Point

Even though Margaret reports no symptoms, diabetic retinopathy is often asymptomatic in the early stages. With worsening glycaemic control, the risk increases. She's overdue for her annual screen — needs a referral back to the optometrist.

Document

Last optometry review ~14 months ago — reported normal. No current visual symptoms. Overdue for annual diabetic retinal screening. Recommend: optometrist referral.

7

Kidneys & Cardiovascular Risk

Margaret's eGFR has dropped from 72 to 68 and her Urine ACR has crossed into the abnormal range (4.2 mg/mmol). Her lipids are also above target. This is an important clinical conversation.

Suggested Approach

  • Explain kidney function in plain language: "Your kidney blood tests are showing some early changes. There's a small amount of protein leaking into your urine that wasn't there before — that can be an early sign that the diabetes is starting to affect your kidneys."
  • Reassure but be honest: "It's early, and there are things we can do about it. That's why we check every year."
  • Discuss cardiovascular risk: "Your cholesterol numbers have also gone up. With diabetes, we like to keep these lower than normal because of the extra risk to your heart."
  • Ask: "Any swelling in your ankles? Shortness of breath? Chest pain?"

Margaret Says

"My kidneys? That's a bit scary. Is it serious? I don't have any swelling or anything like that. No chest pain. I do get a bit puffed going up stairs but I put that down to being unfit."

Key Points

  • Urine ACR 4.2 mg/mmol (normal <3.0) — this is early albuminuria, a marker of diabetic nephropathy. Still early and potentially reversible with good BP and BGL control.
  • eGFR 68 — CKD stage 2. Not yet requiring specialist referral (that's at eGFR <30), but trend is important.
  • LDL 3.2 mmol/L — well above the target of <2.0 for a diabetic patient. Atorvastatin dose may need reviewing.
  • Perindopril (ACE inhibitor) is renoprotective — important to maintain.

Document

Discussed kidney results — early albuminuria noted (ACR 4.2, was 2.8). eGFR declining (68, was 72). No oedema, no chest pain. SOB on exertion — ?deconditioning vs review. LDL 3.2 (target <2.0). For GP review: repeat ACR to confirm, Atorvastatin dose review, BP target review, Perindopril to continue.

8

Lifestyle Assessment

You need to explore Margaret's diet, physical activity, smoking status, and alcohol intake. These are modifiable risk factors and form a core part of the care plan.

Suggested Questions

  • Diet: "Can you walk me through a typical day of eating?" / "Who does the cooking and shopping?"
  • Activity: "What does a typical day look like physically?" / "You mentioned your knees have stopped you walking — are there other things you can do?"
  • Smoking: "You quit smoking 5 years ago — well done. Any temptation to go back?"
  • Alcohol: "How much would you drink in a typical week?"
  • Hydration: "What do you mainly drink during the day?"

Margaret Says

Diet: "I have toast for breakfast. Might have a sandwich or crackers and cheese for lunch. Dinner is whatever's easy — sometimes just eggs on toast again, or a frozen meal. I snack on biscuits with my tea. I know it's terrible."

Activity: "Not much, honestly. I potter around the house and garden. I used to walk but my knees hurt too much now. I'd like to do something but I don't know what I can do with bad knees."

Smoking: "No, I'm done with that. Don't miss it at all."

Alcohol: "I'll have a couple of glasses of wine on the weekend if my friend comes over. Maybe two or three."

Key Points

  • Diet is high in refined carbohydrates and low in vegetables, protein, and fibre. This directly impacts BGL control. She would benefit significantly from a dietitian referral.
  • Activity is very limited. OA knees are a real barrier. An exercise physiologist could design a program around her limitations (e.g., chair exercises, swimming, resistance bands).
  • Smoking cessation — maintained. Positive reinforcement.
  • Alcohol — 2–3 standard drinks, weekends only. Within guidelines but worth monitoring. Alcohol can affect BGL.

Document

Diet: high refined carbohydrate, low vegetable/protein intake. Cooks minimally — lives alone. Activity: minimal, limited by OA knees — ceased walking. Willing to explore alternatives. Ex-smoker 5 years — no relapse risk. Alcohol: 2–3 std drinks/week (social). Recommend: dietitian referral, exercise physiologist referral.

9

Mental Health & Wellbeing

Diabetes distress and depression are common in people managing chronic conditions. Living alone in a small town adds another layer. A brief check here is important.

Suggested Approach

  • "Managing diabetes is a lot of work. How do you feel about it all?"
  • "Do you ever feel overwhelmed or fed up with it?"
  • "How's your sleep?"
  • "Do you get out socially? See friends or family regularly?"
  • If indicated, consider a screening tool (e.g., K10, PHQ-2) per your practice protocol

Margaret Says

"I have my moments. Some days I think, 'What's the point?' — especially when the numbers keep going up no matter what I do. But I'm not depressed or anything. I see my friend Helen most weeks. My daughter calls every day. I sleep okay — up to the loo once or twice at night, but that's just age, isn't it?"

Key Points

  • "What's the point?" — this is diabetes distress, not necessarily clinical depression, but worth monitoring
  • Nocturia (up 1-2x/night) — could be related to hyperglycaemia (osmotic diuresis) rather than "just age"
  • She does have social connections — positive protective factor
  • Consider recommending a mental health review at the next visit if distress continues

Document

Reports occasional diabetes distress ("what's the point") but denies persistent low mood. Social connections maintained. Sleep adequate but nocturia 1–2x/night — ? related to hyperglycaemia. Monitor mental health at review. Consider K10 screening at next visit.

10

Referrals, Goals & Wrapping Up

You've gathered comprehensive information. Now it's time to discuss what support Margaret can access, set some goals together, and explain what happens next with the care plan.

Suggested Approach

  • Summarise what you've discussed: "We've covered a lot today. Let me summarise the main things."
  • Explain the allied health referral process: "As part of your care plan, the GP can refer you to some specialists who are partly covered by Medicare — up to 5 visits a year. Plus, because you have diabetes, you can also access group sessions for things like exercise and diet."
  • Discuss which referrals you'd recommend (for GP to finalise)
  • Ask Margaret about her own goals: "What would you most like to get on top of?"
  • Explain next steps: "I'll write all this up for Dr [GP]. They'll review it, finalise the plan, and write the referrals. You'll get a copy of the plan."

Margaret Says

"Honestly, I'd like to get my sugars back under control. And I'd really like to be able to do some kind of exercise — I miss being active. I didn't know I could see all those people through Medicare. That's good to know."

Key Points

  • Let the patient identify their own goals — they're more likely to engage
  • Margaret's goals align with clinical priorities — this is a good sign
  • Many patients don't know about allied health entitlements under the GPCCMP. Education about what's available is part of your role
  • Frame referrals as support, not criticism — "These people can help you with the things we've talked about"

Document

Patient goals: improve blood sugar control, return to physical activity. Discussed allied health referral options. For GP: finalise GPCCMP, write referrals, review medications. Plan copy to patient.

📋 T2DM Care Plan Interview Checklist

Use this as a reference during real care plan interviews. Click to check off items as you go.

  • Open the conversation — explain purpose, set expectations, ask about concerns
  • General wellbeing — energy, sleep, mood, social situation
  • Review HbA1c — explain result, compare to previous, explore reasons for change
  • Home BGL monitoring — frequency, equipment, understanding of results
  • Medication review — adherence, understanding, side effects, hypo history
  • Feet — symptoms (tingling, numbness, pain), self-checks, podiatry status, visual inspection
  • Eyes — last optometrist visit, any visual changes, retinal screen due?
  • Kidneys — review eGFR and ACR, explain results in plain language
  • Cardiovascular risk — BP, lipids, review targets, symptoms
  • Diet — typical intake, barriers, cooking/shopping capacity
  • Physical activity — current level, barriers, willingness to change
  • Smoking and alcohol — current status, quantity
  • Mental health — diabetes distress, mood, sleep, social connections
  • Allied health referrals — identify needs, explain entitlements, prioritise
  • Patient goals — what matters to them, barriers, action plan
  • Explain next steps — GP review, plan finalisation, referral letters, plan copy
  • Document everything — structured notes for GP to review and finalise GPCCMP
