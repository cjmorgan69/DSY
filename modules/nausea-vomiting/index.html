Nausea & Vomiting — Volunteer AO Scenario

Walk-through scenario exercise with integrated break points for skills practice, clinical discussion, and operational decision-making.

SAAS Nausea & Vomiting — AO Pre-Hospital / Rural 2 x Vol AO ~90 minutes

Title & Context

A 62-year-old female on a rural property has been vomiting persistently for eight hours and is now unable to keep fluids down. Her husband calls when she nearly faints walking from the bathroom. A 2-person Volunteer AO crew responds from the local station.

Source Protocol: SAAS Nausea and Vomiting — AO Protocol. Ondansetron 4 mg ODT administration with contraindication and precaution screening, EOC Clinician escalation pathway, and safe transport planning.
Setting: Wednesday evening, 2035 hours, mid-winter. Rural farmhouse 5 km west of Cummins, SA. Nearest hospital: Cummins District Hospital (~7 min drive). Limited backup. EOC Clinician available by phone.

Learning Objectives

On completion of this scenario walk-through, participants will be able to:

  • Screen for Ondansetron contraindications and precautions before administration
  • Administer Ondansetron 4 mg ODT using correct technique (dry hands, tongue placement, dissolve, swallow)
  • Select appropriate age-based Ondansetron dose (2 mg / 4 mg)
  • Identify when EOC Clinician consultation is required (persistent symptoms or more than one precaution)
  • Perform structured reassessment using Corpuls3 and clinical observations
  • Deliver an ISBAR handover for a patient with persistent nausea and vomiting

Pre-Brief (~10 minutes)

Review the following protocol content before commencing the scenario. Use the toggle sections below to expand each topic. Allocate crew roles: Clinician (patient contact, history, medications) and Driver (observations, Corpuls3, documentation).

Ondansetron is an antiemetic used to control uncontrolled nausea and vomiting, or to facilitate safe transport where vomiting poses risk (head, spine, or eye trauma).

Key principle: Ondansetron is NOT effective for motion sickness. Do not administer where gastric emptying benefits the patient (e.g. certain poisonings).

Available as an Oral Dissolving Tablet (ODT) — safe, convenient, and particularly useful for patients who are actively vomiting or where oral route is preferred. The ODT dissolves on the tongue and does not require water.

Do NOT administer Ondansetron if:
  • Known allergy or hypersensitivity to Ondansetron or any component of the formulation
  • Patient is taking Apomorphine in the setting of Parkinson's disease

Always ask the patient directly: “Are you allergic to any medications?” and “Do you take any medications for Parkinson’s disease?”

Confirm with the patient before administration:
  • Known or congenital prolonged QT syndrome
  • History of irregular heart conditions (atrial fibrillation) or heart failure
  • Known metabolic derangements — particularly low magnesium or potassium

A single precaution does not prevent first-dose administration, but must be documented. If more than one precaution exists, consult the EOC Clinician before administering.

AgeDoseNotes
2 to <6 years2 mg ODTSingle dose only
6 to <16 years4 mg ODTSingle dose only
Adults (≥16 years)4 mg ODTSingle dose; second dose via EOC Clinician authorisation only
Repeat dose: The EOC Clinician may advise a further 4 mg ODT dose for adults only. Paediatric patients receive a single dose only — no repeat without specific medical direction.
  1. Ensure your hands are completely dry — the ODT dissolves on contact with moisture
  2. Peel back the foil on the blister pack — do NOT push the tablet through the foil (it will crumble)
  3. Place the ODT directly on top of the patient’s tongue
  4. Instruct the patient: “Let this dissolve on your tongue, then swallow — do not chew it”
  5. No water is required — the ODT dissolves within seconds
  6. Document: drug name, dose, time administered, route (ODT), patient response
Tip: If the patient is actively vomiting, wait for a brief pause between episodes to place the ODT. The rapid dissolve time means it is absorbed even in patients with ongoing nausea.

Consult the EOC Clinician if:

  • Nausea and/or vomiting persist after the first dose of Ondansetron
  • More than one precaution exists (consult before any administration)
  • Patient is deteriorating or new symptoms develop
  • Uncertainty about whether Ondansetron is indicated

The EOC Clinician may advise a further 4 mg ODT (adults only), discuss alternative management, and/or arrange transport options.

Real-Job Translation

On a real job, you read the MDT while the Driver starts the vehicle. Discuss roles out loud. Clinician plans assessment approach. Driver confirms route and estimates arrival time. Both mentally prepare for a possibly dehydrated, unsteady patient on a rural property at night.

Scene: Gravel driveway leads to a weatherboard farmhouse. Porch light on. David (husband, 65) meets you at the front door, visibly worried. The house smells of cleaning products — he has been cleaning up after repeated vomiting episodes. Margaret is seated on the bathroom floor with a bucket, pale and exhausted.

David reports: “She started vomiting around lunchtime after eating leftover chicken. It just hasn’t stopped. She tried to walk to bed about 20 minutes ago and went really wobbly — I thought she was going to hit the deck.”

Break Point 3

Initial Observations & Corpuls3 Setup

Type: Discussion / Practical Skill

Facilitator Prompts
  • Walk through your Corpuls3 setup — what are you monitoring and why?
  • The heart rate is 104 and irregularly irregular — what does that tell you?
  • BGL is 4.8 and she is on Metformin — is this a concern?
  • Her capillary refill is 3 seconds — what does this indicate?
Expected AO Reasoning
  • Corpuls3: SpO2 probe, NIBP cuff, 4-lead ECG — standard monitoring for a medical patient
  • Irregularly irregular = AF — consistent with her known history, but rate of 104 may be slightly elevated (dehydration, stress)
  • BGL 4.8 is low-normal in a diabetic patient on Metformin who hasn’t eaten — needs monitoring, not treatment yet
  • Capillary refill 3 seconds indicates moderate dehydration — supports clinical picture
  • Manual RR count is important — Corpuls3 does not always capture respiratory rate accurately
Key Teaching Points
  • AF at 104 bpm in a known AF patient is not alarming but document it — tachycardia may be dehydration-driven
  • Always manually count RR — do not rely solely on the monitor
  • Manual BP check if NIBP is unreliable (movement artefact from vomiting)
  • BGL monitoring is especially important in diabetic patients who cannot eat
Real-Job Translation

On a real job, the Driver sets up the Corpuls3 while the Clinician takes history. This happens simultaneously — not one after the other. The Driver calls out observations as they come through. The Clinician listens while talking to the patient. Both crew members build the clinical picture together.

Clinical Decision Point

You now have a clear clinical picture. Margaret has persistent nausea and vomiting for 8 hours, is moderately dehydrated, and has a known history of AF. She needs symptom control and transport for further care.

Working Diagnosis

Likely food poisoning / acute gastroenteritis — supported by timeline (30 min post reheated chicken), isolated vomiting without systemic red flags, and husband ate same meal without symptoms (individual susceptibility or Margaret’s portion may have been stored differently).

Protocol Selection

SAAS Nausea and Vomiting — AO Protocol is indicated. The patient has uncontrolled nausea and vomiting and requires safe transport.

Break Point 4

Screening Tools, Protocol & Working Diagnosis

Type: Decision-Making

Facilitator Prompts
  • What is your working diagnosis and why?
  • What other diagnoses should you consider?
  • Is Ondansetron indicated for this patient?
  • Walk me through the contraindication check — can she receive Ondansetron?
  • What precautions does this patient have?
  • Can you give the first dose, or do you need to call the EOC Clinician first?
Expected AO Reasoning
  • Working diagnosis: food poisoning / acute gastroenteritis (timeline, symptoms, mechanism)
  • Differentials to consider: bowel obstruction (no abdo distension or severe pain), medication side effect (Metformin can cause nausea), cardiac event (no chest pain, no acute ECG changes)
  • Ondansetron IS indicated — uncontrolled nausea and vomiting
  • Contraindication check: No known allergy to Ondansetron. Not on Apomorphine. → CLEAR to proceed
  • Precaution check: History of AF = 1 precaution
  • One precaution does NOT prevent first-dose administration — document the precaution, proceed with caution
  • If she had >1 precaution (e.g. AF + known low potassium), would need to call EOC BEFORE giving
Key Teaching Points
  • Contraindications are ABSOLUTE — if present, do NOT give Ondansetron
  • Precautions require careful screening — one precaution allows first dose, more than one requires EOC consultation first
  • The question about Apomorphine must be asked directly — do not assume
  • Ondansetron is NOT indicated where gastric emptying benefits the patient (certain poisonings) — not relevant here
  • Document your screening clearly in the ePCR
Real-Job Translation

On a real job, this decision point happens quickly — you’ve taken the history, heard the medications, and your mental checklist fires: “AF — that’s a precaution for Ondansetron.” You confirm with the patient, check for other precautions, and decide you can proceed with the first dose. This structured screening becomes second nature with practice.

Treatment Phase — Ondansetron ODT Administration

You have confirmed Ondansetron is indicated and safe to administer. Margaret is an adult (62 years) — the dose is 4 mg ODT.

Medication Safety — 5 Rights Check:
  • Right patient: Margaret Henley, 62 years old, confirmed identity
  • Right drug: Ondansetron 4 mg Oral Dissolving Tablet
  • Right dose: 4 mg (adult dose)
  • Right route: Oral Dissolving Tablet (ODT) — placed on tongue
  • Right time: Now — clinical indication present, contraindications cleared

Practical Skill — ODT Administration (Practice This)

  1. Confirm patient consent: “Margaret, I’d like to give you a tablet that dissolves on your tongue to help with the nausea — is that okay?”
  2. Ensure your hands are completely dry — the tablet dissolves on contact with any moisture
  3. Remove the ODT from the blister pack by peeling back the foil — do NOT push the tablet through (it will crumble and break)
  4. Ask Margaret to open her mouth and place the ODT directly on top of her tongue
  5. Instruct: “Let that dissolve on your tongue, then swallow — don’t chew it, and you don’t need any water”
  6. The ODT dissolves within approximately 10–15 seconds
  7. Confirm with patient: “Has that dissolved? Good — just swallow now”
  8. Document in ePCR: Ondansetron 4 mg ODT administered at [time], one precaution noted (AF), monitoring for effect
  9. Set a reassessment timer — 15 minutes post-administration
Common Errors to Avoid:
  • Handling the ODT with wet or sweaty hands — it dissolves immediately
  • Pushing the tablet through the blister foil — this crushes the fragile ODT
  • Telling the patient to chew the tablet or wash it down with water — neither is required or appropriate
  • Forgetting to document the precaution (AF) alongside the administration
Break Point 5

Treatment Initiation & Medication Safety

Type: Practical Skill

Facilitator Prompts
  • Talk me through the 5 Rights before you administer
  • Show me how you open the blister pack — why not push it through?
  • Demonstrate the administration — hands, placement, patient instruction
  • What are you documenting in the ePCR right now?
  • What would you do differently if this patient was 4 years old?
Expected AO Reasoning
  • 5 Rights check completed and verbalised aloud to crew partner
  • Dry hands — critical for handling ODT
  • Peel foil, not push through — ODT is fragile
  • Place on tongue, allow to dissolve, swallow — no chewing, no water
  • If patient was 4 years old (≥2 to <6 years) — dose would be 2 mg, single dose only
  • If patient was 10 years old (≥6 to <16 years) — dose would be 4 mg, single dose only
  • Document: drug, dose, time, route, precaution noted, monitoring plan
Key Teaching Points
  • Verbalise the 5 Rights to your crew partner — this is a safety check, not a formality
  • ODT technique is simple but must be practiced — handling errors waste medication
  • Age-based dosing must be known — it is different across three age groups
  • Always document the precaution alongside the medication — it justifies your clinical reasoning
Real-Job Translation

On a real job, this takes about 60 seconds. You open the blister, place the tablet, and the patient dissolves and swallows it. The simplicity of ODT administration is one of its advantages — but the decision-making beforehand (contraindications, precautions, dose selection) is where the clinical skill sits. Practice the technique so the administration is smooth and confident, leaving your cognitive capacity for the assessment.

Break Point 6

Reassessment & Escalation Decision

Type: Decision-Making / Discussion

Facilitator Prompts
  • The first dose partially worked — what does the protocol say to do next?
  • What information are you giving the EOC Clinician on the phone?
  • Why is the BGL trending relevant for this patient?
  • The EOC has authorised a second dose — how do you document this?
  • What if the patient had TWO precautions — would your approach to the first dose have been different?
Expected AO Reasoning
  • Protocol requires EOC consultation when symptoms persist after first dose
  • Structured phone report: patient demographics, presentation, treatment given, response, precaution status, current obs
  • BGL trending down in a T2DM patient on Metformin who cannot eat = hypoglycaemia risk
  • Document: “EOC Clinician consulted at [time], authorised second Ondansetron 4 mg ODT”
  • If >1 precaution existed: would have called EOC BEFORE giving even the first dose
Key Teaching Points
  • Trends in observations are more valuable than single readings — track the direction
  • EOC consultation is a protocol requirement, not a sign of weakness — it protects the patient and you
  • Second dose is for ADULTS ONLY — paediatric patients get a single dose
  • BGL monitoring in diabetic patients who cannot eat is essential throughout the job
  • Always document EOC authorisation clearly — it is a medicolegal record
Real-Job Translation

On a real job, calling the EOC Clinician is a 2–3 minute phone call. Be concise, structured, and have your obs in front of you. The Clinician will ask specific questions. Your preparation for this call (having obs, history, and treatment response ready) makes it efficient and professional.

Extrication & Transport

Margaret’s nausea has significantly improved (2/10). Vomiting has ceased. She is stable but dehydrated and requires IV fluid replacement at hospital. The EOC Clinician has advised transport to Cummins District Hospital.

Extrication Plan

  • Use the green carry chair to wheel Margaret from the lounge to the stretcher — do NOT ask her to walk (minimises exertion and syncope risk in a dehydrated patient)
  • Ensure emesis bags are within reach during transfer
  • Secure on stretcher in a position of comfort — semi-reclined if tolerated, lateral if ongoing nausea risk
  • Continue Corpuls3 monitoring during transport
  • Keep emesis bags, towels, and suction accessible
  • Reassess BGL en route — if <4 mmol/L, consider oral glucose if tolerated

Break Point 7

Extrication & Transport Planning

Type: Practical Skill / Discussion

Facilitator Prompts
  • Why carry chair rather than walking the patient?
  • What position are you transporting Margaret in?
  • What equipment must be within arm’s reach during transport?
  • What are you monitoring en route and how often?
  • What would change your transport urgency?
Expected AO Reasoning
  • Carry chair minimises patient exertion — dehydrated patients are at syncope risk if they walk
  • Semi-reclined or lateral position depending on nausea status
  • Emesis bags, suction, towels within reach — vomiting can recur unpredictably
  • Obs every 5–10 minutes en route (short transport) — especially BGL
  • Deterioration triggers: active vomiting recurs, BGL drops below 4, altered consciousness, new symptoms (chest pain, abdominal rigidity)
Key Teaching Points
  • The carry chair is your default patient movement tool — patients with nausea, dizziness, or weakness should not walk unsupported
  • Position of comfort is patient-led but clinically guided
  • Short transport does not mean no monitoring — patients can deteriorate quickly
  • Pre-notify the receiving facility so they can prepare IV access and fluids
Real-Job Translation

On a real job, this is a 7-minute drive. The Clinician is in the back monitoring Margaret. The Driver has pre-notified the hospital. Even on short transports, keep watching the patient and the monitor. Have suction ready — the motion of the vehicle can trigger vomiting in patients with ongoing nausea.

Handover — ISBAR

On arrival at Cummins District Hospital, deliver a structured ISBAR handover to the receiving nurse or medical officer.

—— ISBAR HANDOVER ——
I — Identify: “I’m [name], Volunteer AO from Cummins. This is Margaret Henley, 62 years old.”
S — Situation: “Margaret has had persistent nausea and vomiting for approximately 8 hours, likely food poisoning from reheated chicken at lunch. Her husband called us after she nearly fainted walking from the bathroom.”
B — Background: “She has a history of atrial fibrillation on Metoprolol and Apixaban, and Type 2 Diabetes on Metformin. Allergic to Penicillin — gets a rash. No surgical history of note.”
A — Assessment: “On our assessment she was pale, dehydrated, cap refill 3 seconds. Heart rate 104 irregularly irregular, BP 108/72, SpO2 97%, BGL 4.8. 4-lead showed AF at rate, no acute changes. We identified AF as a precaution for Ondansetron and administered 4 mg ODT at 2045. Nausea partially improved — we consulted the EOC Clinician who authorised a second 4 mg ODT at 2105. Nausea now 1/10, no further vomiting. Current obs: HR 90, BP 116/78, SpO2 98%, BGL 4.5 — trending low-normal.”
R — Recommendation: “She’ll likely need IV fluids and ongoing BGL monitoring given she’s on Metformin and hasn’t eaten. The EOC Clinician was happy with transport here for further assessment.”
Break Point 8

ISBAR Handover

Type: Practical Skill

Facilitator Prompts
  • Deliver the ISBAR handover to the group as if to a receiving nurse
  • What specific information does the hospital need about the Ondansetron?
  • Why is mentioning the AF precaution important in the handover?
  • What recommendation are you making and why?
Expected AO Reasoning
  • Structured ISBAR handover covering all elements concisely
  • Hospital needs to know: drug, dose, time, route, number of doses, EOC authorisation for second dose, precaution documented
  • Mentioning AF as a precaution alerts receiving staff to potential QT concerns and guides their ongoing monitoring
  • Recommendation reflects what the patient needs next (fluids, BGL monitoring) — this shows clinical reasoning
Key Teaching Points
  • ISBAR is a framework, not a script — adapt it to the patient but hit every letter
  • Include trends in your handover, not just current numbers — “BP was 108 now 116”
  • Mention medications given including EOC authorisation — this is part of the medication chain
  • A good recommendation shows you understand what the patient needs, not just what you did
Real-Job Translation

On a real job, you deliver this handover at the hospital bed or triage desk. The receiving nurse is often multitasking — be concise, structured, and clear. Hand over the ePCR or paperwork. Confirm they have received the key points. Do not leave until the handover is acknowledged.

Debrief (10–15 minutes)

Gather the crew and work through the following reflective questions. There are no wrong answers — the goal is to consolidate learning and identify areas for further practice.

Reflective Questions

How did you decide whether this patient was time-critical or not? What would have changed your assessment?
Walk through the contraindication and precaution screening process. How confident are you performing this quickly on a real job?
Margaret had one precaution (AF). If she also had known low potassium, how would your approach to the first dose have changed?
How did you structure your EOC Clinician phone call? What information did you have ready before calling?
Why is BGL monitoring particularly important in this patient? What would you do if BGL dropped to 3.5 mmol/L?
How did the Clinician and Driver roles work during this job? Was anything unclear about who was responsible for what?
Reflect on your ISBAR handover. Was it concise? Did you include trends? Did you make a recommendation?

Take-Home Messages

1. Ondansetron ODT is simple to administer but requires structured screening — contraindications are absolute, precautions require clinical judgement.
2. One precaution allows first-dose administration with documentation. More than one precaution requires EOC consultation before giving.
3. Persistent nausea after the first dose triggers EOC Clinician consultation — this is protocol, not optional.
4. The second dose is for adults only — paediatric patients receive a single dose.
5. Trends in observations matter more than single numbers — track direction and communicate changes.
6. Ondansetron is NOT effective for motion sickness — do not administer for this indication.
7. Early escalation to the EOC Clinician is a sign of good clinical practice, not uncertainty. Have your information ready before you call.

Optional Scenario Variations

Use these variations to extend the session, increase complexity, or tailor to specific learning needs.

Variation 1 — Paediatric Patient (4-Year-Old)

Change the patient to a 4-year-old child with vomiting secondary to gastroenteritis. Dose becomes 2 mg ODT (single dose only). No repeat dose available for paediatric patients. Focus on age-appropriate communication, parental consent, and the technique of placing an ODT on a young child’s tongue. Discuss BGL monitoring in a fasting child and transport urgency for paediatric dehydration.

Variation 2 — Multiple Precautions (AF + Known Low Potassium)

Margaret now has two precautions: AF and known hypokalaemia. This changes the approach — the protocol requires EOC Clinician consultation before administering any Ondansetron. The crew must call the EOC before treatment. Discuss how this changes your workflow and what information the EOC Clinician needs to make a decision.

Variation 3 — Head Trauma with Nausea

Change the scenario to a 45-year-old male who fell from a ladder, hit his head, and is now persistently vomiting. Ondansetron is indicated to facilitate safe transport in patients with head trauma where vomiting complications risk is high. Focus shifts to: is this vomiting from the head injury (raised ICP)? GCS monitoring, c-spine considerations, and the critical importance of antiemetic administration before transport to prevent aspiration and ICP spikes.

Variation 4 — Deterioration En Route

During transport, Margaret’s BGL drops to 3.2 mmol/L and she becomes diaphoretic and confused. Nausea returns. The crew must now manage acute hypoglycaemia in a patient who cannot reliably swallow. Discuss: oral glucose if conscious and able to swallow vs. EOC Clinician escalation for further direction. This variation tests multi-problem management and prioritisation.
