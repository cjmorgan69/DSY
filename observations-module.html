Core Concept

Why This Is Critically Important

As a Volunteer Ambulance Officer, you are often the first clinically trained person at a patient's side. The observations you record in those first minutes are not just numbers on a screen ‚Äî they are the foundation upon which every clinical decision is built.

When you call the EOC (Emergency Operations Centre) Clinician for remote clinical guidance, they cannot see, hear, or feel your patient. They are making potentially life-altering treatment decisions based entirely on what you tell them. If the numbers are wrong, delayed, or missing ‚Äî the clinical advice you receive may not match what your patient actually needs.

Similarly, when paramedics arrive on scene, the first set of observations you've already captured gives them a baseline to measure change against. A blood pressure you took 8 minutes ago might be the single most important piece of data they use to decide between two very different treatment pathways.

The SA Health Rapid Detection and Response (RDR) system exists specifically because research has proven that clinical deterioration is almost always preceded by measurable changes in vital signs ‚Äî often 6 to 24 hours before a critical event. In the pre-hospital setting, you may only have minutes. Your observations are the early warning system.

üö® Critical Safety Point

Inaccurate or delayed observations have been directly linked to failure-to-rescue events across healthcare systems. In the pre-hospital setting, a missed deterioration trend means a missed opportunity to escalate ‚Äî and that gap can be fatal. Every number matters. Every minute matters.

üí° Think About It This Way

The EOC Clinician is effectively treating your patient blind. Your observations are their eyes, ears, and hands. The more accurate and timely the data you provide, the better they can guide your care. You are their clinical sensor on the ground.

Key Knowledge Areas

A complete set of observations aligned with the RDR chart includes:

  • Respiratory Rate (RR) ‚Äî counted manually over 30‚Äì60 seconds, not estimated
  • Oxygen Saturation (SpO‚ÇÇ) ‚Äî via pulse oximetry on the Corpuls 3
  • Supplemental O‚ÇÇ Flow Rate ‚Äî if oxygen is being administered, record L/min and delivery method
  • Blood Pressure (BP) ‚Äî systolic and diastolic, using NIBP on the Corpuls 3
  • Heart Rate / Pulse Rate ‚Äî from Corpuls 3 monitoring or manual palpation
  • Temperature ‚Äî tympanic or other appropriate method
  • Level of Consciousness ‚Äî using AVPU or GCS as appropriate
  • Pain Score ‚Äî 0‚Äì10 numeric rating scale
  • Blood Glucose Level (BGL) ‚Äî when clinically indicated
  • ECG rhythm ‚Äî 4-lead ECG via the Corpuls 3

Respiratory rate is the most commonly missed or estimated vital sign ‚Äî yet it is one of the earliest and most sensitive indicators of deterioration. Always count it properly.

The Rapid Detection and Response (RDR) observation chart was introduced by SA Health in 2012/2013 and revised in 2020. It uses a colour-coded, track-and-trigger system to identify physiological deterioration early.

How the colour zones work:

White Normal range ‚Äî continue routine monitoring
Yellow Mild abnormality ‚Äî increase monitoring frequency, RN/RM review
Orange Moderate abnormality ‚Äî MDT review required, escalate
Red Severe abnormality ‚Äî triggers Medical Emergency Response (MER)

Why this matters for you: Even though you're in the pre-hospital environment, understanding the RDR framework means you can communicate observations in a language that hospital clinicians, EOC clinicians, and receiving paramedics instantly understand. You know which numbers are concerning vs critical.

Connect your patient to the Corpuls 3 as early as possible. The sooner you have monitoring running, the sooner you have a baseline ‚Äî and the sooner the EOC Clinician can access live data to guide your care remotely. Set the NIBP to auto-cycle every 5 minutes for unstable patients.

When you contact the EOC Clinician for clinical guidance, your observations need to be delivered in a structured, concise format. The standard frameworks used are:

ISBAR Framework

  • I ‚Äî Identification (your name, unit, callback)
  • S ‚Äî Situation (what's happening right now)
  • B ‚Äî Background (relevant history, events leading up)
  • A ‚Äî Assessment (your observations and clinical impression)
  • R ‚Äî Recommendation (what you think is needed / what you're asking for)

ATMIST AMBO (Clinical Handover)

  • A ‚Äî Age
  • T ‚Äî Time of incident
  • M ‚Äî Mechanism / Medical complaint
  • I ‚Äî Injuries / assessment findings
  • S ‚Äî Signs (vitals) and symptoms
  • T ‚Äî Treatment given
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Communication Points

When relaying vitals to the EOC Clinician: state the exact number, the time it was taken, and any trend (e.g., "BP was 145/90 at 1420, now 110/70 at 1428 ‚Äî that's a dropping trend"). Trends are often more clinically significant than single readings.

A single blood pressure reading tells you where a patient is right now. Two readings with timestamps tell you where they're heading ‚Äî and that's what saves lives.

  • Baseline observations should be taken as soon as it is safe and practical to do so ‚Äî ideally within the first 2‚Äì3 minutes of patient contact
  • Repeat observations should be taken at regular intervals (every 5 minutes for unstable patients, every 10‚Äì15 for stable)
  • Document the time of every reading ‚Äî "a few minutes ago" is not clinically useful
  • Trend recognition is what separates a data collector from a clinician ‚Äî a heart rate climbing from 88 ‚Üí 102 ‚Üí 118 over 15 minutes tells a story that "HR 118" alone does not

Real-World Example: A patient with chest pain has a BP of 135/85 on your first reading. You might think "that's fine." But if the paramedic arrives 12 minutes later and measures 95/60 ‚Äî they now know there has been a 40-point systolic drop. Without your first reading and its timestamp, they'd only see a "low-ish" BP and might not recognise the urgency of the change.
