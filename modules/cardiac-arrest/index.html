Chest Pain to Cardiac Arrest — Rural ED ALS Scenario

Scenario Overview

Scenario Title

Acute Coronary Syndrome with Sudden Cardiac Arrest in a Rural ED

Setting & Context

  • Location: Small rural hospital ED in South Australia, Level 4–5 facility
  • Distance to regional centre: 65 km (approximately 1 hour by road)
  • Time of day: 22:15 (10:15 PM), Saturday evening
  • Day of week: Saturday — limited after-hours services
  • Current ED status: One other patient in acute bed (stable, awaiting morning discharge). One nursing staff member on break.
  • Staffing present: RN1 (Triage/Primary), RN2 (available within 2 minutes), Doctor (GP/VMO, on call, 10 minutes away by car)
  • Nearest retrieval service: MedSTAR (South Australia Ambulance Service) — estimated response 40–60 minutes depending on current commitments
Rural ED Context

After-hours pathology is not available. Point-of-care troponin assay is not on-site. ECG capability is available but no on-site cardiologist. This scenario emphasises early recognition, stabilisation, pharmacological management per ANZCOR ALS, and timely escalation to MedSTAR for urgent cardiac care retrieval.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this scenario, participants will be able to:

  • Recognise acute coronary syndrome (ACS) presentation and triage appropriately using ATS category 2
  • Conduct rapid ABCDE assessment and initiate early interventions (oxygen, IV access, ECG, analgesia)
  • Identify red flags for imminent cardiac arrest in a patient with ACS
  • Initiate cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) and attach defibrillator within 2 minutes of arrest
  • Execute ANZCOR ALS protocol: rhythm assessment, shock delivery, adrenaline timing, amiodarone dosing
  • Manage hands-on CPR rotations and minimise interruptions in a 3-person team
  • Communicate effectively with MedSTAR for urgent cardiac retrieval using ISBAR
  • Prepare patient for transfer and manage pre-hospital care during transport delay
  • Reflect on rural ED resource constraints and team decision-making under pressure

Key Rural Context: The doctor is 10 minutes away. The ambulance is 40–60 minutes away. You are the immediate resuscitation team. Every action in the first 4 minutes counts.

Monitoring & Resuscitation

  • Adrenaline 1:1000 (1 mg/mL, 10 mL ampoule) — for IV injection during arrest
  • Amiodarone 300 mg IV bolus (pre-drawn or ready to draw)
  • Aspirin 300 mg (chewable or IV)
  • Nitrates (GTN spray or sublingual)
  • Morphine or other IV opioid analgesic
  • Atropine (for symptomatic bradycardia if needed)

Assessment & Diagnostic Tools

  • 12-lead ECG machine
  • Blood glucose meter and test strips
  • Urinalysis capability (if available)
  • Bloods for FBC, U&E, troponin (if pathology available; otherwise arrange transfer for troponin)
  • Portable chest X-ray capability (if radiographer available; not essential for this scenario)

Documentation & Communication

  • Resuscitation record sheet (ANZCOR template or local equivalent)
  • Patient observation chart
  • Phone / communication system for MedSTAR and regional centre contact
  • SA Health Clinical Directives (Acute Coronary Syndrome, Cardiac Arrest)
Equipment NOT on site (rural context)

No CT scanner, no MRI, no invasive cardiac monitoring (central line, intra-arterial), no thrombolytic therapy (not indicated in ANZCOR for this scenario), no on-site cardiology, limited after-hours pathology.

We're here to learn together. If things go sideways or you need to pause and reset, just say 'time out.' Everyone will stop, and we'll rewind and try again.

This is a safe space. You're part of a real team facing a real problem. Let's make sure we do right by this patient."

Role Allocation ▼

RN1 — Triage & Primary Nurse

  • First contact with patient and paramedics (if arriving by ambulance)
  • Conducts ATS triage
  • Initiates HIRAID: History, Red Flags, Assessment
  • Establishes IV access if not done by paramedics
  • Obtains 12-lead ECG
  • During cardiac arrest: Primary chest compression provider (first 2 minutes) then rotates out; calls out events and times
  • Communicates with patient and family

RN2 — Secondary Nurse / Support

  • Joins RN1 after initial assessment triggered by triage category
  • Sets up monitoring (cardiac monitor, SpO2, NIBP, temperature)
  • Prepares medications (aspirin, GTN, morphine)
  • Documents observations and interventions in real-time
  • During cardiac arrest: Assists with defibrillator pads, prepares and administers IV medications (adrenaline, amiodarone) on doctor's direction
  • Takes compression breaks and rotates with RN1 to prevent fatigue
  • Maintains IV access and ensures no interruptions to compressions

Doctor (GP/VMO or Rural Generalist)

  • Arrives approximately 10 minutes after patient presentation (by design, to test RN-led initial care)
  • Conducts focused secondary assessment
  • Makes clinical decisions: diagnosis, treatment escalation, retrieval call
  • During cardiac arrest: Clinical lead — directs rhythm analysis, shock delivery, drug timing, CPR quality
  • Contacts MedSTAR and coordinates retrieval
  • Prepares clinical handover
Facilitator Note

If the facilitator is a doctor, they can play the role of arriving doctor. If not, a pre-briefed confederate or another staff member can fulfil this role. The point is to test the nursing team's early response and then introduce the doctor's leadership during the arrest phase.

Presentation & Triage Phase

1 BREAK POINT: Triage Decision
Discussion + Practical Skill

Scenario Trigger

Time: 22:15 (10:15 PM), Saturday

The ambulance radio crackles. Paramedics are bringing in a patient — ETA 3 minutes. RN1 is briefed verbally by the radio operator:

Ambulance Radio Handover (Abbreviated)

"RN1, Ambulance 42 inbound. 65-year-old male, acute onset chest pain for 20 minutes. Started as central crushing chest pain, radiating to left arm. Patient diaphoretic, nauseous. Vitals: HR 98, BP 165/95, RR 18, SpO2 96% on room air. We've got two large-bore IVs in situ, running 0.9% saline. Oxygen applied at 2 litres per minute nasal prongs. No acute breathing difficulty. Patient given 300 mg aspirin and GTN spray once, which hasn't relieved pain. ETA your facility 2 minutes. Over."

Patient Presentation

Patient arrives by ambulance on stretcher. He is:

  • Conscious, alert, speaking in full sentences but distressed
  • Clutching chest, pale, visibly sweating
  • Breathing rapidly (RR 22)
  • Complaining of severe central chest pain (8/10), radiating to left arm and jaw
  • Wife present, anxious, holding his hand

Facilitator Prompts for RN1

  • "What triage category do you assign to this patient and why?"
  • "What are the immediate actions triggered by this triage category?"
  • "Where does this patient go — waiting room, acute bed, resuscitation area?"
  • "What is your first priority in the next 30 seconds?"

Expected Clinical Reasoning

  • ATS Category 2 (Urgent) — chest pain with haemodynamic compromise (tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis) suspicious for acute coronary syndrome
  • Immediate bed allocation to acute assessment area (not waiting room)
  • First priority: continuous monitoring, pain relief, ensure oxygen delivery, prepare for doctor review
  • Recognise red flags for cardiac event: crushing chest pain, radiation to arm, diaphoresis, nausea, cardiac risk factors (age 65)

Key Teaching Points

  • ATS Category 2 patients require immediate assessment and treatment initiation by nursing staff
  • In a rural ED with doctor 10 minutes away, RN-led initial care is critical
  • Chest pain + diaphoresis + haemodynamic changes = treat as ACS until proven otherwise
  • Paramedic handover provides baseline data; however, ongoing monitoring and reassessment are essential

Real-Job Translation

At 3 AM on a Sunday, this patient walks through the door or arrives by ambulance. You triage within seconds. If you categorise as Category 3, the patient waits, potentially deteriorates, and you've missed the golden window. ATS Category 2 means this patient goes straight to a bed with continuous monitoring, and you alert available staff to stand by. In a rural ED, triage is your first intervention.

RN1 Action

RN1 escorts patient to acute assessment bed, connects to continuous cardiac monitor and SpO2 probe, and calls for RN2 to assist immediately.

Paramedic Handover — Full ISBAR

Paramedic to RN1 (handover at bedside):

I — Identify: "This is Mr. David Thompson, 65 years old. We have his date of birth as 15th March 1960."

S — Situation: "Mr. Thompson presented with acute onset central chest pain about 25 minutes ago. He was at home, watching television with his wife. Pain started suddenly, described as crushing, 8 out of 10 severity."

B — Background: "He has a history of hypertension, controlled on medications. He's a former smoker, quit 10 years ago. No previous cardiac history that we know of. Wife says he's been stressed at work lately. Medications: lisinopril 10 mg daily, atorvastatin 40 mg daily. Allergies: none known."

A — Assessment: "Current vitals: HR 100, BP 168/98, RR 22, SpO2 98% on 2L nasal prongs. He's pale, diaphoretic, and complaining of pain radiating to his left arm and jaw. No signs of respiratory distress. Abdomen soft. Both legs intact, good peripheral perfusion. We're concerned this is ACS."

R — Recommendation: "We've established two large-bore IVs, given aspirin 300 mg and GTN spray once. We'd recommend rapid ECG, serial troponins if available, and urgent cardiology review. We've called ahead, and he's ready for handover to your care for ongoing management and possible retrieval coordination."

Primary Survey & Initial Assessment (HIRAID Framework)

2 BREAK POINT: First Impression & Primary Survey
Practical Skill + Assessment

Facilitator Prompt

"RN1, you're now at the bedside with the patient connected to monitoring. What's your first 60-second assessment? Walk me through your doorway assessment and primary survey using ABCDE."

Expected Actions

A — Airway

  • Patient is alert and speaking in full sentences → airway patent
  • No signs of airway obstruction, stridor, or difficulty swallowing
  • Position upright or semi-recumbent for comfort
  • No immediate airway intervention needed

B — Breathing

  • RR 22 (slightly elevated but within compensatory range)
  • SpO2 98% on 2L nasal prongs (adequate)
  • Bilateral air entry on auscultation (no crackles, wheeze, or focal consolidation)
  • Chest rise symmetrical, no accessory muscle use
  • Current oxygen adequate; continue 2L nasal prongs; escalate if RR increases or SpO2 drops

C — Circulation

  • HR 100 (tachycardia — likely pain and stress response)
  • BP 168/98 (hypertensive, consistent with acute cardiac event)
  • Skin pale and diaphoretic (sympathetic activation)
  • Capillary refill <2 seconds (adequate perfusion peripherally)
  • Radial pulse strong and regular → alert team that rhythm may change
  • Two IV lines already in situ (paramedic-established) — check patency and draw initial bloods if appropriate

D — Disability (Neurological)

  • AVPU: Alert
  • GCS 15/15
  • Pupils equal, reactive to light
  • No focal neurological deficit
  • Appropriate responses to commands

E — Exposure & Environment

  • Expose chest briefly for ECG placement and to check for any trauma
  • Keep patient warm (blankets) — hypothermia is a modifiable risk factor in cardiac events
  • Ensure privacy; wife present but may need to step back for urgent interventions

Observations — Full Set

  • HR: 100 bpm
  • BP: 168/98 mmHg
  • RR: 22 breaths/min
  • SpO2: 98% on 2L nasal prongs
  • Temperature: 36.8°C (normal)
  • Blood glucose (via finger-stick): 6.2 mmol/L (normal — rules out hypo- or hyperglycaemia as primary cause)
  • Pain score: 8/10 central chest pain, radiating to left arm and jaw

Key Teaching Point

The primary survey takes 60–90 seconds. At this point, you've identified the patient is in potential cardiogenic shock with severe pain. The combination of chest pain + diaphoresis + hypertension + tachycardia is classic ACS. Red flags are flying. This patient needs a doctor urgently and may deteriorate. Call for the doctor NOW if not already done.

Real-Job Translation

This is the moment where a rural nurse recognises: "This isn't just chest pain. This is a sick patient. I need my doctor here in the next 5 minutes or I'm escalating." You don't wait for ECG results; you act on the clinical presentation.

3 BREAK POINT: History & Red Flag Screening (HIRAID)
Structured History Taking + Red Flag Assessment

Facilitator Prompt

"RN1, while RN2 is setting up full monitoring and preparing medications, you're taking a focused history and screening for red flags. What do you ask, and what are you looking for?"

HIRAID — History Phase

Presenting Complaint & HPC (History of Presenting Complaint)

  • "Can you tell me when the chest pain started?" → 25 minutes ago, at home
  • "What were you doing when it started?" → Sitting watching TV, sudden onset
  • "How would you describe the pain?" → Crushing, central, 8/10
  • "Does it move anywhere?" → Yes, radiates to left arm, jaw, and between shoulder blades
  • "Have you had anything like this before?" → No, first episode
  • "What have you taken for it?" → Wife gave aspirin and GTN spray (paramedics), mild relief only (pain now 7/10)
  • "Any shortness of breath, nausea, or dizziness?" → Yes, feels nauseous, not actually vomited, no shortness of breath, not dizzy

Past Medical History

  • "Do you have high blood pressure?" → Yes, on medication for 10 years
  • "Any heart problems?" → No
  • "Diabetes?" → No
  • "Kidney disease?" → No
  • "Lung disease?" → No
  • "Recent surgery or illness?" → No

Medications & Allergies

  • Lisinopril 10 mg daily (ACE inhibitor for hypertension)
  • Atorvastatin 40 mg daily (statin for cholesterol)
  • Allergies: None known (NKDA)

Social History (Relevant to Rural Context)

  • "Do you smoke?" → Former smoker, quit 10 years ago
  • "Alcohol use?" → Social drinker, a few beers on weekends
  • "Work stress?" → Wife mentions he's been stressed lately, working long hours
  • "Support at home?" → Wife present and supportive; lives locally
  • "Any recent travel or immobility?" → No

HIRAID — Red Flag Screening

Red flags specific to ACS in rural ED context:

Red Flag Question Finding Significance
Age >55 and male? Yes — 65M Major risk factor
Crushing, pressure-like, or central chest pain? Yes — crushing, central Classic ACS presentation
Radiation to arm, neck, jaw, or back? Yes — left arm, jaw, between shoulder blades Classic ACS radiation pattern
Associated dyspnoea, diaphoresis, nausea, or syncope? Yes — diaphoresis and nausea present Indicates severe event, worse prognosis
Hypertension and tachycardia on examination? Yes — BP 168/98, HR 100 Sympathetic activation, possible cardiogenic shock developing
Poor response to GTN? Minimal relief after GTN spray Suggests ACS rather than stable angina
Previous cardiac history or strong family history? No direct history but on statin (suggests cardiovascular risk profile) Supports ACS likelihood

Clinical Synthesis

Red flag count: 7/7 positive indicators for acute coronary syndrome. This patient is at imminent risk of deterioration, including cardiac arrhythmia and arrest.

Critical Escalation Trigger

At this point in the history, RN1 should be thinking: "This is an acute MI until proven otherwise. I need my doctor here in minutes, not hours. If my doctor is more than 5 minutes away or unavailable, I am calling MedSTAR now for retrieval coordination."

Key Teaching Point

Red flag screening is not a checkbox exercise. Each red flag raises the probability of acute pathology. When you accumulate 5+ red flags in a single patient, escalation is not optional — it's mandatory. Rural clinicians must develop an intuition for this: "This patient is going bad. I'm getting help."

Real-Job Translation

At 2 AM, you're the only experienced nurse on shift. You take this history, count the red flags, and realise this man is potentially 5 minutes away from a life-threatening arrhythmia. You call the doctor and say, "I need you here now." You also inform the front desk to be ready to call MedSTAR if the doctor advises retrieval.

Diagnostic Investigations

12-Lead ECG (Immediate)

RN1 or RN2 obtains ECG while doctor is in transit.

Scenario ECG findings (describe to group):

  • Sinus rhythm, rate 100 bpm
  • ST elevation in leads II, III, aVF (inferior ST-elevation MI — STEMI)
  • Reciprocal ST depression in leads I and aVL
  • T-wave inversion in aVF
  • Interpretation: Acute inferior STEMI — time-critical emergency
⏱ Time-Critical Action

ECG interpretation confirms STEMI. This patient requires urgent reperfusion therapy (primary PCI at a tertiary cardiac centre) or consideration of thrombolytic therapy. In this rural setting, retrieval to a cardiac catheterisation laboratory is the definitive pathway. Call the doctor and MedSTAR immediately.

Point-of-Care Testing (available in this rural ED)

  • Blood glucose: 6.2 mmol/L (normal)
  • Urinalysis: Not critical in acute setting but available if needed
  • Troponin assay: NOT available in this rural ED after hours — patient will require transfer for serial troponins and risk stratification

Bloods for Sending

If pathology available after hours (often limited in rural settings), draw:

  • FBC (full blood count)
  • U&E (urea, electrolytes, creatinine)
  • LFTs (liver function tests)
  • Blood glucose (already done POC, but can confirm on lab)
  • Troponin (if assay available; if not, will be done at receiving facility)

Note: In many rural EDs after hours, pathology may not be immediately available. The clinical decision is made on history, examination, and ECG findings, not on troponin results.

Clinical Decision Point & Escalation

4 BREAK POINT: Clinical Decision Point — Working Diagnosis & Escalation
Decision-Making + Communication

Scenario Timing

Time: 22:28 (10:28 PM) — Approximately 13 minutes after patient arrival. Doctor has just walked through the ED door, summoned urgently by RN1.

Doctor's Summary from RN1

RN1 to Doctor (ISBAR format):

I: "This is David Thompson, 65-year-old male, wife present."

S: "Acute onset central crushing chest pain 35 minutes ago, radiating to left arm and jaw. Diaphoretic, nauseous. Pain 7/10 despite aspirin and GTN. Not responding to analgesia."

B: "History of hypertension on lisinopril, on statin. Former smoker. No previous cardiac history."

A: "Vitals: HR 100, BP 168/98, RR 22, SpO2 98% on 2L. Pale, diaphoretic. ECG shows ST elevation in inferior leads — looks like acute inferior STEMI."

R: "I think this patient needs urgent cardiac assessment. We need to decide about retrieval."

Doctor's Assessment & Working Diagnosis

Doctor reviews ECG, confirms:

  • ST-elevation MI (STEMI) — inferior territory
  • This is a time-critical emergency requiring reperfusion therapy
  • This rural ED does not have cardiac catheterisation facilities or thrombolytic pathway
  • Patient requires urgent transfer to tertiary cardiac centre
  • Nearest facility with primary PCI capability: 65 km away, approximately 1 hour by road

Facilitator Prompts for Doctor

  • "What is your working diagnosis?"
  • "What are the treatment options available in this rural setting?"
  • "Is this patient suitable for local management, or does he need retrieval?"
  • "When should you call MedSTAR, and what information will you give them?"
  • "What are you concerned might happen in the next 30 minutes?"

Expected Clinical Reasoning

  • Acute inferior STEMI — time-critical diagnosis requiring intervention within minutes, not hours
  • Rural ED has no PCI capability and thrombolytic therapy is not first-line per current guidelines
  • Patient is haemodynamically relatively stable now but at high risk of arrhythmia (inferior MI predisposes to bradycardia, heart block, and VF)
  • MedSTAR retrieval is the appropriate pathway
  • Immediate goals: stabilise, monitor closely, prepare for deterioration, initiate retrieval, and provide ALS if arrest occurs

Escalation Decision

Decision: MedSTAR Retrieval — Urgent.

This patient requires urgent transfer to a tertiary cardiac centre for primary PCI or advanced cardiac support. Retrieval is not optional. Call MedSTAR immediately.

Real-Job Translation

In a real rural ED, this conversation between nurse and doctor takes about 2 minutes. The doctor reviews the ECG, says, "Yes, this is a STEMI. We're calling the helicopter or retrieval team right now," and the nurse begins preparing the patient for transfer while the doctor makes the phone call. There is no debate — the pathway is clear.

Key Teaching Point

STEMI is a code word in rural emergency medicine. It means: drop everything, call for help, and prepare the patient for rapid retrieval. Every minute of delay increases the size of the MI and mortality risk. Rural doctors must have a low threshold for recognising STEMI and a high threshold for contacting retrieval services.

Phone Call to MedSTAR Coordination Centre

I need to provide you with clinical details so we can arrange transport to [Regional Cardiac Centre]."

MedSTAR: "Roger, go ahead with patient details. We have one retrieval team available and we can be airborne in approximately 20 minutes if weather permits, or road transport in 45 minutes. Ground transport is on standby. What's your patient info?"

Doctor (uses ISBAR):

I: "Patient is David John Thompson, DOB 15/03/1960, 65-year-old male."

S: "Acute inferior STEMI. Chest pain onset 35 minutes ago. Presented with crushing central chest pain, radiating to left arm and jaw. Diaphoretic and nauseous."

B: "PMH: Hypertension on lisinopril. Takes atorvastatin. Former smoker. No known cardiac history. Allergies: none known."

A: "Current vitals: HR 100, BP 168/98, RR 22, SpO2 98% on 2L nasal prongs. Alert and communicating. 12-lead ECG confirms ST elevation in inferior leads II, III, aVF with reciprocal changes. Patient given aspirin 300 mg and GTN spray. Two IV cannulae in situ. Pain currently 7/10."

Facilitator Note

In this scenario, retrieval is available within 20–45 minutes. In some real rural areas, retrieval may take 2–4 hours depending on weather and availability. The principles remain the same: early recognition, stabilisation, and coordination.

Treatment & Stabilisation Phase

5 BREAK POINT: Treatment Initiation & Medication Safety
Practical Skill + Medication Administration

Facilitator Prompt

"Doctor, you've called MedSTAR. Now you're preparing this patient for transport and managing acute pain and risk of deterioration. Walk me through your immediate treatment plan and medication administration. RN2, you're ready to help administer drugs. What's the priority order?"

Treatment Priorities (Post-MedSTAR Call)

1. Analgesia (First Priority — pain management is critical in ACS)

Medication: Morphine IV

  • Indication: Severe chest pain (7/10) not responding to aspirin and GTN; morphine is the opioid of choice in acute MI
  • Dose: 3–5 mg IV bolus, titrated every 5–10 minutes to effect (maximum 20 mg)
  • Route: IV (fast onset)
  • Monitoring: After administration, monitor for respiratory depression, hypotension, bradycardia
  • Safety check: Right patient (ID band), right drug (morphine), right dose (3 mg IV), right route (IV), right time (now)
  • RN2 action: Draw morphine 3 mg from vial, verify with doctor, confirm patient ID, administer IV slowly over 1–2 minutes, document time and dose
⏱ Expected Outcome Post-Morphine

Within 5 minutes, patient's pain should reduce to 4–5/10. Patient becomes calmer, respiratory rate stabilises, and blood pressure remains stable or increases slightly due to pain relief. If pain persists, second dose 2–3 mg given after reassessment.

2. Continuation of Anti-thrombotic Therapy

Aspirin (Already given by paramedics — dose 300 mg) — No further dose needed acutely

  • Paramedics administered 300 mg chewable aspirin — this is the loading dose for ACS
  • Maintenance dose (75 mg daily) will be prescribed at receiving facility or as part of discharge if patient remains locally
  • Document in ED record that aspirin already given

Additional Antiplatelet Consideration (P2Y12 Inhibitor — e.g., Clopidogrel/Ticagrelor)

  • In a rural ED, dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor) is often initiated by the receiving cardiac centre or via telephone consultation
  • Some rural protocols may include clopidogrel 600 mg loading dose if advised by regional cardiology
  • Scenario assumption: Doctor contacts the regional cardiac centre or follows SA Health clinical directive for dual antiplatelet therapy
  • For this scenario, assume doctor decides to hold P2Y12 inhibitor pending receiving team advice (conservative but appropriate in rural context)

3. Nitrates (Already given by paramedics — GTN spray)

  • Paramedics administered GTN spray (dose typically 0.4–0.6 mg sublingual) — minimal effect
  • Do NOT repeat GTN given hypotension risk and now receiving morphine
  • After pain relief with morphine, if blood pressure remains adequate (SBP >90 mmHg), GTN spray can be repeated if needed, but morphine is preferred in acute MI

4. Anticoagulation

Unfractionated Heparin (UFH) or Low-Molecular-Weight Heparin (LMWH)

  • Indication: STEMI requires anticoagulation to prevent thrombus propagation
  • Dose (UFH): 60–100 units/kg IV bolus (typically 5,000–10,000 units), then infusion
  • Scenario decision: Doctor may initiate UFH IV bolus if local protocols allow, or may defer to receiving facility (time-sensitive but not absolutely critical if transfer is within 1 hour)
  • RN2 action: If directed, draw heparin 5,000 units, verify with doctor, administer IV bolus
  • Alternative: Some protocols use LMWH (e.g., enoxaparin 30 mg IV + 1 mg/kg SC) — check local directive

5. Oxygen Therapy (Reassess)

  • Current SpO2 98% on 2L nasal prongs is adequate
  • Target: SpO2 94–98% (avoid hypoxia and hyperoxia)
  • No need to increase oxygen unless SpO2 drops below 94%
  • Continue current oxygen prescription

6. Monitoring Setup

  • Continuous 3-lead cardiac monitoring (or 12-lead if continuous monitor available)
  • SpO2 probe on finger
  • NIBP measurement every 15 minutes (or continuous if available)
  • Temperature monitoring (already done — 36.8°C)
  • IV access secured (two cannulae already in place) — check patency
  • Do NOT remove monitoring or disconnect IV until retrieval team takes over

Real-Job Translation

This is medication time in a rural ED. You have 2 RNs, 1 doctor, and a critically ill patient. One nurse is drawing up morphine while the other checks the IV site. The doctor is either administering or observing. Someone is documenting. Everyone is verbalising what they're doing: "3 mg morphine, IV push, David Thompson." This is not a casual moment — it's deliberate, checked, and purposeful.

Key Teaching Point

In acute cardiac care, medication errors are lethal. The five rights (patient, drug, dose, route, time) are non-negotiable. In a rural ED where there's no pharmacist on-site and no IV verification system, the nurse and doctor must be the safety net. Use checklists, verbalise each step, and never rush.

Post-Treatment Observations (Next 5 Minutes)

Expected Clinical Response

  • Pain: Reduces from 7/10 to 3–4/10 after morphine (takes 5 minutes)
  • HR: May decrease slightly to 95 bpm (less pain, less sympathetic drive)
  • BP: Remains 160–165/95 (morphine may cause mild hypotension, but usually well-tolerated)
  • RR: May decrease slightly to 18–20 (pain relief reduces compensatory tachypnoea)
  • SpO2: Remains 98%
  • Mood: Patient more settled, less anxious; wife reassured by visible pain relief
  • Skin: Still pale but less diaphoretic as pain decreases
  • Mental status: Alert but drowsy (morphine side effect) — appropriate
⏱ Time: 22:33 (10:33 PM) — 18 minutes post-presentation

Patient is now stabilised with pain controlled. Retrieval team is en route. Current status: relative stability with underlying acute MI.

However, this is the calm before the storm. Inferior MI predisposes to sudden rhythm changes, bradycardia, and conduction disturbances.

Team must remain vigilant. Defibrillator pads should be on the patient now, not in the cupboard.

Cardiac Arrest & ANZCOR ALS Implementation

6 BREAK POINT: Cardiac Arrest Scenario
High-Fidelity Resuscitation Scenario

Scenario Trigger

Time: 22:37 (10:37 PM) — 22 minutes post-presentation

Patient has been relatively stable for the past 4 minutes. He's now drowsy from morphine. Monitoring shows sinus rhythm, HR 95. RN2 is rechecking vitals.

Suddenly: Cardiac monitor alarm sounds. RN2 looks at screen and calls out, "He's lost his pulse! Monitor shows VF!"

COLLAPSE ANNOUNCEMENT

RN2: "Patient is unresponsive and pulseless. Monitor shows ventricular fibrillation. Starting CPR now!"

Facilitator Role During Arrest

Facilitator steps into active coaching role. As the arrest unfolds, facilitator calls out key milestones and checks team performance against ANZCOR protocol. Facilitator may also play the role of defibrillator voice prompts or time keeper.

ANZCOR ALS Protocol — Step by Step

Immediate Actions (First 10 Seconds)

  • RN1: Verify unresponsiveness — shake patient's shoulders, call out "David! Are you okay?"
  • RN1: Check for carotid pulse (no longer than 10 seconds) — "I cannot feel a pulse"
  • RN1: Activate Emergency Response — call out "CODE BLUE — Cardiac arrest!" or activate emergency call system (pre-arranged signal in rural ED)
  • RN2: Ensure defibrillator is immediately available — if not already on patient, apply pads NOW (or paddles if manual defibrillator)
  • Doctor: Arrives at bedside (or is already there if timing allows)

CPR Initiation (RN1 begins immediately)

  • Position: Patient supine on firm surface (ED trolley with CPR board underneath)
  • Hand Position: Heel of hand on lower half of sternum, second hand on top, arms straight, shoulders directly above hands
  • Compression Technique: Push hard and fast — 30 compressions at rate 100–120 bpm (approximately 2 compressions per second)
  • Compression Depth: At least 5 cm (depress sternum by 5–6 cm in adult)
  • Recoil: Allow full recoil of chest between compressions — do not lean on chest
  • Minimise Interruptions: Compressions should only stop for: rhythm check (2–3 seconds), defibrillation (if indicated), and planned handover
⏱ COMPRESSIONS START IMMEDIATELY

No delay. No "wait for ECG." No "let me check rhythm first." The moment you confirm pulselessness and unresponsiveness, BEGIN COMPRESSIONS. In a rural ED with limited staff, every second of delay is a second of no blood flow to the brain.

Ventilation Strategy

  • Initial Approach: High-quality compressions are the priority. If BVM is available and RN2 is trained, provide ventilations at a ratio of 30 compressions : 2 breaths
  • BVM Delivery: RN2 applies BVM to patient's face, ensuring seal, and delivers 2 breaths (each breath takes 1 second, chest should rise)
  • Alternative: If BVM coordination is not smooth, focus on compressions alone (compression-only CPR is effective in witnessed VF arrest)
  • Target: 30 compressions followed by 2 breaths; repeat cycle continuously

Defibrillator Application (RN2 — Concurrent with Compressions)

  • Pads Placement (if not already on patient):
    • Right pad: Right of sternum, below clavicle, lateral to nipple
    • Left pad: Left anterior axillary line, level of 5th intercostal space (nipple level)
  • Patient Preparation: Ensure chest is dry; if very hairy, may need to clip (do not shave — wastes time)
  • Pad Adherence: Press firmly to ensure good contact
  • Defibrillator Mode: Ensure manual defibrillator is set to "Analyse" or allow semi-automatic defibrillator to analyse rhythm

First Rhythm Check (Approximately 2 Minutes into CPR)

After 30 compressions + 2 breaths × 10 cycles (approximately 2 minutes), PAUSE for rhythm check.

  • RN1: Stops compressions
  • RN2/Doctor: Briefly checks monitor or defibrillator screen
  • Finding: Ventricular Fibrillation (VF) confirmed
  • Action: SHOCK

First Defibrillation

  • Defibrillator Charge: Select 200 J (monophasic) or 120–200 J (biphasic) — or follow device guidance
  • Safety Call: Doctor or RN2 calls out: "Shocking. Everyone clear!" or similar
  • Visual Check: Ensure no one is touching patient or bed
  • Deliver Shock: Press button(s) on defibrillator — patient will jerk
  • Immediately Resume Compressions: RN1 resumes chest compressions without waiting for monitor reassessment
  • No Pulse Check — Just Resume CPR

Drug Administration — First Drug Cycle (Approximately 4 Minutes into CPR)

After 2 minutes of CPR + 1 shock, assess rhythm again.

  • Rhythm Check: Still VF or now asystole/PEA
  • If VF persists: Deliver second shock (200–360 J depending on device)
  • After Second Shock (or if PEA/Asystole): Establish IV access (if not already secure) or IO (intraosseous) access
  • First Drug: Adrenaline 1 mg IV/IO

Adrenaline Administration — First Dose

  • Drug: Adrenaline (Epinephrine) 1:1000 solution
  • Dose: 1 mg (which is 1 mL of 1:1000 solution)
  • Route: IV or IO (intravenous or intraosseous)
  • Timing: After second shock or after 4 minutes of CPR if first shock did not restore circulation
  • Preparation: RN2 draws 1 mL adrenaline from 10 mL ampoule into 10 mL syringe, then flushes line with 20 mL 0.9% saline to ensure drug reaches systemic circulation
  • Administration: Doctor or senior RN injects adrenaline IV bolus rapidly, immediately followed by saline flush
  • Purpose: Increases coronary and cerebral perfusion pressure, improves likelihood of ROSC (return of spontaneous circulation)
  • Documentation: Time of administration, dose, route, and person administering recorded

After Adrenaline: Immediately resume compressions for another 2 minutes without further rhythm check.

Continued Resuscitation Cycles

Pattern every 2 minutes:

  • 2 minutes CPR (30:2 compression to ventilation ratio)
  • Brief pause for rhythm check (<10 seconds)
  • If VF: Deliver shock
  • Resume compressions immediately
  • Every 4 minutes (every 2nd rhythm check): Give adrenaline 1 mg IV/IO (i.e., at 4 min, 8 min, 12 min, etc.)

Amiodarone Administration (If VF/Pulseless VT Persists)

Amiodarone — Second Drug Cycle

  • Indication: VF or pulseless ventricular tachycardia (VT) that does not respond to initial shock and adrenaline
  • Timing: After third shock (i.e., at approximately 6–8 minutes into resuscitation) if VF/VT persists despite adrenaline
  • Drug: Amiodarone 300 mg IV bolus
  • Dose: 300 mg (typically supplied as 3 mL of 100 mg/mL solution, or pre-drawn in some EDs)
  • Route: IV or IO
  • Administration: RN2 draws amiodarone (or uses pre-prepared syringe), doctor or senior RN injects IV rapidly, followed by saline flush
  • Purpose: Antiarrhythmic agent that may improve likelihood of defibrillation success in refractory VF
  • Note: Amiodarone is given ONCE in a resuscitation — not repeated every cycle like adrenaline

After Amiodarone: Resume compressions. Continue 2-minute cycles, continuing adrenaline every 4 minutes (at 8 min, 12 min, etc. from start).

Compression Rotation (Critical in Rural ED)

Scenario: Team is RN1, RN2, and Doctor. All three must share compression load to prevent fatigue.

  • First 2 minutes: RN1 performs compressions
  • At 2-minute mark (after first rhythm check): RN1 and RN2 swap — RN2 takes over compressions, RN1 moves to ventilation/monitoring
  • At 4-minute mark: Doctor takes over compressions (or RN2 continues if doctor is needed for other tasks)
  • At 6-minute mark: RN1 returns to compressions (rotated out to recover)
  • Continue rotation every 2 minutes to prevent fatigue-induced poor quality compressions
⏱ Fatigue = Death

If one person does compressions for more than 2 minutes, chest compression depth and rate decline significantly. In a rural ED with only 2–3 people, YOU MUST rotate every 2 minutes. No excuses. A tired nurse doing shallow compressions is worse than no compressions at all.

Real-Job Translation

In a real rural ED arrest at 2 AM, this is the reality: One nurse is pushing on a man's chest while another is trying to get a large IV in. The doctor is calling MedSTAR and managing the airway. Someone's watching the clock and calling out "2 minutes, prepare to swap." Someone else is drawing up adrenaline, reading the vial label twice, and asking the doctor "Is this right?" There's no calm, no silence. There's focused chaos with clear roles.

Scenario Milestone

Time: 22:47 (10:47 PM) — 10 minutes into arrest

Patient remains in VF despite three shocks and adrenaline × 2. Amiodarone has been given. Compressions are ongoing. Retrieval team is now only 10–15 minutes away.

Team is working hard but patient shows no signs of ROSC.

Scenario Variation: Return of Spontaneous Circulation (ROSC)

Possible Endpoint 1: ROSC Achieved

Alternative scenario (if time permits or if facilitator directs):

At the 10-minute mark, after the 5th shock and 3 doses of adrenaline, the monitor shows a narrow complex rhythm with a pulse check detecting a carotid pulse. Patient has return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC).

  • RN1: "I've got a pulse! Carotid pulse present!"
  • Stop CPR. Check blood pressure: 92/58 (hypotensive)
  • Patient is unconscious, not breathing spontaneously. Airway at risk.
  • Doctor: "Prepare for intubation or airway management. Get suction ready."
  • Transition to Post-Resuscitation Care (see Section 11 below)

Scenario Variation: Refractory Arrest

Possible Endpoint 2: Prolonged Resuscitation without ROSC

Scenario decision point (at 15–20 minutes of CPR):

Patient remains in asystole or PEA despite continuous CPR, adrenaline, amiodarone, and advanced interventions. Retrieval team is still 5 minutes away.

  • Doctor Decision: "We're now 15 minutes into arrest with no signs of ROSC. Patient is cold, asystole is persistent. Let's contact MedSTAR to discuss ongoing resuscitation or consideration of termination of resuscitation."
  • MedSTAR Consultation: Likely to advise continuing CPR until retrieval team arrival (to allow for extracorporeal resuscitation options if available at receiving facility) or discuss termination of resuscitation criteria
  • In most rural ED scenarios: CPR is continued until retrieval team takes over or termination of resuscitation criteria are met (e.g., >30 minutes of CPR with no response, pre-existing fatal injury, etc.)

Reassessment, Sustained Resuscitation & Retrieval Coordination

7 BREAK POINT: Sustained Resuscitation & Escalation
Decision-Making + Team Communication

Scenario Context (Continuing Arrest)

Time: 22:47 (10:47 PM) — 10 minutes into cardiac arrest

Patient remains in VF/asystole. Despite three shocks, three doses of adrenaline, and amiodarone, no ROSC. Compressions are ongoing. Retrieval team is now 10–15 minutes away.

Facilitator Prompts

  • "Team, you're 10 minutes into resuscitation. What's your plan? Do you continue, escalate, or consider termination?"
  • "Doctor, have you spoken to MedSTAR recently? Do they have an ETA?"
  • "How is team fatigue? Who needs a break?"
  • "What if the retrieval team is still 20 minutes away when you've done 20 minutes of CPR?"

Expected Clinical Reasoning

  • In a refractory arrest (no ROSC after 10 minutes), continued high-quality CPR is still indicated until retrieval team arrival or termination criteria met
  • Most rural protocols continue resuscitation for up to 20 minutes in a witnessed arrest with ongoing efforts
  • Termination of resuscitation decisions are typically made after discussion with senior doctor and retrieval team
  • Fatigue management is critical — team must continue rotation every 2 minutes
  • Ongoing adrenaline given every 4 minutes

Key Teaching Point

In rural resuscitations, the decision to continue or stop is not binary. It's a continuous reassessment: Is the patient responding? Is there a reversible cause? How much longer can we sustain this? When does it become futile? These are the conversations the doctor has with the team and with retrieval services.

Real-Job Translation

At 11 PM on a Saturday, you're doing compressions on a man you know from the community. His wife is in the waiting room. You've been pushing on his chest for 10 minutes. You're tired. The next compression provider is also tired. The doctor is looking at the monitor with a grim expression. The phone rings — it's MedSTAR with an ETA. You keep going because there's hope, because the patient deserves it, because that's what you do. This is the emotional reality of rural emergency medicine.

Ongoing Resuscitation — Cycles 6–10

Standard Cycle Continuation (Minutes 10–20 of Arrest)

  • Every 2 minutes: Brief pause, rhythm check (<10 seconds)
  • Rhythm options:
    • VF → Shock delivered, resume CPR
    • Pulseless VT → Shock delivered, resume CPR
    • Asystole → No shock, resume CPR
    • PEA (Pulseless Electrical Activity) → No shock, resume CPR + treat underlying cause
  • Every 4 minutes (at 8 min, 12 min, 16 min marks): Adrenaline 1 mg IV/IO given after rhythm check
  • Compression team rotates every 2 minutes
  • Airway management: Continue BVM or consider placement of laryngeal mask or endotracheal tube if skilled provider available (in this rural scenario, BVM is likely ongoing)

Reassessment for Reversible Causes (4Hs & 4Ts)

During ongoing resuscitation, the team should consider reversible causes of cardiac arrest:

4Hs (Hypoxia, Hypovolaemia, Hyperkalaemia/Hypokalaemia, Hypothermia)
Hypoxia: Patient on oxygen; BVM delivering ventilations — unlikely cause
Hypovolaemia: No evidence of bleeding; patient has acute MI — not hypovolaemic
Hyperkalaemia/Hypokalaemia: Acute MI; electrolyte derangement possible but not immediately reversible in rural ED
Hypothermia: Patient normothermic (36.8°C) — not a factor
4Ts (Thromboembolism, Tension Pneumothorax, Tamponade, Toxins)
Thromboembolism (Pulmonary): Inferior MI with VF is consistent with acute coronary thrombosis — primary cause, not reversible in ED
Tension Pneumothorax: No signs (JVD, tracheal deviation, unilateral absent breath sounds) — unlikely
Tamponade: Possible complication of MI but not acutely reversible in rural ED
Toxins: No history of drug ingestion; patient collapsed with ACS prodrome — not applicable

Conclusion: No immediately reversible causes identified. Continue resuscitation per protocol.

MedSTAR Communication — Update

At approximately 12–15 minutes of resuscitation, doctor should have contact with MedSTAR for update and discussion:

Doctor's Second Call to MedSTAR

Doctor (to MedSTAR): "This is Dr. [Name] from [Hospital]. I'm calling with an update on David Thompson. He collapsed into cardiac arrest approximately 12 minutes ago. He was in VF, we've shocked him three times, given three doses of adrenaline and amiodarone. We're currently getting ongoing CPR with no return of spontaneous circulation. He remains in asystole. We're continuing resuscitation. What's your ETA?"

MedSTAR: "Roger. We're now 8 minutes out. We have a retrieval team standing by. Continue your resuscitation efforts. We're briefing the receiving cardiac centre now. What's his current status?"

Doctor: "Asystole on monitor. Pupils dilated. No signs of life. We'll continue CPR and have him packaged and ready for handover when you arrive."

MedSTAR: "Understood. We'll be with you shortly. Out."

Preparation for Retrieval & Transport

7b BREAK POINT: Transfer Preparation & Packaging
Practical Skill + Continuity Planning

Scenario Timing

Time: 22:55 (10:55 PM) — 18 minutes into arrest. MedSTAR is now 2 minutes away.

Facilitator Prompt

"Team, retrieval is almost here. What needs to be done NOW to prepare the patient for handover? What documentation, what equipment, what clinical information?"

Packaging Checklist — Patient Preparation

  • Airway: Ensure airway management is optimized — BVM in use or advanced airway in place. Suction available and functional.
  • Breathing: Oxygen tubing secured, spare oxygen cylinders (portable) available for transport
  • Circulation: Defibrillator pads on and connected to portable monitor. Two IV lines secured, locks functional. Fluids ready if needed.
  • Disability: Patient remains unconscious — head midline, neck protected from further injury
  • Exposure: Minimal exposure; patient covered for warmth and dignity (important for family)
  • Monitoring Cable: Ensure all monitoring cables are not tangled; defibrillator can be transferred to retrieval team's equipment if needed
  • Medications: Have remaining adrenaline ampoules and emergency drugs in pocket or easily accessible for retrieval team

Documentation Preparation

  • ED Record: Complete resuscitation note with timeline of events, interventions, drugs given, times, and doses
  • Arrest Timeline: Clearly documented:
    • Time of arrest (22:37)
    • Time CPR started (22:37)
    • Time of first shock (22:39)
    • Times of adrenaline doses (22:41, 22:45, 22:49)
    • Time of amiodarone (22:43)
    • Total duration of resuscitation (18 minutes at handover)
    • Rhythm changes noted (VF → Asystole at 22:50 approx.)
  • Current Vitals: Last recorded observations (asystole, no palpable pulse, dilated pupils)
  • Drug Allergies & PMH: Clearly visible on patient ID or documentation
  • Contact Information: Wife's details for family communication post-handover
  • 12-Lead ECG Tracing: If available, copy of initial ECG showing STEMI for retrieval team

Family Communication

Important Rural Context

David's wife is in the waiting room. She's been waiting for 18 minutes during the resuscitation. Someone from the nursing team should have been updating her at regular intervals — at 5 minutes ("We're working on him, the doctor is here"), at 10 minutes ("We're continuing resuscitation"), and now at 18 minutes before handover. This is a critical component of rural ED care.

  • Before Handover: A nurse (ideally RN1 who has rapport) should speak briefly with wife: "The retrieval team is arriving in the next minute. They're taking David to the regional cardiac centre for more advanced care. We'll arrange for you to go with him or meet him there. We'll keep you informed every step."
  • Emotional Support: Wife may be distressed. Offer presence, clear communication, and facilitate any last words or prayers if appropriate
  • Practical Support: Offer phone to contact family members, arrange transport, provide comfort

Real-Job Translation

This is the moment where rural emergency nursing is as much about the family as about the patient. While the doctor and RNs are preparing the patient for transport, another team member has informed the wife what's happening, answered her questions, and made her feel heard. In a small town, you'll see that wife again. She'll remember how she was treated during the worst moment of her life.

Contingency: Delayed Retrieval

Scenario Variation: Retrieval Delayed 30+ Minutes

What if MedSTAR is delayed by weather, mechanical issues, or competing calls?

  • CPR continuation: Rural protocols typically continue resuscitation for 20–30 minutes with ongoing efforts
  • Role rotation continues: If retrieval is delayed to 30+ minutes, team fatigue is critical issue
  • Repeated adrenaline: Continue 1 mg IV/IO every 4 minutes as long as resuscitation continues
  • Reassessment of futility: After 20–30 minutes with no ROSC and persistent asystole, discussion with senior doctor about termination of resuscitation
  • Communication with family: Keep wife informed of delay and current status
  • Psychological support for team: Rural teams doing CPR for 30+ minutes on a community member experience significant emotional toll

ISBAR Handover to Retrieval Team

8 BREAK POINT: ISBAR Handover Practice
Communication Skill

Scenario Trigger

Time: 22:57 (10:57 PM) — MedSTAR retrieval team arrives. Helicopter has landed nearby or retrieval vehicles at ED entrance.

Paramedic-led retrieval team (or flight paramedic if helicopter) enters ED. They have portable monitor, equipment, and advanced airway capability.

Facilitator Prompt

"Doctor, you're now handing over this patient to the retrieval team. Use ISBAR format. What do you tell them? Be comprehensive — they need the full picture to take over safely."

ISBAR Handover — Doctor to Retrieval Team Lead

Full Handover

I — Identify

"This is David John Thompson, date of birth 15th of March 1960, 65 years old. He's a local resident. His wife is present and will travel with the retrieval team if possible."

S — Situation

"David presented to our ED at approximately 22:15 with acute onset central crushing chest pain. Initial assessment and ECG confirmed acute inferior STEMI. We initiated MedSTAR retrieval immediately. At 22:37, approximately 20 minutes after presentation and after receiving aspirin, GTN, and morphine, he collapsed into cardiac arrest. He's been in cardiac arrest for approximately 20 minutes now."

B — Background

"Past medical history: hypertension on lisinopril 10 mg daily, on atorvastatin 40 mg daily for cholesterol. Former smoker, quit 10 years ago. No previous cardiac history. Wife reports recent work stress. Allergies: none known. He was alert and communicating until arrest. No prior neurological issues."

A — Assessment

"Initial 12-lead ECG showed ST elevation in inferior leads II, III, aVF with reciprocal changes in I and aVL, consistent with acute inferior STEMI. Troponin was not available at our facility due to after-hours limitations, but clinical presentation and ECG are diagnostic. He's currently in asystole on the monitor. We've administered three doses of adrenaline at 1 mg IV, amiodarone 300 mg IV, and delivered three DC shocks. No return of spontaneous circulation. He's receiving ongoing CPR at a quality depth and rate of 100–120 bpm. Airway is being managed with bag-valve-mask ventilation. Two large-bore IV cannulae are in place and secure. He's normothermic at 36.8°C."

R — Recommendation

"I recommend you continue resuscitation during transport with ongoing CPR, adrenaline every 4 minutes, and further shocks if any shockable rhythm develops. He requires urgent transfer to [Regional Cardiac Centre] for advanced cardiac care, potential extracorporeal resuscitation if available, and definitive cardiac intervention. We're happy to continue any aspect of care or provide further information as needed. Family support will be important during transport."

Handover Additions — RN Contribution

RN1 or RN2 may also provide supplementary information:

  • "IV access is secure — two 18-gauge cannulae, both patent and locked. We've been flushing after each drug."
  • "He responded minimally to GTN but pain reduced to 3/10 after morphine 3 mg IV before arrest."
  • "Pupils are now dilated — 20 minutes of asystole means significant hypoxic-ischaemic injury is likely, but we're continuing as you've arrived."
  • "Wife is understandably distressed but aware of what's happening. She's consented to travel with you."

Retrieval Team Handover

Retrieval Team Lead may respond:

"Thank you for the comprehensive handover. We'll take over compressions now. Can you brief us on where you're putting the patient for transport? We'll continue ANZCOR protocol with ongoing CPR, adrenaline every 4 minutes, and any shocks if rhythm changes. We'll initiate our transport and transfer to the cardiac centre. We'll contact them en route. Thanks for your excellent pre-hospital care and stabilisation."

Key Teaching Points

  • ISBAR is a standard, clinically effective handover structure used across Australia
  • Comprehensive handover reduces errors and ensures continuity of care
  • In rural ED, the handover is often the final clinical interaction — make it count
  • Tone and clarity matter: be professional, calm, and evidence-based

Real-Job Translation

A retrieval team doesn't want to hear a rambling story. They want facts: patient identification, what happened, what you did, what didn't work, and what they should know. A good handover takes 2–3 minutes and hits all the key points. A poor handover leaves them guessing and can lead to missed critical information. This is how information cascades from rural to tertiary care.

Post-Handover Team Debrief (Brief)

After retrieval team departs, RN1, RN2, and Doctor remain in ED. Family has travelled with retrieval team.

  • Vital Sign Check: Ensure both RNs are physically okay — offer water, rest, brief decompression time
  • Quick Huddle: Doctor: "That was a good effort. We recognised STEMI early, called for help on time, and provided high-quality resuscitation. I know it's tough when a patient arrests, especially someone from the community. How are you both doing?"
  • Practical Tasks: RN2 begins cleaning and restocking the ED — replacing IV packs, refilling medication trays, restocking the resuscitation trolley
  • Brief Note: RN1 ensures ED record is complete and transmitted to receiving hospital

Debrief (12–15 minutes)

9 BREAK POINT: Structured Debrief
Reflection & Learning

Facilitator Sets the Tone

Facilitator (after scenario ends):

"Team, let's pause the scenario and debrief. This was a high-acuity, emotionally charged situation. I want to acknowledge the work you've done here today — recognising STEMI, calling for help, running a resuscitation, and handing over to retrieval. Let's now reflect on what went well, what could be different, and what we've learned."

Debrief Structure — Advocacy-Inquiry Model

Round 1: What Went Well? (Positive Feedback)

Facilitator prompt: "Let's start with something positive. What did you see the team do really well? RN1, you want to start?"

  • RN1 response (example): "I think we recognised the red flags early. The combination of chest pain and diaphoresis and that ECG pattern made it clear this was a STEMI. We didn't waste time."
  • RN2 response (example): "I thought the compression rotations were smooth. We swapped at the right times, and no one person got too fatigued."
  • Doctor response (example): "The team communication was excellent. When things got chaotic, people were clear about who was doing what. The handover to MedSTAR was thorough and professional."

Round 2: What Could Be Different? (Constructive Critique)

Facilitator prompt: "Now, let's think about opportunities for improvement. What would you do differently if you ran this again? Not wrong — just different. RN2, thoughts?"

  • RN2 response (example): "I think we could have placed defibrillator pads earlier. We waited until after the initial assessment. In a patient this unstable, pads should be on immediately after triage."
  • Doctor response (example): "Fair point. I also wonder if we should have called the regional cardiology centre earlier, even before MedSTAR, to alert them to the incoming STEMI. Some protocols recommend that."
  • RN1 response (example): "I was worried about the family communication. I only spoke to the wife briefly. In a real scenario, I'd want to check on her more often, especially during the arrest."

Round 3: System Issues & Operational Learning

Facilitator prompt: "Any system or process issues you noticed? Things about our ED or protocols that need attention?"

  • RN1 response (example): "The resuscitation medication draw-up is a bit slow. We don't have pre-loaded syringes for adrenaline or amiodarone. If we had those ready in a crash box, we'd save 20–30 seconds."
  • Doctor response (example): "That's a great point. Also, I'd like to revisit our STEMI alert pathway. Can we get a pager notification to the regional centre automatically when we do an ECG that shows STEMI? That would save time."
  • RN2 response (example): "Family space during resuscitation — we don't have a quiet room for them to wait. Wife was in the general waiting area hearing alarms and commotion. We should think about a designated family support space."

Round 4: Emotional Debrief

Facilitator prompt: "This is a challenging scenario emotionally. In rural ED, you know the community. David might be someone you saw at the coffee shop last week. How are you feeling?"

  • Reflection time: Allow 1–2 minutes of silence if needed. Some team members may be genuinely affected.
  • RN1 response (example): "Honestly, it's hard. I do know people like David in this town. You wonder if you could have done more, even though logically I know we did everything right."
  • Doctor response (example): "That's normal. Rural medicine is intimate. The distance and resource limitations can feel frustrating. But I want to reinforce: STEMI is a medical emergency. Even at a tertiary centre with all the resources, not every patient has a good outcome. We did our part well. The retrieval team and the cardiac centre team will do theirs."
  • Facilitator closing: "Take care of yourselves today. Debrief with colleagues if you need to. And know that this level of emotional investment is what makes rural nurses and doctors so valuable to their communities."

Key Learnings — Summary

Facilitator synthesis (2–3 minutes):

  • Clinical: STEMI recognition, early escalation, ANZCOR ALS protocol, medication safety, high-quality CPR
  • Communication: ISBAR handovers, team communication, MedSTAR coordination, family communication
  • Operational: Resource optimisation, medication preparation, team rotation, fatigue management
  • Emotional/Professional: Rural ED reality, community connection, sustainability of rural clinicians, peer support importance

Real-Job Translation

In a real ED debrief after a cardiac arrest, this conversation happens over coffee or during a brief break in the shift. It might be more informal, but the elements are the same: recognition of effort, identification of what worked and what didn't, system feedback, and acknowledgment of the emotional toll. In rural settings, these debriefs are especially important because there are fewer staff, less anonymity, and higher emotional stakes.

The core scenario can be adapted to create different learning opportunities. Choose one or more variations based on time and learning priorities.

Variation 1: Patient Deteriorates Before Retrieval Arrives ▼

Modification: At the 15-minute mark of resuscitation, patient remains in asystole. Heart rate is zero, pupils fixed and dilated. MedSTAR calls: "ETA now 25 minutes due to weather delay."

Learning Focus:

  • Sustained resuscitation with no hope of ROSC
  • Termination of resuscitation conversation
  • Discussion with family about poor prognosis and potential for continuing resuscitation vs stopping
  • Emotional impact on team

Scenario End: Doctor consults with family. Wife decides to stop resuscitation after 20 minutes. Team ceases CPR with respect and dignity.

Variation 2: ROSC Achieved — Post-Resuscitation Care ▼

Modification: After 10 minutes of CPR and three shocks, monitor shows organized rhythm. RN1 detects a carotid pulse. Patient has return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC).

Learning Focus:

  • Recognition of ROSC
  • Post-resuscitation stabilisation
  • Airway management in an unconscious patient post-arrest
  • Fluid management and vasopressor support
  • Targeted temperature management
  • Rapid reassessment and preparation for transfer

Scenario End: Patient is stabilised, intubated (if within scope), transferred to retrieval team with improved prognosis and higher likelihood of good outcome.

Variation 3: Second Patient Arrives During Resuscitation ▼

Modification: At the 8-minute mark of David's resuscitation, ambulance arrives with a second patient — a motor vehicle accident with head injury, GCS 8, requiring airway management.

Learning Focus:

  • Resource allocation under pressure
  • Triage decisions when staffing is limited
  • Continuation of resuscitation with diverted attention
  • Need for external support (calling additional staff, urgent MedSTAR second retrieval)
  • Rural ED operational strain

Scenario End: Team continues CPR on David while RN2 triages the new patient as ATS Category 1. Doctor must decide: stay with David or assess new patient? Additional staff called. Operational chaos and learning about rural ED limits.

Variation 4: Patient Refuses Transfer ▼

Modification: Before cardiac arrest, after ECG confirms STEMI and doctor recommends retrieval, patient refuses transfer. "I don't want to go to the city. I want to stay here. You treat me here."

Learning Focus:

  • Patient autonomy vs clinical recommendation
  • Informed refusal process
  • Risk communication
  • Legal and ethical frameworks in rural ED
  • Documentation of refusal
  • Rural context: patient may know the doctor, may distrust "the city hospital," may have family/care responsibilities locally

Scenario End: Doctor discusses risks with patient, explains why STEMI requires intervention in a cardiac centre, documents refusal, and potentially sees patient deteriorate into arrest (reinforcing why transfer was recommended).

Variation 5: Communication Failure with Retrieval Service ▼

Modification: Doctor calls MedSTAR for STEMI retrieval. Line is busy. Doctor waits 2 minutes, calls again. Radio static, poor connection. Information is missed or misunderstood. ETA is confused — was it 20 minutes or 40 minutes?

Learning Focus:

  • Communication barriers in rural settings
  • Backup communication plans
  • Persistence and escalation when initial contact fails
  • Confirmation of critical information (repeat-back)
  • Documentation of communication details
  • Rural reality: sometimes the phone system fails

Scenario End: Doctor eventually reaches MedSTAR, confirms ETA, and ensures all information is verified. Team remains prepared with contingency plan if retrieval is significantly delayed.

Variation 6: Provider Medication Error During Resuscitation ▼

Modification: During the 4-minute mark of resuscitation, RN2 prepares to draw adrenaline. In the chaos, draws wrong vial — picks up a medication that looks similar (e.g., atropine instead of adrenaline).

Learning Focus:

  • Double-check systems in high-stress scenarios
  • Error recovery and communication
  • Speaking up about mistakes
  • Medication safety culture
  • After-action analysis of near-misses

Scenario End: RN1 or Doctor catches the error before administration. Team discusses briefly, corrects, and continues. Debrief focuses on error prevention systems.

Key Takeaways

Clinical Priorities in Rural Cardiac Arrest with Underlying ACS:

  • Early Recognition: Acute chest pain + ECG changes + red flags = STEMI until proven otherwise
  • Rapid Escalation: STEMI is time-critical. Call MedSTAR early. Don't delay for test results.
  • Initial Stabilisation: Oxygen, IV access, analgesia, aspirin, and anticoagulation buy time before arrest
  • Arrest Response: ANZCOR ALS protocol is your framework. CPR, defibrillation, adrenaline, amiodarone — in sequence and on time
  • High-Quality CPR: Depth 5–6 cm, rate 100–120 bpm, minimal interruptions, team rotation every 2 minutes
  • Retrieval Coordination: ISBAR handover, family communication, documentation — all part of the chain of survival

Rural ED Operational Reality:

  • You are the first responder. The doctor may be 10 minutes away. The retrieval team may be 40 minutes away.
  • RN-led early care is often the difference between life and death
  • Communication with MedSTAR and regional centres is your lifeline
  • Resource constraints are real — no CT, no thrombolytics, limited after-hours pathology
  • The community knows you. This patient may be someone you know. Acknowledge that without letting it paralyse you.
  • Fatigue management is critical — rotate every 2 minutes, support your team, debrief together

Team & Communication Focus:

  • Clear role allocation prevents confusion and duplication
  • Structured handovers (ISBAR) reduce errors and ensure continuity
  • Closed-loop communication: "I'm getting adrenaline." "Got it. One milligram IV. Administering now."
  • Speaking up about safety concerns is not insubordination — it's professionalism
  • Debrief after high-acuity events: what went well, what could be different, what we learned
Final Note for Facilitators

This scenario is designed to be run multiple times with the same team or different configurations. Each run can emphasise different phases (e.g., focus on triage and recognition in one run, focus on resuscitation in another). Variations allow for different learning outcomes. The emotional debrief is as important as the clinical debrief, especially in rural settings where staff may experience burnout and compassion fatigue. Use this session to build team resilience, reinforce competence, and celebrate the critical work rural emergency clinicians do every day.
