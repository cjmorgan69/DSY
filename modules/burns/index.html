CPP-006-AO — Burns (AO)

Facial Burns — Fire Pit Incident with Accelerant

A 42-year-old male has sustained facial burns after diesel ignited while being poured onto a backyard fire pit. Bystanders have commenced first aid. Your Volunteer AO crew is responding.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, crew members will be able to:

  • Apply CPP-006-AO Burns protocol to a facial burn patient
  • Recognise signs that increase suspicion of airway burn and explain why ongoing monitoring is critical
  • Complete burn cooling in accordance with the 20-minute protocol, accounting for cooling already provided by bystanders
  • Correctly apply a cling film burn dressing to the face using a non-circumferential technique
  • Administer Paracetamol and Methoxyflurane within AO scope for burns pain management (CPP-013-AO)
  • Identify when to request clinical support for a Paramedic crew and communicate via EOC Clinician
  • Monitor for hypothermia during cooling and manage environmental conditions
  • Deliver an accurate ISBAR handover including burn mechanism, TBSA estimate, and airway status

Context

  • Weekday evening, approximately 18:45 hours — still light but cooling down
  • Rural property on the outskirts of town
  • Patient was lighting a backyard fire pit using diesel as an accelerant
  • The diesel ignited suddenly as it was being poured — flash burn to the face
  • Friends on scene have commenced cooling with running tap water — approximately 15 minutes completed before your arrival
  • Nearest Paramedic crew is 45 minutes away if requested
  • Nearest country hospital is approximately 25 minutes transport

Facilitator Note

Remind the crew: this patient has singed facial hair (eyebrows and beard) but currently shows no signs of airway compromise. The key learning is recognising red flags and maintaining vigilance — airway oedema can develop progressively over hours. The crew should treat this as a significant burn requiring EOC Clinician consultation.

Protocol Principle (CPP-006-AO): Burns are best treated by removal of the heat source and effective cooling by irrigation to minimise tissue damage and pain, and then additional analgesia to achieve pain control. If there is a suspected airway burn, airway management and urgent transport take precedence over burn cooling.

Scene Description

You arrive at a rural property. A gravel driveway leads to the backyard. You can see a fire pit still smouldering approximately 8 metres from the house. A 20-litre diesel container sits on the ground near the fire pit — cap off. Two friends are standing near a garden tap on the side of the house. One is holding a hose, directing water over the face of a man seated in a plastic chair. The patient is upright, eyes closed, and appears to be in significant pain. You can see redness and blistering on his face. His eyebrows and the front of his beard appear singed. He is alert and talking to his friends, though his voice sounds normal.

Facilitator Prompts

  • Is the scene safe to approach? What hazards do you see?
  • What is encouraging about what you see?
  • What is your immediate concern despite the good signs?

Expected AO Reasoning

  • Safety first: Fire pit is smouldering (not actively flaming) — low risk but maintain distance. Diesel container open nearby — keep away from ignition sources
  • Encouraging: Patient is upright, alert, talking — airway patent right now. Bystanders have done excellent first aid with water cooling
  • Concern: Facial burns with singed facial hair = red flag for potential airway burn per CPP-006-AO. Must formally assess airway even though patient is currently talking
  • Patient is outdoors in cooling evening — hypothermia risk during continued cooling

Key Teaching Points

  • Scene safety is not a tick-box exercise — on this job, there is an open diesel container near a smouldering fire. That gets moved or flagged before you commit to the scene.
  • Singed facial hair (eyebrows, nasal hair, eyelashes/brows) is a specific red flag listed in the protocol for suspected airway burn. Even if the patient seems fine right now, that finding drives your ongoing monitoring plan.
  • Acknowledge good bystander first aid — it makes a difference to outcomes and builds trust with the patient.

Real-Job Translation

The first 10 seconds on scene tell you a lot. You are looking for: is the patient up or down? Are they talking or silent? Is the scene safe? Is anyone else injured? On a burns job you are also looking for the mechanism — where was the fire, what was the fuel, how far was the patient from the ignition point. All of that information goes into your handover later.

Screening Tools, Protocols & Working Diagnosis
Decision-Making

Clinical Assessment

AO1 completes a focused burn and airway assessment.

Burns Assessment Findings

  • Mechanism: Flash burn — diesel accelerant igniting on fire pit. Brief exposure (patient turned away). No prolonged contact burn.
  • Location: Burns to forehead, bilateral cheeks, nose, and upper lip. Singed eyebrows and anterior beard.
  • Depth: Superficial to superficial partial thickness — erythema with some early blistering, blanches on pressure, painful to touch
  • Estimated TBSA: Approximately 3% (partial face involvement — Rule of Nines: head = 9%, face alone ~4.5%, partial involvement ~3%)
  • Hands/other areas: Nil — only facial involvement

Airway Assessment Findings

  • Singed eyebrows and beard — YES (red flag per protocol)
  • Singed nasal hairs — NO
  • Soot around nose or mouth — NO
  • Hoarseness or difficulty speaking — NO (voice clear and normal)
  • Wheeze — NO
  • Stridor — NO
  • Shortness of breath — NO (RR 20, SpO2 97%)
  • Altered conscious state — NO (GCS 15)
Protocol Guidance (CPP-006-AO): Signs increasing suspicion of airway burn include facial burns, singed nasal hairs or eyelashes/brows, and/or soot around nose or mouth. Monitor for hoarseness, difficulty speaking, wheeze, stridor, shortness of breath or altered conscious state.

Working Diagnosis

Superficial partial thickness facial burn (~3% TBSA) secondary to flash burn from diesel accelerant. No current airway compromise — however, facial burns with singed facial hair require ongoing airway monitoring. This is a significant burn requiring EOC Clinician consultation.

Per CPP-006-AO, if there is a suspected airway burn, the protocol is clear:

  • Airway management and urgent transport take precedence over burn cooling
  • Request clinical support
  • Consult EOC Clinician for advice
  • Provide urgent transport and notify receiving facility

In this scenario, the patient currently has no signs of airway compromise despite the facial burn location. The singed eyebrows/beard are a risk indicator, not a confirmed airway burn. The correct approach is: continue burn management per protocol, maintain close airway monitoring, and escalate immediately if any signs develop.

Thermal injury to the upper airway causes progressive oedema (swelling) that may take minutes to hours to develop. A patient who is initially talking clearly can deteriorate to complete airway obstruction. This is why the protocol emphasises ongoing monitoring even when initial assessment is reassuring. Facial burns, singed facial hair, and mechanism involving flash/flame or enclosed spaces all increase the index of suspicion.

For Volunteer AO crews, the key action is: recognise the risk, monitor continuously, and escalate early. You do not need to wait for the airway to obstruct before calling for help.

Facilitator Prompts

  • What is your working diagnosis?
  • Is this patient's airway safe right now? Will it stay safe?
  • What specific signs are you going to monitor for?
  • Does this count as a "significant burn" under the protocol?
  • What are the implications of that classification?

Expected AO Reasoning

  • Facial burn with singed hair = significant burn requiring EOC consultation
  • Airway is patent now but at risk — must monitor for hoarseness, stridor, wheeze, dyspnoea, altered GCS
  • If any airway signs develop: stop everything, manage airway, and transport urgently
  • TBSA estimation helps communicate severity — ~3% is relatively small in area but facial involvement makes it significant
  • This is not a "wait and see" situation — proactive monitoring and early escalation are required

Key Teaching Points

  • The TBSA percentage does not tell the whole story. A 3% burn sounds small, but location matters — facial burns are always classified as significant.
  • Singed facial hair is a red flag, not a diagnosis. Your job is to recognise the flag and act on it — monitor the airway and be ready to escalate.
  • A clear voice and normal SpO2 right now does not mean the airway is guaranteed to stay clear. Plan for deterioration.

Real-Job Translation

On a real job, this is the point where you should be forming your plan: finish cooling, manage pain, dress the burn, consult EOC, and transport. You should also be thinking ahead — if this patient's airway deteriorates en route, what is your backup plan? Where is the nearest Paramedic crew? What can you do with your AO skill set? Think two steps ahead.

Treatment Initiation & Medication Safety
Practical Skill

Treatment Plan Overview

Treatment follows CPP-006-AO in this order:

  1. Complete burn cooling (additional 5 minutes with running water to achieve 20-minute total)
  2. Administer analgesia — Paracetamol 1g PO + Methoxyflurane (Penthrox) inhaler
  3. Apply cling film burn dressing once cooling is complete
  4. Manage environmental conditions — prevent hypothermia
  5. Consult EOC Clinician (significant burn — facial involvement)

Step 1 — Complete Burn Cooling

  • Confirm timing with bystanders: "How long has the water been running on the burn?"
  • Continue running tap water over the burned area for a minimum additional 5 minutes (to achieve 20-minute total per protocol)
  • Water should be cool running water — not ice, not freezing
  • Monitor temperature during cooling — patient is already 36.3°C
  • Wrap a blanket around the patient's torso and lower body to manage heat loss while cooling the face
Hypothermia Risk: The protocol explicitly states: "Manage environmental conditions to ensure patients do not become hypothermic during cooling or irrigation." It is evening, the patient is outdoors, and has been wet for 15+ minutes. Blankets and sheltering from wind are essential.

Step 3 — Burn Dressing (Cling Film)

Once cooling is complete (20-minute total achieved), apply a cling film dressing to the cooled burn area.

StepDetail
1. Ensure safetyEnsure appropriate PPE is worn — clean gloves
2. Prepare cling filmDiscard first 5–10 cm of cling film from the roll (the exposed edge is not sterile — the roll underneath is essentially sterile)
3. Apply to burnLay sheets of cling film over the cooled burn area. For facial burns: lay flat sheets across the burned areas of the face. Do NOT wrap circumferentially around the head or neck.
4. LayeringApply in overlapping layers as needed to cover the full burn area. Cling film is transparent — allows ongoing visual inspection without removing the dressing.
5. SecureFor facial burns, cling film generally adheres to itself and moist skin. No circumferential bandaging around the head is required.
6. Continue coolingPer protocol: continue cooling over the burn cover as needed. If the patient reports increased pain, gently irrigate cool water over the cling film.
Why Cling Film? Polyvinyl chloride film (cling film) is ideal for burns dressing. It is pliable, non-adherent, impermeable, acts as a barrier, minimises exposure to infection, and is transparent for ongoing inspection of the burn without removing the dressing.
Critical Rule — Facial Application: Never apply cling film circumferentially around the face or head. Lay flat sheets only. Ensure nostrils and mouth remain completely clear and unobstructed at all times.

Hydrogel (Burnaid) dressings are carried on SAAS vehicles in two sizes: face mask and large rectangle. However, per CPP-006-AO and clinical skills guidance:

  • Hydrogel should only be considered in situations where cooling by other means is unavailable
  • In this scenario, running water was available and used effectively — hydrogel is not indicated
  • If hydrogel had been used: maximum application time is 20 minutes (10 minutes for neonates)
  • Hydrogel dressings must be removed once cooling is completed before applying cling film
  • Do not use hydrogel on chemical burns

Step 4 — EOC Clinician Consultation

AO2 contacts EOC Clinician to consult on this significant burn.

  • Facial burns = significant burn per protocol — consultation is required
  • Provide: patient age, mechanism, burn location and estimated TBSA, depth, airway status, current treatment, observations
  • Request guidance on: transport destination, need for Paramedic rendezvous, any additional treatment advice
  • If near Adelaide metro area, EOC Clinician may advise direct transport to burns unit (RAH for adults, WCH for paediatrics)
  • In a rural setting, EOC may arrange MedSTAR retrieval or advise transport to nearest ED with onward transfer

Facilitator Prompts

  • Walk me through your treatment plan in the order you would do it.
  • How much more cooling does this patient need? How did you work that out?
  • What checks do you need to do before giving Paracetamol? Before Penthrox?
  • Demonstrate how you would set up and explain the Penthrox inhaler to the patient.
  • Show me how you would apply cling film to a facial burn. What is the critical safety rule?
  • Why are you consulting EOC Clinician on this job?
  • At what point would you request a Paramedic crew?

Expected AO Reasoning

  • Cooling first (complete the 20 minutes), analgesia early (give during cooling), dress after cooling is complete
  • Paracetamol and Penthrox together provide multimodal pain relief within AO scope
  • Paracetamol takes 30–60 mins for full effect — give it early so it is working during transport
  • Penthrox provides rapid-onset analgesia while waiting for Paracetamol to take effect
  • Cling film is non-circumferential on the face — laid in flat sheets, never wrapped around the head
  • EOC consultation is mandatory for significant burns — facial involvement meets this threshold
  • Request Paramedic if pain is uncontrolled after Paracetamol + Penthrox (max 2 bottles)

Key Teaching Points

  • Sequence matters: complete cooling, analgesia, dress, consult, transport. But do not delay analgesia while waiting for cooling to finish — give Paracetamol and Penthrox early during cooling.
  • Cling film on the face is laid in flat sheets — never circumferential. Keep the airway clear. This is the most commonly asked question about facial burn dressing.
  • Two analgesics are better than one — Paracetamol + Penthrox gives both sustained and rapid-onset relief. This is within AO scope and should be used.
  • If pain is still uncontrolled, you have escalation options — but the Paramedic is 45 minutes away. Factor that into your transport decision. A rendezvous en route may be more efficient than waiting on scene.

Real-Job Translation

On a real job, pain management is one of the things patients remember most. A burns patient in severe pain needs you to act confidently and quickly. Have your medications checked, drawn, and ready. Give Paracetamol first (it takes longer to work), then set up Penthrox for immediate relief. Talk the patient through what you are doing — "This is a pain-relieving inhaler, it will start working within a few breaths." Be calm, be competent, and communicate.

  • Wrap patient in dry blankets — torso, limbs, around shoulders

  • Airway remains clear — but must continue monitoring. This does not mean the risk has passed.

  • Temperature dropping — time to stop cooling, warm the patient, and move to the ambulance
  • Paramedic not immediately required but remains an option — communicate status to EOC

Key Teaching Points

  • Reassessment is not optional — it tells you whether your treatment is working and whether the patient is stable or deteriorating.
  • A reducing pain score is a good sign, but the job is not over. Analgesia can wear off, and airway oedema can still develop.
  • Temperature management on burns jobs is a practical challenge — especially outdoors. Once cooling is done, warming the patient becomes the priority.
  • Your escalation threshold should be low, not high. If in doubt, call EOC and request support. It is easier to stand down a Paramedic crew than to wish you had called one earlier.

Real-Job Translation

Reassessment is where you prove your treatment is working. Get a second set of obs, compare them to the first, and document the trend. If the numbers are better and the patient reports less pain, you are on the right track. If not, change your plan. This is not a linear process — you assess, treat, reassess, adjust. Every time.

Extrication & Transport Planning
Decision-Making

Extrication

  • Patient is ambulant — seated in a chair in the backyard
  • Assist patient to stand and walk to the ambulance, or use carry chair if unsteady
  • Ensure patient is wrapped in blankets before moving through the cool evening air
  • Patient to be positioned seated upright on the stretcher — this assists with any potential airway oedema management and is more comfortable for facial burns
  • Secure Penthrox inhaler so patient can continue self-administering during transport
  • Ensure Corpuls3 is reconnected and monitoring continuously during transport

Transport Decision

Per CPP-006-AO, the crew should consult EOC Clinician regarding transport destination for significant burns:

  • If near Adelaide metro: Consider direct transport to a burns unit — Royal Adelaide Hospital (adult) or Women's and Children's Hospital (paediatrics)
  • If regional/rural (this scenario): Transport to nearest appropriate ED (country hospital — approx 25 minutes). EOC Clinician and receiving facility can arrange onward transfer to RAH Burns Unit if required (via MedSTAR retrieval or road transfer).

For this scenario: Transport to the nearest country hospital (25 mins). Notify receiving facility of incoming burns patient. EOC Clinician to coordinate onward management.

In-Transport Monitoring Plan

  • Continuous SpO2 and ECG via Corpuls3
  • NIBP every 5 minutes during transport
  • Verbal airway checks every 2–3 minutes: ask the patient to speak — listen for any change in voice quality
  • Pain score reassessment every 5 minutes
  • Temperature check at midpoint of transport
  • Visual inspection of burn through cling film — look for changes
  • Penthrox management — is the patient still self-administering? Is a second bottle needed?
If Airway Deterioration Occurs During Transport:
  • Position patient upright
  • Apply high-flow oxygen via non-rebreather mask (15L/min)
  • Prepare suction
  • Contact EOC Clinician immediately — request urgent Paramedic rendezvous or diversion to nearest facility
  • Airway management and transport take precedence over all other treatment
  • Consider: is the current destination still appropriate, or should you divert?

Facilitator Prompts

  • How are you going to move this patient to the ambulance?
  • What position are you transporting in and why?
  • What is your transport destination and why?
  • What is your monitoring plan during the 25-minute transport?
  • What is your contingency if the airway deteriorates en route?

Expected AO Reasoning

  • Patient is ambulant — assist to walk, carry chair if needed. Minimise exertion.
  • Seated upright position for transport — facilitates airway drainage and is more comfortable
  • Nearest country hospital is 25 minutes — appropriate for initial assessment and stabilisation. Burns unit transfer can be arranged from there.
  • Continuous monitoring is essential — particularly verbal airway checks. A change in voice quality is an early sign of developing oedema.
  • Have a contingency plan for airway deterioration: oxygen, suction, EOC call, possible diversion

Key Teaching Points

  • Transport position matters. Seated upright is preferred for facial burns — it reduces oedema formation and keeps the airway in a favourable position.
  • The 25-minute transport window is your highest-risk period for airway deterioration. Keep talking to the patient — if their voice changes, you need to know immediately.
  • Pre-notify the receiving hospital. A burns patient arriving unannounced creates delays. A phone call ahead allows the ED to prepare.
  • Your contingency plan does not need to be complex. It needs to exist. "If the airway goes, I will do X, Y, Z." Have that plan before you start driving.

Real-Job Translation

The transport phase is where many crews relax — the hard work feels done. On a facial burns job, the transport phase is actually when you need to stay the most alert. Airway oedema develops over time, and you are now 25 minutes away from definitive help. Talk to your patient continuously. Ask them to count to ten out loud every few minutes. If the voice changes, act immediately.

ISBAR Handover
Practical Skill

Facilitator Direction

The crew has arrived at the country hospital. AO1 delivers an ISBAR handover to the receiving ED clinician. AO2 provides the latest set of observations and manages the Corpuls3 transfer.

Sample ISBAR Handover

ComponentContent
I — Identify "I'm [Name], Ambulance Officer from [Station]. This is our patient — I'll hand over to you now."
S — Situation "We have a 42-year-old male with facial burns sustained approximately 45 minutes ago. He was pouring diesel onto a backyard fire pit when it ignited, causing a flash burn to his face."
B — Background "No significant past medical history. No regular medications. No known allergies. The burns involve the forehead, bilateral cheeks, nose, and upper lip — estimated 3% TBSA, superficial to superficial partial thickness. His eyebrows and beard are singed. He has had 20 minutes total cooling with running water — 15 minutes by bystanders, 5 minutes by us."
A — Assessment "Current observations: GCS 15, heart rate 94 sinus rhythm, BP 138/86, respiratory rate 18, SpO2 98% on room air, temperature 36.1. Pain currently 5 out of 10, down from 8 on arrival. Airway is currently patent — no hoarseness, stridor, or wheeze. However, he has facial burns with singed facial hair, so ongoing airway monitoring is required."
R — Recommendation "We have applied cling film dressing to the facial burns. He has received Paracetamol 1 gram oral and Methoxyflurane via inhaler for pain management. We have consulted EOC Clinician who is aware. We recommend ongoing airway monitoring given the facial burn mechanism and continued pain management. He may need referral to RAH Burns Unit — EOC can assist with retrieval coordination."

Facilitator Prompts

  • Deliver your ISBAR handover as you would on a real job.
  • What is the single most important thing the receiving clinician needs to know about this patient?
  • What would you do differently if the patient had developed hoarseness during transport?
  • Is there anything you would add or change in that handover?

Expected AO Reasoning

  • The ISBAR should be structured, concise, and prioritise the key clinical risk — airway status
  • The most important information: facial burns with singed facial hair, currently no airway compromise but requires ongoing monitoring for delayed oedema
  • Include mechanism (diesel accelerant, flash burn), burn details (location, TBSA, depth), treatment given, and observations trend
  • If hoarseness had developed: this becomes a time-critical airway handover — lead with "this patient has a deteriorating airway" and request immediate senior clinician assessment

Key Teaching Points

  • A good handover saves time and prevents errors. The receiving team should not have to ask you for basic information — give it to them in a logical order.
  • For burns handovers, the receiving team needs to know: mechanism, time of burn, cooling duration, burn location and TBSA estimate, depth, airway status, and treatment given.
  • Lead with the risk. For this patient, the risk is potential delayed airway compromise. Make sure that message is clear — do not bury it in the middle of your handover.
  • Practice ISBAR until it is automatic. On a real job, you will be tired, stressed, and distracted. If the structure is drilled, it comes out right even under pressure.

Real-Job Translation

Handover is the last clinical act of the job and one of the most important. A clear, structured ISBAR ensures continuity of care. The receiving team will base their initial management plan on what you tell them. If you forget to mention the singed facial hair, they may not assess for airway burn risk as urgently. Your words directly affect the patient's ongoing care.

Debrief (10–15 minutes)

Reflective Questions

1. What went well in the way you managed this patient? What would you do the same way on a real job?
2. The patient had singed facial hair but no current airway compromise. How did that finding change your management plan compared to a burn on, say, the forearm?
3. Bystanders had already started cooling. How did you confirm the timing, and how did it affect your cooling plan?
4. Walk me through your pain management sequence. Why did you give both Paracetamol and Penthrox, and in what order?
5. At what point would you have requested a Paramedic crew? What specific triggers would change your escalation decision?
6. How did you manage the hypothermia risk? What practical steps did you take?
7. During the 25-minute transport, what was your monitoring plan for this patient's airway? How often, and what were you specifically looking for?

Take-Home Messages

1. Facial burns are always significant — regardless of TBSA. They require EOC Clinician consultation, close airway monitoring, and a low threshold for escalation.
2. Singed facial hair is a red flag, not a diagnosis. It increases your suspicion of potential airway burn — act on that suspicion with monitoring and early escalation, do not wait for signs to develop.
3. Cooling is 20 minutes with running water. Bystander cooling counts — confirm timing and complete the remainder. Once done, stop cooling and actively warm the patient.
4. Cling film on the face is laid in flat sheets — never circumferential. Keep nostrils and mouth clear. It is transparent, non-adherent, and allows ongoing inspection.
5. Pain management within AO scope is Paracetamol plus Methoxyflurane. Give them both, give them early, and reassess. If pain is still uncontrolled, escalate via EOC for Paramedic support — do not delay.
6. Hypothermia is a real risk on burns jobs — especially with prolonged outdoor cooling in the evening. Manage environmental conditions actively: blankets, shelter, ambulance heating.
7. The transport phase is not the relaxation phase. For facial burns, the highest risk of airway deterioration may occur during transport. Talk to your patient continuously and listen for voice changes.

Optional Scenario Variations

Variation 1 — Developing Airway Compromise

Ten minutes into transport, the patient's voice becomes hoarse and he reports a "tight" feeling in his throat. SpO2 drops to 94%. What changes in your management? This variation tests the crew's ability to switch from routine transport to time-critical airway management: upright positioning, high-flow oxygen, EOC Clinician contact, request for Paramedic rendezvous or diversion, and pre-notification of receiving facility.

Variation 2 — Paediatric Patient (8-year-old)

The patient is an 8-year-old child who was standing near the fire pit when the diesel ignited. The child has burns to the face and both hands. A parent is present and distressed. This variation tests: paediatric TBSA estimation (Rule of Nines differs for children), communication with a frightened child, parent management, modified medication dosing, and the need for transport to Women's and Children's Hospital (paediatric burns unit).

Variation 3 — Extensive Burns with Pain Uncontrolled

The same 42-year-old, but the flash burn also affected his chest and both forearms (estimated 22% TBSA). His pain score remains 9/10 after Paracetamol and two bottles of Penthrox. He is agitated and distressed. This variation tests: escalation for Paramedic support (IV/IM analgesia), larger TBSA management, fluid resuscitation awareness, and coordination of a rendezvous en route to hospital. The Paramedic crew is 45 minutes away — where do you meet?

Variation 4 — Remote Property, No Running Water

Same mechanism, but the property is remote with no running water supply (tank is empty). The only cooling available is a limited supply of bottled water and your on-board saline. This variation tests: decision to use Burnaid hydrogel dressings when running water is unavailable, correct application of the hydrogel face mask, timing limits (20 minutes maximum), and the importance of removing hydrogel before applying cling film.
