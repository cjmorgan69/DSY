Envenomation — AO Walk-Through Scenario

Title & Context

Module: Envenomation — Walk-Through Scenario (Snake Bite)

Protocol: CPP-008-AO Envenomation — AO

Crew: 2-person Volunteer AO crew

Setting: Rural property, Lower Eyre Peninsula — Saturday afternoon in January

Duration: Approximately 60–75 minutes (including debrief)

This session walks through a suspected brown snake bite on a rural property. The scenario focuses on pressure immobilisation bandaging, limb and whole-body immobilisation, minimal patient exertion, early EOC Clinician consultation, and transport decision-making with limited backup.

Australian native snake bite is the most common envenomation emergency in South Australia. Early cardiovascular collapse leading to cardiac arrest is the most significant immediate risk.

Learning Objectives

  1. Apply the CPP-008-AO Envenomation protocol accurately for a native snake bite to a limb
  2. Demonstrate correct pressure immobilisation bandage (PIB) technique on a lower limb
  3. Demonstrate vacuum mattress application with head elevation (20–30°)
  4. Minimise patient exertion throughout the entire encounter
  5. Identify early signs of systemic envenomation and escalation triggers
  6. Deliver a structured ISBAR handover for a snake bite patient
  7. Articulate the rationale for NOT washing the bite site or applying a tourniquet

Pre-Brief (approx 10 minutes)

+

Protocol Refresher — CPP-008-AO

Before beginning the scenario, ensure both crew members are familiar with the following key points from the Envenomation protocol:

Australian Native Snake Bites — Key Principles

  • The most significant immediate clinical risk is early cardiovascular collapse leading to cardiac arrest
  • Envenomation can also cause paralysis, muscle breakdown (rhabdomyolysis), renal damage, and coagulopathy
  • Do NOT wash the bite site — venom residue is needed for venom detection kit (VDK) at hospital
  • Do NOT apply a tourniquet — if one is already applied, consult EOC Clinician before removal
  • Ensure minimal patient exertion — movement accelerates venom absorption through the lymphatic system
  • Request clinical support for all native snake bites

Management — Bite to a Limb

  • Apply pressure immobilisation bandage to the affected limb
  • Immobilise the limb with a rigid splint
  • Immobilise the whole patient with a vacuum mattress if available
  • Where possible, position with head elevated approximately 20–30 degrees
  • Provide transport and notify the receiving facility

Management — Bite to Head or Torso

  • Cover bite area with a simple dressing only
  • Pressure immobilisation bandage is NOT applicable to head/torso
  • Same principles: minimal exertion, immobilise, transport

Treatment Phase — AO Protocol (Skills Practice)

This is the primary skills practice section. Both crew members should physically practise the pressure immobilisation bandage and splinting technique.

Step 1 — Preparation

  • Reassure the patient: "Greg, we're going to put a firm bandage on your leg to slow down the venom. It's important you stay completely still — don't move your leg at all. We'll do all the work."
  • Gain verbal consent
  • Instruct the patient: "Do not move. We will move you when we're ready."
  • Gather equipment: 2–3 broad elastic bandages (10–15 cm), SAM splint, pen/marker
  • Position yourself at the affected limb — remove the damp cloth carefully

Step 2 — Mark the Bite Site

  • If puncture marks are visible, mark their location on the skin with a pen
  • In this scenario, the wash has partially obscured the marks — mark the approximate area
  • This mark will be transferred to the outside of the bandage after application

Step 3 — Apply the Pressure Immobilisation Bandage

First bandage — directly over the bite site:

  • Place the start of the bandage directly over the bite site
  • Wrap firmly around the calf 2–3 times to anchor the bandage
  • This provides direct pressure over the bite to slow lymphatic drainage

Second bandage — full limb coverage:

  • Begin at the toes — start wrapping from the most distal point of the limb
  • Wrap firmly up the limb towards the trunk (toes, foot, ankle, calf, knee, thigh)
  • Overlap each wrap by approximately half the bandage width
  • Continue as far up the limb as possible — ideally to the groin
  • Use a third bandage to complete full limb coverage if needed

Firmness guide:

  • As firm as you would bandage a sprained ankle
  • Should be able to slide one finger underneath the bandage
  • Firm enough to compress lymphatic flow — NOT tight enough to occlude arterial flow
  • This is NOT a tourniquet

Step 4 — Mark the Bite Site on the Bandage

  • Using your pen, draw a clear mark (cross or circle) on the outside of the bandage over the bite location
  • This allows hospital staff to locate the bite for venom detection without removing the bandage

Step 5 — Splint the Limb

  • Apply a rigid splint (SAM splint or improvised) along the length of the limb
  • Immobilise the joints above and below the bite — for calf: immobilise the ankle and the knee
  • Secure the splint over the bandage with additional bandage or tape
  • The goal: the patient cannot bend or move the affected limb

Step 6 — Check Distal Circulation

  • Check right foot: colour (pink, not blue/white), warmth, sensation (can feel touch), movement (can wiggle toes — gently), capillary refill (less than 2 seconds)
  • If circulation is compromised — bandage is too tight — loosen slightly and recheck
  • Recheck distal circulation every 5 minutes

Critical Reminders

  • Do NOT remove the bandage once applied — it is only removed in hospital with antivenom available and IV access established
  • Do NOT allow the patient to walk — not to the toilet, not to the ambulance, not at all
  • Keep the limb at or below heart level until positioned on the vacuum mattress
Break Point 5Practical Skill

Treatment Initiation & Bandaging Technique

Facilitator Prompts

  • "Both crew — practise the PIB on each other now. Start from the bite, then full limb, distal to proximal."
  • "Check your partner's bandage — can you slide a finger underneath?"
  • "Where have you marked the bite on the bandage? Can you see it clearly?"
  • "How have you immobilised the ankle and knee joints?"
  • "Check distal circulation — what are you looking for?"
  • "Greg's wife asks: 'Should I have left the wound alone?' — what do you say?"

Expected AO Reasoning

  • The PIB slows lymphatic spread of venom — it buys time for transport to definitive care
  • Starting distally and wrapping proximally ensures even compression
  • Marking the bite on the bandage allows hospital VDK swab without removing the bandage
  • Splinting prevents limb movement — any muscle contraction accelerates lymphatic flow
  • Distal circulation checks confirm the bandage is not acting as a tourniquet
  • Response to wife: "You did the right thing calling us quickly. In future, the best first aid is to keep them still and put a firm bandage on — but what matters now is we've got it under control."

Key Teaching Points

  • The most common error with PIB is applying it too loosely — firm like a sprained ankle
  • Full limb coverage (toes to groin) is the gold standard — a bandage only over the bite is insufficient
  • Practise this skill until it is automatic — you do not want to be learning this technique at 0200 on a dark verandah
  • The bandage is the single most important prehospital intervention for Australian snake bite

Real-Job Translation

On a real job, the PIB is the difference-maker. It is your treatment. Everything else — Corpuls3, oxygen, vacuum mattress — supports this. If you can apply a solid PIB and keep the patient still, you have done the most important thing. Practise it until your hands know what to do.

Reassessment & Escalation Decision

Facilitator Prompts

  • "Has this patient improved, stayed the same, or deteriorated?"
  • "What are you telling the EOC Clinician right now?"
  • "Do you need MedSTAR? Why or why not?"

Expected AO Reasoning

  • Slight BP drop combined with rising HR suggests early compensatory response

  • Early hospital notification allows the ED to prepare antivenom, VDK, bloods, and resuscitation equipment

Real-Job Translation

On a real job, you are constantly reassessing. You might only get two sets of obs before you need to move. But those two sets tell you a story: if the numbers are moving the wrong way, you move faster. You don't wait for the patient to arrest to decide this is serious.

Extrication & Transport

The patient is on the back verandah (elevated, 3 steps down to ground level). The ambulance is parked approximately 15 m from the base of the steps.

Extrication Plan

  1. Green carry chair to the stretcher — the patient must NOT walk
    • Position the carry chair next to the patient on the verandah
    • Carefully assist Greg from the deck chair into the carry chair, supporting the splinted right leg
    • One crew member manages the carry chair; the other supports and protects the bandaged/splinted limb
    • Carry down the steps — controlled, steady, communicate
  2. Stretcher with vacuum mattress prepared at ground level
    • Vacuum mattress is already laid out flat on the stretcher, valve closed, beads distributed
    • Transfer Greg from carry chair onto the stretcher/vacuum mattress
    • Minimise movement — guide and support, do not rush
  3. Vacuum mattress application
    • Position Greg supine on the mattress
    • Mould the mattress around the patient — head, shoulders, torso, limbs
    • Ensure the bandaged/splinted right leg is well-supported but accessible for circulation checks
    • Elevate the head end approximately 20–30 degrees
    • Open valve and extract air with the vacuum pump until the mattress is rigid
    • Close the valve to maintain vacuum
    • Apply retaining straps
    • Confirm patient is secure, comfortable, and the limb is accessible
  4. Load into ambulance and depart
    • Load stretcher into ambulance
    • Clinician travels in rear with the patient
    • Continue Corpuls3 monitoring, keep suction and airway equipment within reach
    • Have vomit bag/kidney dish ready

Transport — En Route to Port Lincoln Hospital

  • Destination: Port Lincoln Hospital ED (~30 minutes)
  • Driving: Smooth, steady — avoid sudden braking or acceleration
  • Monitoring: Continuous Corpuls3, repeat full obs every 5–10 minutes
  • Watch for: Worsening tachycardia, hypotension, respiratory depression, altered consciousness, coagulopathy (bleeding from gums, IV sites, or bite site through bandage)
  • Airway: Position for drainage if vomiting continues — slight left lateral tilt if possible within vacuum mattress, suction ready
  • If cardiac arrest occurs: Commence CPR per protocol — early cardiovascular collapse is the primary lethal mechanism

Extrication & Transport Planning

Facilitator Prompts

  • "Talk me through how you get Greg from the verandah to the ambulance without him taking a single step."
  • "Why the carry chair and not just walking him down the steps?"
  • "Walk through the vacuum mattress application — what angle for the head?"
  • "What are you preparing for in the back of the ambulance during the 30-minute transport?"
  • "At what point during transport would you upgrade your concern?"

Expected AO Reasoning

  • The carry chair eliminates all walking — this is non-negotiable for snake bite. Any muscle contraction in the legs accelerates lymphatic spread of venom
  • The vacuum mattress provides whole-body immobilisation AND allows head elevation to 20–30 degrees as per protocol
  • During transport: airway management and suction are the priority preparations — if this patient's paralysis progresses, airway and breathing will fail first
  • Upgrade concern if: HR greater than 120, systolic BP less than 100, any signs of paralysis (ptosis, difficulty swallowing), altered consciousness, or active bleeding

Key Teaching Points

  • The carry chair is your best tool for minimising patient exertion — especially from elevated surfaces like verandahs, steps, or upper floors
  • Vacuum mattress with head elevation is specifically mentioned in CPP-008-AO — this is not optional, it is the protocol
  • 30 minutes in the back of an ambulance with a deteriorating snake bite patient is a long time — you need a plan for the worst case
  • Smooth driving reduces patient movement and anxiety — communicate with your driver

Real-Job Translation

On a real job, the extrication is where crews often lose discipline. The patient says "I can walk" — you say "We need you completely still." The wife offers to help him down the stairs — you politely take over. Every step that patient takes is venom moving through their system. The carry chair, the vacuum mattress, the smooth transport — this is the care.

Handover — ISBAR

You arrive at Port Lincoln Hospital ED. The team has been pre-notified and is expecting you.

ISBAR Handover

I — Identification

"This is [your name], Volunteer AO crew from [station]. This is Greg Thompson, 52-year-old male."

S — Situation

"Greg has a suspected brown snake bite to his right lower leg, approximately 75 minutes ago. He is symptomatic with vomiting, headache, and progressive tachycardia."

B — Background

"He was clearing brush on a rural property at Wangary when he was bitten. He saw a brown snake approximately 1.5 metres long. He walked about 100 metres back to his house before we arrived. His wife washed the bite site with water before our arrival. Past history of hypertension, takes Perindopril 5 mg daily. No allergies."

A — Assessment

"On our arrival his HR was 92 and he had a headache and mild nausea. We applied a pressure immobilisation bandage to the full right leg, splinted the limb, and immobilised him in a vacuum mattress with head elevated 20 degrees. On reassessment his HR had increased to 104, BP dropped slightly to 130/78, and he vomited once. Current obs: HR 108, BP 126/76, RR 20, SpO2 97% on room air, GCS 15. No ptosis, swallowing intact, no bleeding from other sites. Distal circulation intact throughout."

R — Recommendation

"He will need venom detection testing — noting the bite was washed. Bloods for coagulation and renal function. Observation for progressive envenomation. Possible antivenom. We have not removed the pressure bandage — the bite site is marked on the outside of the bandage. EOC Clinician was consulted and is aware."

Break Point 8Practical Skill

ISBAR Handover

Facilitator Prompts

  • "Clinician — deliver the full ISBAR handover out loud, as if you're standing in the ED."
  • "What specific information does the ED need for a snake bite patient?"
  • "What do you mention about the bite site being washed?"
  • "What is your recommendation to the receiving team?"

Expected AO Reasoning

  • ISBAR is structured and ensures nothing is missed — particularly time of bite, snake description, distance walked, and first aid applied
  • Mentioning the washed bite is critical — it tells the ED that VDK swab may be unreliable and they may need to rely on clinical presentation and bloods
  • Trending observations (HR 92, 104, 108) are more valuable than single numbers — include the trend
  • Clearly state the bandage has NOT been removed and that the bite site is marked — hospital will manage bandage removal under controlled conditions

Key Teaching Points

  • A good ISBAR takes 60–90 seconds and gives the receiving team everything they need
  • Practise it out loud — not in your head. The handover is a clinical skill like any other
  • For snake bite, the critical handover elements are: snake type, time of bite, patient exertion after bite, first aid applied, symptom progression, obs trends
  • Do NOT remove the PIB at handover — that is a hospital decision

Real-Job Translation

On a real job, the ED may be busy when you arrive. A clear, structured ISBAR delivered calmly gets your patient prioritised correctly. If you mumble through it or forget the timeline, critical information is lost. Write key points on your glove or a note card during transport if it helps.

Debrief (10–15 minutes)

Reflective Questions

  1. What were your initial priorities when you read the MDT — and did they change after you arrived on scene?
  2. How confident did you feel applying the pressure immobilisation bandage? What would you do differently next time?
  3. At what point did you recognise this patient was deteriorating, and what triggered that recognition?
  4. How did you manage the bystander situation — the wife washing the bite, the anxiety — while staying focused on treatment?
  5. Walk through your extrication plan. Was there a moment where the patient could have been allowed to exert themselves? How did you prevent that?
  6. How did you divide tasks between clinician and driver? Was communication clear throughout?
  7. If this patient had arrested in the back of the ambulance during transport, what would you have done?

Take-Home Messages

  1. All Australian native snake bites are potentially lethal — a well-appearing patient can arrest without warning
  2. The pressure immobilisation bandage is your primary treatment — firm, full limb, distal to proximal, and do not remove it
  3. Minimal patient exertion is non-negotiable — no walking, no standing, no helping. Use the carry chair
  4. Do not wash the bite site — venom detection requires trace venom on the skin. If it has been washed, document it and move on
  5. Trending observations detect deterioration early — baseline obs, then reassess, then compare. The direction of change is your early warning
  6. Early EOC Clinician contact and hospital notification — puts the system on notice and ensures antivenom is ready on arrival
  7. Prepare for the worst during transport — airway, suction, and a plan for cardiac arrest should always be in your mind for snake bite

Optional Scenario Variations

Use these to extend the session, challenge experienced crews, or revisit on a different training night.

Variation 1 — Bite to the Torso

Greg was bitten on the left flank while bending over. A pressure immobilisation bandage cannot be applied to the torso.

Management change: Cover the bite area with a simple dressing only. Focus on whole-body immobilisation (vacuum mattress), minimal exertion, and rapid transport. Same escalation pathway.

Teaching focus: Not all bites can be bandaged — know the protocol for non-limb bites.

Variation 2 — Patient Found in Paddock

Greg is 200 m from the house, lying in long grass near the water tank. He called his wife on his mobile. He has not walked since the bite.

Management change: Extrication over rough ground — may need to carry equipment to the patient. Apply PIB in the field, then stretcher carry back to the ambulance. More physically demanding for the crew.

Teaching focus: It is better for the patient to stay still and for you to go to them, than for the patient to walk to you.

Variation 3 — Rapid Cardiovascular Collapse

During transport, Greg becomes drowsy, HR spikes to 130, BP drops to 88/50. He then loses consciousness and becomes pulseless.

Management change: Cardiac arrest management. Crew must extract from vacuum mattress rapidly, commence CPR, apply AED/Corpuls3 defib pads, manage airway and ventilation, and continue transport under CPR.

Teaching focus: This is the lethal pathway for snake envenomation. Early cardiovascular collapse. Be ready for it.

Variation 4 — Child Bitten in the Garden

8-year-old bitten on the right hand while playing near a woodpile. Parents unsure of snake type but describe it as brown. Child is crying, frightened, hand is swollen.

Management change: Same protocol applies. PIB to the full right arm (fingers to axilla). Reassurance is critical. Use age-appropriate communication. Paediatric obs and monitoring. Consider early MedSTAR notification for paediatric envenomation.

Teaching focus: Paediatric snake bite — same protocol, smaller patient, higher risk due to lower body mass relative to venom dose.
