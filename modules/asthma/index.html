Asthma – AO Protocol

SAAS CPP-050-AO | Severity & Treatment Table

Walk-Through Scenario – 2-Person Volunteer AO Crew

Scenario Overview

An 8-year-old girl with known asthma is having difficulty breathing at home. Mum has tried her reliever puffer but she's not improving. This scenario covers the full spectrum from assessment through to treatment and escalation for a deteriorating paediatric asthma patient.

Key Concept: Asthma treatment focuses on relief of bronchospasm using bronchodilators. Severity is determined by ability to speak, work of breathing, AND oxygen saturations — never SpO₂ alone.

Learning Objectives

  • Accurately classify asthma severity using the SAAS Asthma Severity Table
  • Demonstrate correct MDI + spacer technique for paediatric patients
  • Prepare and administer nebulised salbutamol and ipratropium
  • Recognise signs of deterioration requiring escalation to EOC Clinician
  • Differentiate asthma from anaphylaxis and follow appropriate pathway
  • Understand IPPV considerations in life-threatening asthma

Required Equipment

Assessment

  • Corpuls3 monitor
  • SpO₂ probe (paediatric)
  • Stethoscope
  • ePCR tablet

Treatment

  • MDI spacer device
  • Salbutamol MDI (100 microg/puff)
  • Nebuliser mask (paediatric)
  • Salbutamol nebules (2.5mg & 5mg)
  • Ipratropium nebules (250 & 500 microg)
  • Normal saline 3mL
  • Oxygen cylinder + regulator
  • BVM with paediatric mask

Session Structure

Total Duration: 45–60 minutes

  • Pre-Brief: 10 minutes
  • Scenario Walk-Through: 25–35 minutes (8 break points)
  • Debrief: 10–15 minutes

Pre-Brief (≈10 minutes)

Facilitator sets the scene and reviews key concepts with the crew before running the scenario.

Brief Crew On:

  • This is a walk-through with pause points for discussion
  • Clinician and Driver roles — both will be hands-on
  • Focus on assessment, severity classification, and treatment selection
  • Emphasis on recognising deterioration and EOC escalation triggers
Facilitator Reminder: If the patient shows signs consistent with anaphylaxis (urticaria, angioedema, known allergen exposure), crew should be redirected to CPP-003-AO Anaphylaxis protocol — adrenaline first, bronchodilators second.
Break Point 1 Discussion / Planning

Dispatch & En-Route Phase

MDT Information:

CASE: 2847
Priority 2 – Breathing Difficulty
8 y/o female, difficulty breathing
Known asthmatic – reliever not working
Mother on scene, child conscious
Location: 47 Murray Street, Port Lincoln
Cross: Boston Road
Contact: 0412 XXX XXX (Mum – Sarah)

Facilitator Prompts:

  • "What's your initial thinking based on this dispatch info?"
  • "What equipment will you stage at the door?"
  • "What severity are you anticipating and why?"
  • "What would change your approach if this was anaphylaxis?"

Expected AO Reasoning:

8-year-old with known asthma, reliever not effective = likely more severe than mild. Should prepare for nebuliser therapy. Need to rule out anaphylaxis on arrival. Stage: Response bag, Corpuls3, oxygen, nebuliser setup, MDI + spacer.

Key Teaching Points:

  • "Reliever not working" suggests severity beyond mild
  • Prepare for nebulised medication — don't just grab MDI
  • Child conscious is reassuring but doesn't rule out severe asthma
Real-Job Translation: En-route mental preparation prevents fumbling on scene. Anticipate worst-case (severe asthma → life-threatening) while hoping for best-case (mild, responds to MDI).
Break Point 2 Discussion / Assessment

Doorway / First Impressions

Scene Description:

You arrive at a suburban home. Mum meets you at the door looking worried. She leads you to the lounge room where Lily (8 y/o) is sitting upright on the couch, leaning forward with her hands on her knees. You can hear audible wheeze from the doorway. She looks at you but doesn't speak.

Facilitator Prompts:

  • "What do you see and hear from the doorway?"
  • "What severity category does this presentation suggest?"
  • "Any safety concerns in this environment?"
  • "What's your immediate priority?"

Expected AO Reasoning:

Tripod position, audible wheeze, not speaking = more severe asthma (talks in phrases/words or unable to speak). Need to get oxygen and nebuliser ready immediately. Scene safe — domestic setting, no hazards. Priority: Primary survey + severity classification.

Key Teaching Points:

  • Tripod position = respiratory distress — body positioning to maximise breathing
  • Not speaking on arrival is a red flag for severity
  • Audible wheeze indicates significant bronchospasm
Real-Job Translation: Your doorway impression often tells you more than 5 minutes of questions. A child who can't greet you is working hard to breathe.
Break Point 3 Practical Skill / Assessment

Initial Observations & Corpuls3 Setup

Scenario Progression:

You approach Lily. She looks at you with wide eyes. When you ask her name, she says "Li...ly" in two separate breaths. Mum says "she's been like this for about 20 minutes, her puffer isn't helping at all."

Facilitator Prompts:

  • "Driver — what observations are you getting and in what order?"
  • "Clinician — what history are you getting from Mum while obs are done?"
  • "What specific features are you looking for on assessment?"

Observations (Driver to obtain):

Corpuls3 Readings:
SpO₂: 91% (room air)
HR: 128 bpm
RR: 32/min (count manually to confirm)
Skin: Pale, using accessory muscles
Wheeze: Widespread bilateral on auscultation

History (Clinician to obtain):

  • Trigger: Playing outside, dust and pollen high today
  • Onset: Gradual over 1 hour, worsening rapidly last 20 mins
  • Reliever used: 4 puffs salbutamol MDI 20 mins ago — no improvement
  • PMHx: Asthma since age 3, previous hospital admission age 5
  • Medications: Salbutamol PRN, no preventer currently
  • Allergies: Nil known
  • Last ate: Lunch 2 hours ago

Expected AO Reasoning:

Speaking in single words, SpO₂ 91%, tachycardic, accessory muscle use = MORE SEVERE asthma. Not just mild. No urticaria/angioedema = asthma not anaphylaxis. Previous admission = higher risk patient. Need nebulised bronchodilators NOW.

Key Teaching Points:

  • SpO₂ < 95% on room air + difficulty speaking = more severe
  • Previous hospital admission indicates higher-risk asthmatic
  • No improvement with reliever = escalate treatment
  • Always exclude anaphylaxis features
Real-Job Translation: Your Corpuls3 confirms what your eyes told you at the door. SpO₂ alone never determines severity — it's the whole picture.

Standard MDI + Spacer Administration:

  • Shake MDI canister well (5 seconds)
  • Insert MDI into spacer
  • Patient places mouth around spacer mouthpiece (seal lips)
  • Actuate one puff into spacer
  • Patient takes 4 slow deep breaths (or 4 tidal breaths if child)
  • Wait 30 seconds between puffs
  • Repeat for prescribed number of puffs
Under 4 years: Consider adding a resuscitation mask to the spacer to improve medication delivery — creates better seal for small faces.
Break Point 4 Decision-Making

Screening Tools, Protocols & Working Diagnosis

Facilitator Prompts:

  • "Based on your assessment, what severity classification applies?"
  • "What protocol pathway are you following?"
  • "Have you ruled out anaphylaxis? How?"
  • "What treatment does the severity table direct you to?"

Expected AO Reasoning:

Working Diagnosis: More Severe Asthma (CPP-050-AO)
Rationale: Speaks in words only, SpO₂ 91%, distressed, accessory muscle use
Not anaphylaxis because: No urticaria, no angioedema, no known allergen exposure, gradual onset consistent with asthma exacerbation
Treatment indicated: Nebulised salbutamol 5mg + ipratropium 500 microg (patient is 8 y/o = >6 years)

Key Teaching Points:

  • Severity classification drives treatment selection
  • Always consider: "Could this be anaphylaxis?"
  • Document your reasoning for pathway selection
  • Age determines dosing (under 6 vs over 6)
Real-Job Translation: Your clinical reasoning must be clear enough to explain to the EOC Clinician when you call. "More severe asthma — speaking words only, sats 91%, I'm starting nebulised bronchodilators."
Break Point 5 Practical Skill

Treatment Initiation & Medication Safety

Scenario Progression:

You've classified Lily as MORE SEVERE asthma. Time to commence nebulised bronchodilator therapy.

Medication Dosing: More Severe Asthma (≥6 years)

Salbutamol
5mg nebule (5mg/2.5mL)
Use one full nebule
Ipratropium
500 microg nebule (500 microg/2mL)
Use one full nebule

Combined volume ≈ 4.5mL — no additional saline required for >6 years

Practical Skill: Nebuliser Setup & Administration

  • Check medications: Correct drug, correct dose, expiry date, patient age
  • Prepare nebuliser chamber: Open and add salbutamol 5mg nebule
  • Add ipratropium: 500 microg nebule to same chamber
  • Assemble: Connect nebuliser mask to chamber, connect to oxygen tubing
  • Oxygen flow: 6-8 L/min to drive nebuliser (mist should be visible)
  • Apply mask: Position comfortably on patient's face
  • Coach breathing: "Breathe normally through your mouth, Lily"
  • Monitor: Watch for improvement, observe for side effects
  • Duration: Until nebule is finished (approximately 10-15 minutes)

Facilitator Prompts:

  • "Talk me through your medication checks"
  • "What oxygen flow rate do you need to drive the neb?"
  • "What side effects might you see from these medications?"
  • "When would you call EOC Clinician for further doses?"

Expected Side Effects (counsel parent):

  • Salbutamol: Tremor, tachycardia, anxiety — usually mild, self-limiting
  • Ipratropium: Dry mouth — minimal systemic effects

Key Teaching Points:

  • Right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, right time
  • 6-8 L/min oxygen flow creates adequate nebulisation
  • Ipratropium added for synergistic bronchodilation in more severe asthma
  • Under 6 years: different doses + needs 3mL saline to make up volume
Medication Safety Check: Under 6 years dosing is DIFFERENT (Salbutamol 2.5mg + Ipratropium 250 microg + 3mL saline). Always confirm patient age before drawing up medications.
Real-Job Translation: Verbalise your medication checks out loud — "Salbutamol 5mg, checking expiry, patient is 8 years old." This becomes habit and catches errors.
Break Point 6 Decision-Making

Reassessment & Escalation Decision

Scenario Progression — Version A (Improving):

After 10 minutes of nebulised therapy, Lily is looking better. She says "I feel a bit better now" — a full short sentence. Wheeze is still present but reduced.

Repeat Observations:
SpO₂: 95% (on neb oxygen)
HR: 112 bpm
RR: 24/min
Work of breathing: Improved, less accessory muscle use

Facilitator Prompts (Improving):

  • "What's your reassessment telling you?"
  • "Is she back to 'mild' now?"
  • "Do you need to call EOC Clinician?"
  • "What's your transport decision?"

Expected AO Reasoning (Improving):

Improvement from words → short sentences, SpO₂ improved, HR decreasing, less work of breathing. Responding to treatment. Continue monitoring, complete neb, transport for medical review. Document improvement. EOC call not immediately required but should be considered for any concerns.

Scenario Progression — Version B (Deteriorating):

After 10 minutes of nebulised therapy, Lily is not improving. She's now barely responding to you, appears drowsy, and her wheeze has become quieter.

Repeat Observations:
SpO₂: 85% (on neb oxygen)
HR: 140 bpm
RR: 12/min (decreasing — ominous sign)
Work of breathing: Paradoxically reduced — exhaustion
Wheeze: Quiet/"silent chest"
RED FLAG: Quiet chest + drowsiness + falling RR = LIFE-THREATENING asthma. This is pre-respiratory arrest. Immediate escalation required.

Facilitator Prompts (Deteriorating):

  • "What does a 'quiet chest' mean in asthma?"
  • "Why is decreasing RR worrying here?"
  • "What severity is she now?"
  • "What are your immediate actions?"

Expected AO Reasoning (Deteriorating):

Severity change: More Severe → LIFE-THREATENING
Signs: Unable to speak, drowsy, poor respiratory effort, silent chest (not moving enough air to wheeze)
Immediate actions:
1. High-flow oxygen (switch from neb to NRB mask at 15L/min)
2. Call EOC Clinician immediately — request clinical support
3. Prepare BVM for assisted ventilation
4. Urgent transport — load and go
5. EOC may advise: Continuous nebs OR IM Adrenaline (10 microg/kg, max 500 microg)

Key Teaching Points:

  • Silent chest = not moving enough air to generate wheeze — WORSE not better
  • Falling respiratory rate in asthma = exhaustion/pre-arrest
  • Drowsiness = hypoxia affecting brain — time critical
  • LIFE-THREATENING requires immediate EOC escalation
Real-Job Translation: Don't be falsely reassured by a "quiet" chest. A child who stops wheezing and becomes sleepy is about to arrest. Call EOC NOW.

Clinical Knowledge: Ventilation in Severe Asthma

If assisted ventilation is required in life-threatening asthma, technique is critical to avoid worsening the situation.

BVM Ventilation in Severe Asthma — MUST be:
  • Small tidal volumes — just enough to see chest rise
  • Slow ventilation rate — approximately 4 per minute
  • Allow adequate chest deflation — the problem is air trapping
  • Gentle pressure — avoid over-pressurising lungs
Why slow and gentle? In severe asthma, air can get INTO the lungs but can't get OUT (bronchospasm/mucus). Fast ventilation causes air trapping, hyperinflation, and can cause pneumothorax or cardiovascular collapse. Let the chest deflate fully before each breath.

IM Adrenaline may be authorised by EOC Clinician for life-threatening asthma not responding to bronchodilators.

IM Adrenaline (EOC authorisation required)

Dose: 10 microg/kg
Maximum single dose: 500 microg
Preparation: Adrenaline 1:1000 (1mg/mL)
Route: IM (anterolateral thigh)
May repeat: PRN (as per EOC direction)

Rationale: Adrenaline causes bronchodilation via beta-2 receptors. Used when nebulised bronchodilators fail or in pre-arrest/arrest situations. Always via EOC consultation for AOs.
Break Point 7 Practical / Planning

Extrication & Transport Planning

Scenario Progression:

Lily has improved with the first nebuliser (Version A scenario). She's speaking in short sentences, SpO₂ 95%, work of breathing improved. Time to move to the ambulance and transport for further assessment.

Facilitator Prompts:

  • "How are you getting Lily to the ambulance?"
  • "What position for transport?"
  • "What monitoring continues en route?"
  • "Mum wants to come — what's your plan?"

Expected AO Reasoning:

Extrication: Green carry chair to stretcher — no walking (minimise exertion)
Position: Sitting upright (Fowler's position) — improves respiratory mechanics
Monitoring: Continuous SpO₂, reassess every 5 minutes, watch for deterioration
Mum: Travel in front with Driver — keeps Lily calm, Mum can provide history at hospital

Key Teaching Points:

  • Carry chair minimises patient exertion and oxygen demand
  • Upright positioning essential for respiratory patients
  • Parent in ambulance often beneficial for paediatric calm
  • Continue treatment en route if required — can repeat nebs per EOC
Real-Job Translation: The trip to hospital isn't "post-treatment" — it's ongoing care. Asthma can deteriorate rapidly. Keep monitoring, keep reassessing.
Break Point 8 Practical Skill

ISBAR Handover

Facilitator Prompts:

  • "Deliver your handover to the ED team using ISBAR"
  • "What are the critical points they need to know?"
  • "What observations are you handing over?"

Example ISBAR Handover:

I — Identify: "Hi, I'm [Name] from SA Ambulance. This is Lily, 8 years old."

S — Situation: "Lily has had an acute asthma exacerbation. She was initially classified as more severe asthma — speaking in words only, SpO₂ 91% on room air. She's responded well to treatment."

B — Background: "Known asthmatic since age 3, previous hospital admission at age 5. On PRN salbutamol only, no preventer. Triggered today by outdoor play — high pollen. Mum gave 4 puffs reliever at home with no improvement."

A — Assessment: "On arrival: SpO₂ 91%, HR 128, RR 32, widespread wheeze, accessory muscle use. We administered nebulised salbutamol 5mg and ipratropium 500 microg. Post-treatment: SpO₂ 95% on room air, HR 112, RR 24, speaking short sentences, wheeze reduced."

R — Recommendation: "She's improved but I'd recommend ongoing monitoring, consideration of steroids, and review of her asthma management plan given no preventer. Mum is here with her."

Key Teaching Points:

  • State severity classification AND what it was based on
  • Include what treatment was given and response
  • Previous admission history is relevant for risk stratification
  • Hand over concerns about current management (no preventer)
Real-Job Translation: A good handover paints a picture. The ED team should understand the trajectory — where she started, what you did, where she is now.

Take-Home Messages

✓

Severity classification drives treatment — use the SAAS Asthma Severity Table every time

✓

SpO₂ alone NEVER determines severity — assess speech, work of breathing, and overall presentation together

✓

Always consider: "Could this be anaphylaxis?" — if yes, adrenaline comes first

✓

Silent chest + drowsiness = pre-arrest — escalate immediately to EOC Clinician

✓

Nebuliser therapy for MORE SEVERE asthma — MDI + spacer is first-line for MILD only

✓

✓

Optional Scenario Variations

Setup: 45-year-old male, no known asthma, acute wheeze and dyspnoea after bushfire smoke exposure.

Key Learning: "Asthmatic symptoms may occur in patients with no history of asthma, especially in the setting of chest infection or smoke inhalation." Treat the presentation, not the history.

Considerations: Rule out cardiac cause (ACS can present with dyspnoea), consider smoke inhalation injury, follow asthma protocol if bronchospasm is primary issue.

Setup: 3-year-old with viral URTI, now wheezing and struggling to breathe. First presentation — no asthma diagnosis yet.

Key Learning: Different dosing for under 6 years. MDI + spacer with resuscitation mask for better seal. Salbutamol 2.5mg + Ipratropium 250 microg (+ 3mL saline) if nebulising.

Considerations: Viral-induced wheeze common in this age group, parental reassurance important, lower threshold for EOC consultation in very young patients.

Setup: 12-year-old at birthday party, eating cake, develops wheeze. Initially treated as asthma but then develops urticaria and facial swelling.

Key Learning: If severe asthma occurs in setting of anaphylaxis, follow anaphylaxis protocol FIRST (CPP-003-AO) — IM Adrenaline before bronchodilators.

Considerations: Recognise evolving anaphylaxis, don't delay adrenaline while giving bronchodilators, request early clinical support.

Setup: 10-year-old at remote campsite, 90-minute transport to hospital. Severe asthma, responds partially to initial neb.

Key Learning: Early EOC consultation essential. May need repeat nebs en route per EOC advice. Plan for deterioration during long transport.

Considerations: Oxygen supply planning, medication supply for repeated doses, rendezvous with backup if available, continuous monitoring even more critical.
