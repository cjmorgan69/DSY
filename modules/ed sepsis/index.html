Sepsis in the Rural ED

SA Health Adult Sepsis Pathway (December 2024) — Simulation Scenario

Section 1

"He's Not Right" — Urosepsis in a 72-Year-Old Farmer

SettingSmall rural SA hospital, Level 4 ED. 65 minutes by road from nearest regional centre.
TimeSunday, 0245 hrs
Staffing2 RNs + 1 Doctor (GP VMO) on call — doctor called in from home
ED Status1 other patient: 78F, stable Colles fracture, awaiting morning ortho review. Resting comfortably.
RetrievalMedSTAR — estimated response time 2–3 hours pending availability and weather
DiagnosticsECG, i-STAT (BGL, lactate, blood gas), urinalysis. No after-hours pathology. No CT. No ultrasound.

Patient arrives by private vehicle. His wife has driven him 25 km from their farm. She comes through the ED entrance supporting him by the arm. He is flushed, confused, and unsteady. She says: "He's not right — he's been crook for two days and now he's talking nonsense."

Section 2

Learning Objectives

  1. Apply the SA Health Adult Sepsis Pathway to recognise infection with organ dysfunction in a rural ED presentation.
  2. Assign an accurate ATS triage category and initiate time-critical interventions using HIRAID.
  3. Demonstrate the sepsis resuscitation bundle — Oxygen, IV Access & Blood Cultures, IV Antibiotics, IV Fluids — within 60 minutes.
  4. Identify Purple and Red Zone RDR observations and escalate appropriately in a resource-limited setting.
  5. Practise structured ISBAR communication for MedSTAR retrieval referral.
  6. Coordinate stabilisation, monitoring, and transfer preparation as a small rural team of 2 RNs and 1 Doctor.
  7. Recognise the emotional and operational complexity of caring for a known community member in a rural ED.

Patient Arrival

0245 Sunday. The ED is quiet. RN1 is at the nursing station. The front door opens. A woman in her late 60s is supporting a tall, thin man who is shuffling and leaning heavily on her arm. He is flushed, diaphoretic, and appears confused. She looks frightened.

"Please help — it's my husband Colin. He's been sick for a couple of days and tonight he started talking rubbish. I couldn't wake him properly. I didn't know what else to do so I drove him in."

Note for facilitator: The Marshes are known in town. Colin farms out near Pallamana. June volunteers at the hospital auxiliary. Staff may know them by name. Acknowledge this dynamic.

Triage Assessment — RN1

RN1 conducts rapid triage assessment:

  • Presenting complaint: 2-day history of increasing malaise, fever and rigors since yesterday, progressive confusion over past 6 hours, decreased urine output
  • Relevant history (from June): Colin had a TURP at the regional hospital 10 days ago. IDC removed 5 days ago at GP follow-up. Type 2 diabetes. Has been "off his food" since the procedure.
  • Appearance: Looks very unwell. Flushed. Diaphoretic. Confused — knows his name but not where he is. GCS approximately 14.
  • Quick observations (if obtainable at triage): HR rapid (palpable radial pulse weak and fast), skin hot and dry centrally, warm peripheries.

ATS Triage Category

ATS Category 2 — Emergency (within 10 minutes)

  • Very unwell appearance with altered conscious state
  • Haemodynamic compromise (tachycardia, weak pulse, flushed)
  • Clinical suspicion of sepsis — infection (recent urological procedure, fever, dysuria) with signs of organ dysfunction (confusion, haemodynamic instability)
  • Immediate allocation to resuscitation / acute bed

Immediate Actions

  • Direct patient to resuscitation bay — do not leave in waiting room
  • Call Doctor (GP VMO) — wake them up, they need to come in now
  • Activate RN2 if not already present
  • Begin primary survey and monitoring setup
  • Stay with the patient — June can provide history alongside care

Triage Decision

Facilitator Prompts

  • "Where does this patient go — waiting room, acute bay, or resus?"
  • "What are the immediate actions you need to take right now?"
  • "Who do you need to call and when?"

Expected Clinical Reasoning

  • RN1 — ATS 2 based on: very unwell appearance, altered conscious state, suspected infection with organ dysfunction. Not ATS 1 (not in cardiac/respiratory arrest or imminent threat to life at this moment) but close. If observations confirm Purple Zone, may escalate to MER equivalent.

  • Doctor called immediately — this is a time-critical presentation.

Key Teaching Points

  • At triage, you do not need a diagnosis. You need to recognise "this patient looks very unwell" and act.

  • ATS 2 means treatment within 10 minutes. The clock starts now.

Real-Job Translation

At 0245, you are likely the only RN awake. You need to simultaneously care for this patient, call the doctor, and get RN2 in. Prioritise: get the patient into the resus bay, call the doctor, then get help. If you cannot do all three at once, patient safety comes first — stay with the patient and call from the bedside phone.

  • Map to Sepsis Pathway RECOGNISE checklist: Looks very unwell, Fever/rigors, New confusion, Tachypnoea, Dysuria/oliguria, Recent procedure, IDC recently removed, Diabetes, Older age

  • A single Purple Zone observation or lactate ≥ 4 mmol/L = MER activation and probable sepsis pathway.
  • Two or more Red Zone observations or lactate ≥ 2 mmol/L = Senior Medical Officer review and possible sepsis pathway.

  • The warm, vasodilated peripheries with low blood pressure = distributive (warm) shock — classic early septic shock.

Real-Job Translation

In a small rural ED at 0300, there is no MER team. You are the MER team. The Purple Zone trigger means you escalate to the most senior clinician available — your GP VMO, who you've already called. If they're not yet on site, you're initiating the sepsis bundle now. Do not wait for the doctor to start oxygen, IV access, and monitoring.

Section 8

Clinical Decision Point

Working Diagnosis

Urosepsis with organ dysfunction — Probable Sepsis, high risk of Septic Shock

Source: Urinary tract, post-TURP with recent IDC removal. Organ dysfunction evidenced by: hypotension, altered mental state, acute kidney injury, raised lactate, metabolic acidosis.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Urosepsis — most likely given the clinical picture
  • DKA — BGL elevated but no ketonaemia, pH only mildly acidotic, lactate more consistent with sepsis
  • Acute urinary retention with obstructive uropathy — possible contributing factor, assess with IDC insertion
  • Post-operative complication (wound infection, abscess) — no external wound from TURP but consider
  • Other infection source — chest (basal creps — aspiration? HAP?), skin, intra-abdominal

Escalation Decision

This patient cannot be managed locally.

  • Probable sepsis with septic shock physiology (hypotension, organ dysfunction, lactate 3.8)
  • AKI (creatinine doubled from baseline) — needs close monitoring and possible renal support
  • Hyperkalaemia (K+ 5.1) — needs cardiac monitoring and may need treatment
  • May require vasopressors if fluid resuscitation does not restore haemodynamic stability
  • Needs ICU-capable facility for definitive management

Actions:

  1. Commence SA Health Sepsis Pathway resuscitation bundle — Oxygen, IV Access & Blood Cultures, Antibiotics, Fluids — WITHIN 60 MINUTES
  2. Contact MedSTAR (13 78 27) for early consultation and retrieval coordination — call early, do not wait until you have "done everything"
  3. Stabilise and prepare for transfer to regional centre ICU

Section 9

Treatment & Stabilisation Phase

WITHIN 60 MINUTES Complete actions 1–4 of the SA Health Adult Sepsis Pathway with ongoing systematic assessment.

1. OXYGEN

RN1 Commence oxygen immediately.

  • Target SpO2 ≥ 95% (this patient is not COPD)
  • Start with nasal prongs 4 L/min, titrate up. If SpO2 remains < 95%, escalate to Hudson mask 6–8 L/min.
  • Document oxygen delivery device, flow rate, and SpO2 response.

2. IV ACCESS & BLOOD CULTURES

RN2 Vascular access and bloods.

  • Insert 2 x large-bore IV cannulae (16G or 18G) — two sites required for blood cultures AND fluid resuscitation.
  • Blood cultures x 2 sets from 2 separate peripheral sites. Collect BEFORE antibiotics if possible — but do not delay antibiotics beyond 60 minutes to obtain cultures.
  • Collect bloods: BGL, Lactate (if not already done on i-STAT), FBC, EUC, LFTs, CRP, Coagulation studies — label, hold for transport with patient (no after-hours lab).
  • Aseptic technique for all blood collection. Each culture set = 1 aerobic + 1 anaerobic bottle.

3. IV ANTIBIOTICS

DOCTOR prescribes. RN2 prepares and administers.

  • Commence within 60 minutes of identifying sepsis.
  • Refer to Therapeutic Guidelines: Antibiotic or local guidelines for empiric urosepsis regimen.
  • Source of infection documented: urinary tract — post-TURP, recent IDC removal.
  • Check allergies: Sulfonamides (rash) — document. This does not preclude most first-line urosepsis antibiotics but must be checked against specific agents prescribed.
  • Note: AKI present (creatinine 210, baseline 120) — dose adjustment may be required for renally cleared agents. Discuss with MedSTAR or ID physician if uncertain.
  • Standard medication safety checks: Right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, right time. Independent double-check for IV antibiotics (NSQHS Standard 4).

4. IV FLUIDS

RN2 / RN1

  • Crystalloid 500 mL bolus STAT — sodium chloride 0.9% or Hartmann's solution.
  • Use pressure bag for rapid infusion if required.
  • Document fluid input/output — commence fluid balance chart.
  • Consider IDC insertion for hourly urine output monitoring (also therapeutic — assess for urinary retention).
  • Repeat bolus if ongoing hypotension (SBP < 90 or MAP < 65).
  • Caution: Colin has mild CKD and is 72 years old. Monitor closely for signs of fluid overload (rising RR, creps, JVP, SpO2 drop). The pathway states: "Closely monitor patients with cardiac or renal dysfunction when repeating fluid boluses."

Additional Interventions

  • IDC insertion — RN2 — for hourly urine output monitoring. Note: post-TURP — use caution, consider smaller catheter. On insertion, record volume drained (if large volume = possible retention contributing to sepsis source).
  • Withhold Metformin — risk of lactic acidosis in AKI and sepsis. DOCTOR to document.
  • Withhold Perindopril & Amlodipine — patient is hypotensive.
  • BGL monitoring — Gliclazide on board and patient not eating. Risk of hypoglycaemia. Recheck BGL hourly.
  • ECG monitoring — continuous, given hyperkalaemia (K+ 5.1), tachycardia, and fluid resuscitation.
  • Temperature management — paracetamol for comfort if temp remains > 38.5 C.
Break Point 5 Practical Skill & Medication Safety

Treatment Initiation & Medication Safety

Facilitator Prompts

  • "RN2 — talk me through preparing and checking the IV antibiotic. What safety steps are you performing?"
  • "How are you going to get two sets of blood cultures from two separate sites? What's your aseptic technique?"
  • "The first 500 mL bolus has gone in. BP is still 90/54. What do you do?"
  • "Colin's creatinine is double his baseline. What does this mean for antibiotic dosing and fluid management?"
  • "Who is doing what? RN1, RN2, Doctor — what are your tasks right now?"

Skills Practice — Demonstrate These

  • IV cannulation x 2 — large bore, two sites. In a dehydrated, septic patient — consider antecubital fossa, external jugular if peripheral access is difficult.
  • Blood culture collection — aseptic technique, correct volume in each bottle (8–10 mL per bottle in adults), 2 sets from 2 separate sites.
  • IV antibiotic preparation — reconstitution, dilution, rate of administration, independent double-check, documentation on medication chart.
  • Rapid IV fluid administration — 500 mL bolus using pressure bag. Reassess after each bolus.
  • IDC insertion — aseptic technique, measure residual volume, connect to hourly drainage bag.

Key Teaching Points

  • Blood cultures before antibiotics — but antibiotics within 60 minutes. If you cannot get cultures, give the antibiotics anyway. Treatment trumps diagnostics.
  • In a rural ED with no after-hours lab, collected bloods travel with the patient. Label correctly, store appropriately (blood cultures at room temperature, not refrigerated).
  • Medication safety in a crisis: it is tempting to rush. Slow down enough to check: right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, right time. One error can compound an already critical situation.
  • Task allocation: RN1 manages monitoring, observations, history documentation, and family liaison. RN2 manages IV access, bloods, medications, fluids, IDC. Doctor assesses, prescribes, and prepares for MedSTAR call. Clear roles prevent duplication and missed tasks.

Real-Job Translation

Two RNs and one doctor managing septic shock. There is no spare staff. If you need to send someone to get the antibiotic from imprest, someone else needs to stay with the patient. Closed-loop communication: "I'm going to get the gentamicin from the drug cupboard — RN1, can you watch the monitor while I'm gone?" "Yes, I've got it." This is real teamwork in a resource-limited setting.

  • "What if MedSTAR says the retrieval team is 3 hours away? What do you do in the meantime?"

Expected Clinical Reasoning

  • DOCTOR — Partial response. BP has improved from 88/52 to 96/60 but remains fragile. Lactate trending down but still well above 2 mmol/L. Ongoing confusion and oliguria indicate persistent organ dysfunction. This patient is not out of danger and needs escalation.
  • RN1 — Reassessment at structured intervals (every 15 min for septic shock). Document trend. Prepare ISBAR data for the doctor. Monitor urine output hourly. Repeat lactate within 2 hours of initial.
  • The pathway REASSESS step reminds us: "For persistent signs of sepsis, escalate to senior consultant, consider source control."

Key Teaching Points

  • Reassessment is not optional — it is a defined step in HIRAID and in the Sepsis Pathway (Steps 5 and 7).
  • Lactate clearance is a key marker. The pathway says: "Repeat lactate within 2 hours if initially elevated — aim < 2 mmol/L." If lactate is not clearing, the patient is not responding adequately.
  • Urine output < 0.5 mL/kg/hr is a medical officer review trigger. With an IDC in place, you can monitor this precisely.
  • The ISBAR retrieval referral is a structured, practised communication. It should be concise, complete, and convey urgency without panic.

Real-Job Translation

MedSTAR may take 2–3 hours. You are managing this patient for the duration. The reassessment loop is your lifeline — it tells you whether you need to do more (another bolus, escalate to vasopressors if you have capability, re-call MedSTAR for urgent advice). If the patient deteriorates, use PACE (Probe, Alert, Challenge, Emergency) to assertively escalate with the retrieval service. You are this patient's advocate.

Section 11

Preparation for Transfer / Retrieval

Packaging the Patient

  • Continuous cardiac monitoring — ensure portable monitor available for transfer
  • IV access — confirm both cannulae patent and flushed, secure with additional taping for transport
  • IV fluids — ensure adequate supply prepared for transfer period
  • Oxygen — ensure adequate cylinder supply for transfer duration (calculate requirement: flow rate x estimated time x 1.5 safety factor)
  • IDC — secure, drainage bag below bladder level, document output
  • Warming — patient may become hypothermic during transfer. Blankets, warm IV fluids if available
  • Medications — ensure any time-critical medications due during transfer are prepared and documented

Transfer Documentation

  • Complete clinical handover document — diagnosis, treatment given, response, outstanding results
  • Observation chart with all timed observations
  • Medication chart with all medications administered (times, doses, routes)
  • Fluid balance chart
  • Blood specimens — labelled and stored correctly, travelling with patient (blood cultures at room temperature)
  • Urine specimen collected
  • Copies of ECG
  • Patient identification — MRN, Medicare, NOK details
  • Advance Care Directive — nil in this case, document discussion with June

Ongoing Monitoring While Awaiting Retrieval

  • Observations every 15 minutes (HR, BP, RR, SpO2, GCS)
  • Urine output hourly
  • Repeat lactate at 2-hour mark
  • BGL hourly (Gliclazide on board, nil oral intake)
  • Reassess response to each fluid bolus
  • Monitor for fluid overload (lung auscultation, SpO2, RR, JVP)
  • Monitor ECG for arrhythmia (hyperkalaemia)

Contingency Planning

  • If retrieval delayed > 2 hours: Continue reassessment loop. Consider third fluid bolus if still hypotensive (with caution re CKD). Re-contact MedSTAR for ongoing management advice.
  • If patient deteriorates: ABCDE reassessment. Consider need for vasopressor support (if syringe driver and noradrenaline available — MedSTAR can guide dosing via phone). Prepare for possible intubation if GCS drops. Re-call MedSTAR with updated ISBAR — escalate urgency.
  • If cardiac arrest: ALS protocol. Continue resuscitation. Notify MedSTAR immediately.

Family Communication

RN1 or DOCTOR — June is in the department. She is frightened. She knows the staff.

  • Explain what is happening in plain language: "Colin has a serious infection that has spread to his bloodstream. His body is struggling to cope. We are giving him antibiotics, fluids, and oxygen. We need to transfer him to [Regional Centre] where they have an intensive care unit."
  • Provide realistic information about retrieval timeline: "The retrieval team is on their way but it may be 2–3 hours. We will look after Colin here until they arrive."
  • Ask about Advance Care Directive or goals of care wishes — sensitively and without causing alarm.
  • Offer practical support: Does June need to call family? Does she need tea? Is she safe to drive later?
  • Acknowledge the relationship: "We know Colin and we are going to do everything we can for him."
Break Point 7 Discussion & Practical

Transfer Preparation & Packaging

Facilitator Prompts

  • "MedSTAR is 2.5 hours away. What monitoring and reassessment are you doing in the meantime?"
  • "What documentation needs to go with this patient?"
  • "The blood cultures — where are they stored? Can they go in the fridge?"
  • "June is crying and asking if Colin is going to die. Who talks to her, and what do you say?"
  • "What if Colin's BP drops to 82/48 while you're waiting for retrieval? What's your plan?"

Expected Clinical Reasoning

  • RN1 / RN2 — Structured ongoing monitoring every 15 minutes. Fluid balance documented. Urine output tracked hourly. Repeat lactate at 2-hour mark. All documentation prepared and organised for handover.
  • DOCTOR — Contingency planning for deterioration. Know what vasopressor support is available (if any). Know when to re-call MedSTAR. Have a plan for airway management if needed.
  • Blood cultures are stored at room temperature — not refrigerated. This is a common error.

Key Teaching Points

  • The wait for retrieval is often the most dangerous period. The patient is unstable, the definitive care is hours away, and your resources are limited. This is where structured reassessment and contingency planning save lives.
  • Family communication is part of clinical care, not an afterthought. In a small town, June knows the staff. This can be both a comfort and an additional emotional burden for the team.
  • Practical transfer preparation: ensure adequate oxygen supply, secured IV access, medications prepared, documentation complete. Poor preparation leads to poor transfers.

Real-Job Translation

At 0400 in a rural ED, waiting for retrieval with a septic patient, a distressed wife, and one other patient in the department — this is the reality of rural emergency nursing. You cannot hand off. You cannot call for backup. You manage the clinical care, the emotional care, and the logistics. Debrief after. Look after each other.

Section 12

Handover to Retrieval Team

MedSTAR retrieval team arrives at approximately 0530. DOCTOR delivers ISBAR handover at the bedside. RN1 provides nursing handover to retrieval nurse/paramedic.

I — Identify
"Colin Marsh, 72-year-old male, MRN [number]. I'm Dr [Name], GP VMO. This is RN [Name], primary nurse. Next of kin is his wife June, who is here with us."
S — Situation
"Colin presented at 0245 this morning with probable urosepsis and septic shock. He is 10 days post-TURP, IDC removed 5 days ago. He was brought in by his wife with 2-day history of increasing malaise, fever, rigors, and progressive confusion. He was hypotensive on arrival — BP 88/52 — with multiple Red and Purple Zone observations."
B — Background
"PMHx: Type 2 diabetes on Metformin and Gliclazide, BPH post-TURP, hypertension on Perindopril and Amlodipine, mild CKD baseline creatinine 120. Allergy to Sulfonamides — rash. All regular medications withheld tonight. He was functionally independent at baseline — his wife confirms he was cognitively normal 48 hours ago. No Advance Care Directive."
A — Assessment
"Current observations at 0515: HR 102, BP 98/62, RR 20, SpO2 97% on 6L Hudson mask, Temp 38.4, GCS 14 — still confused, oriented to name only. BGL 11.8 — trending down. Urine output 25 mL/hr — still below target.

Initial lactate 3.8, repeat at 0430 was 3.2 — trending down but still above 2. Creatinine 210, baseline 120 — AKI. Potassium 5.1. VBG pH 7.29 with metabolic acidosis. ECG shows sinus tachycardia, no acute ST changes. Urinalysis: nitrites, leucocytes, blood all positive.

Treatment given: Oxygen commenced at 0300. Two large-bore IV cannulae placed. Blood cultures x 2 sets collected at 0305 from two peripheral sites — stored at room temperature, travelling with patient. IV antibiotics commenced at 0320 — [name agent and dose as prescribed]. Total IV fluid: 1500 mL NaCl 0.9% (3 x 500 mL boluses). IDC inserted at 0325 — drained 150 mL initially, subsequent output 20–25 mL/hr. Paracetamol 1 g IV given at 0400."
R — Recommendation
"Colin needs ongoing ICU-level monitoring, repeat bloods with formal laboratory pathology, consideration for vasopressor support if BP does not continue to improve, and nephrology input for his AKI. Outstanding: formal blood results not yet available — specimens are labelled and ready. Blood cultures are with the patient. Source control may need imaging at the receiving facility — we have no CT here. June will need to follow by road — she is aware and has family she can contact. Any questions?"
Break Point 8 Communication Skill

ISBAR Handover Practice

Facilitator Prompts

  • "Doctor — deliver the full ISBAR handover to the retrieval team. I'll play the retrieval consultant."
  • "RN1 — what nursing handover do you provide to the retrieval nurse? What specific information do they need from you?"
  • "Was any information missed? What would you add?"
  • "How do you ensure continuity of care during the transfer?"

Expected Clinical Reasoning

  • DOCTOR — Structured, concise, complete ISBAR. Covers identity, clinical situation, relevant background, current assessment with all observations and trends, treatment given with times, and clear recommendations with outstanding issues.
  • RN1 — Nursing handover includes: IV access (sites, gauge, patency), fluid balance (total in, total out), medication chart (all medications given with times), observation trend chart, IDC (size, time inserted, output), BGL trend, oxygen requirement, patient's baseline function and social situation, family status and emotional state.
  • Handover should happen at the bedside so retrieval team can simultaneously assess the patient.

Key Teaching Points

  • A good ISBAR handover is practised, not improvised. In a crisis, you fall to the level of your preparation.
  • Include what you have done AND what still needs to happen. Outstanding results, pending decisions, and ongoing monitoring needs are all part of the handover.
  • The retrieval team was not there at the beginning. They need the whole story, concisely told. Do not assume they know anything.
  • Documentation supports handover. If the notes are incomplete, the handover will be incomplete.

Real-Job Translation

When the retrieval team walks in, you've been managing this patient for 2–3 hours. You're tired, you've been running. The handover is the last critical task before you hand over care. Take 30 seconds to organise your thoughts, use the ISBAR structure, and deliver it clearly. A poor handover can undo good clinical work.

Section 13

Debrief (10–15 minutes)

Structured Debrief — Plus / Delta Model

Opening

"Thank you, everyone. That was a challenging scenario. Let's take a few minutes to debrief. This is a safe space — no judgement. We're here to learn together."

Emotional Check-In

"Before we talk about clinical care — how are you feeling? That was a high-acuity patient, someone known to the community. It's okay if that felt heavy."

  • Acknowledge the emotional weight of caring for known community members
  • Normalise stress responses
  • Remind staff of Employee Assistance Program and peer support options

Review of Learning Objectives

  1. Did we recognise sepsis using the SA Health pathway? When did we identify it?
  2. Did we assign an appropriate triage category and initiate HIRAID?
  3. Did we complete the sepsis bundle within 60 minutes? (Oxygen, IV/Blood Cultures, Antibiotics, Fluids)
  4. Did we identify Purple and Red Zone observations and escalate correctly?
  5. Did we deliver an effective ISBAR to MedSTAR?
  6. Did we coordinate effectively as a team of 2 RNs and 1 Doctor?

Plus (What Went Well)

  • Prompt triage and bed allocation?
  • Early recognition of sepsis using the pathway?
  • Team communication and task allocation?
  • Timely antibiotic and fluid administration?
  • Structured MedSTAR referral?
  • Family communication?

Delta (What Would We Do Differently)

  • Could we have started oxygen sooner?
  • Did we call MedSTAR early enough?
  • Were there any medication safety concerns?
  • Was documentation maintained in real time?
  • Did we reassess frequently enough?
  • Were there communication gaps between team members?

System Issues

  • Was the sepsis pathway easily accessible?
  • Were blood culture bottles and antibiotic stock readily available?
  • Could the i-STAT provide what we needed?
  • Is our local MedSTAR escalation process clear to all staff?
  • Do we have a transfer documentation template that supports comprehensive handover?

Key Take-Home Points

  1. Sepsis is time-critical. The SA Health pathway gives you a structured approach: RECOGNISE, RESPOND & ESCALATE, RESUSCITATE, REFER & REASSESS.
  2. Complete actions 1–4 within 60 minutes: Oxygen, IV Access & Blood Cultures, IV Antibiotics, IV Fluids.
  3. Call MedSTAR early — they are a resource, not a last resort. Regional LHNs: commence treatment and follow local escalation pathways.
  4. Reassess, reassess, reassess. Repeat lactate within 2 hours. Monitor urine output. Trends matter more than single readings.
  5. You are it. In a small rural ED, you are the resuscitation team, the ICU nurse, the pharmacist, and the social worker. Work to your scope. Support each other. Debrief afterwards.
Section 14

Scenario Variations

Variation 1 — Deterioration Before Retrieval

At 0430, Colin's BP drops to 78/44. GCS drops to 12 (E3 V4 M5). Lactate repeat is 4.6 mmol/L — rising. He is becoming peripherally cool and mottled (transition from warm to cold shock). The retrieval team is still 90 minutes away.

Discussion focus: Vasopressor initiation via syringe driver under MedSTAR phone guidance. Airway planning if GCS continues to drop. Re-calling MedSTAR with updated ISBAR to escalate urgency. Managing two RNs' workload when one patient requires near-continuous intervention.

Variation 2 — No Point-of-Care Lactate Available

The i-STAT is out of cartridges for lactate. You cannot measure lactate. How do you assess and monitor for sepsis severity and treatment response without it?

Discussion focus: Clinical assessment of perfusion — CRT, mental state, urine output, BP trend, mottling score. The lactate is a useful tool but not the only one. The SA Health pathway includes clinical signs of organ dysfunction (hypotension, tachypnoea, altered mental state) as triggers independent of lactate.

Variation 3 — Second Patient Arrives

At 0345, a 55-year-old male walks in clutching his chest. He has central chest pain, diaphoresis, and looks pale. Now you have a potential ACS and a septic shock patient simultaneously, with 2 RNs and 1 Doctor.

Discussion focus: Triage prioritisation with two acute patients. Task allocation. Which patient gets the doctor first? Can RN1 manage the sepsis patient independently while RN2 triages the chest pain? When do you call for additional staff? Is there a nurse manager or on-call coordinator?
