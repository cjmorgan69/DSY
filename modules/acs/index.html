Learning Objectives

On completion, participants will be able to:

  • Allocate ATS Category 2 for suspected ACS and articulate the rationale
  • Initiate care under SA Health Standing Medication Orders (aspirin, GTN) before MO review
  • Perform a structured HIRAID assessment identifying cardiac red flags
  • Obtain a 12-lead ECG within 10 minutes and upload to iPOCCS/MUSE
  • Administer aspirin 300mg and GTN per SMO, applying medication safety checks
  • Recognise STEMI (ACOMI) criteria and initiate time-critical escalation via iCARnet / MedSTAR
  • Use SA Health RDR observation charts to detect and escalate deterioration
  • Deliver a structured ISBAR handover to the retrieval team

Pre-Brief

Facilitator — Set the Scene (10 minutes)

This is a walk-through scenario. We pause at break points for discussion, skill practice, and reflection. The goal is to rehearse real clinical workflow in a small South Australian rural ED.

Assign Roles

  • Triage Nurse (TN): Receives patient. Allocates ATS. Initiates SMO medications (aspirin, GTN). First observations. Documents on RDR chart.
  • Bedside Nurse (BN): HIRAID assessment. 12-lead ECG acquisition and iPOCCS/MUSE upload. Medication administration. Ongoing monitoring. ISBAR to MO.
  • Medical Officer (MO): Medical review. ECG interpretation. Diagnosis. iCARnet/MedSTAR contact. Thrombolysis decision. Disposition.
SA Context

In a small SA rural ED, the MO may be on-call from home (20 minutes away) or managing another patient. SA Health Standing Medication Orders authorise nursing staff to administer aspirin and GTN for suspected cardiac chest pain without waiting for a prescription. This is not optional — this is what keeps the patient alive while the MO is en route.

SA Frameworks Orientation

  • SMOs: Aspirin 300mg chewed + GTN 400mcg SL — nurse-initiated for suspected cardiac chest pain
  • iCCnet Pathway: 12-lead ECG within 10 minutes, interpreted by MO or transmitted for remote cardiology review via iPOCCS/MUSE
  • RDR Chart: All observations plotted on SA Health RDR observation chart — colour-coded escalation zones
  • MedSTAR: 13 78 27 — call early for any STEMI or haemodynamically unstable ACS

Presentation Phase — How the Patient Arrives

Scenario — Read to Participants

It is 1845 on a Tuesday evening in late autumn. You are working the evening shift at a small rural SA hospital — approximately 55 minutes by road from the nearest regional hospital with a cardiac catheterisation laboratory.

The MO has just stepped out to the doctors' quarters to make a phone call. The triage nurse is at the desk. The bedside nurse is restocking the resuscitation bay.

A dusty ute pulls into the ambulance bay. A woman rushes in:

"My husband — he's got terrible chest pain. It started about 45 minutes ago loading hay bales. He's still in the car. He doesn't look right."

Triage Vignette

The triage nurse goes to the car. Graham, 58, farmer, is pale, diaphoretic, gripping his sternum. Speaking in short sentences.

  • Alert, orientated, GCS 15
  • "Crushing" central chest pain, 9/10, left arm and jaw radiation
  • Onset ~45 minutes ago during exertion
  • Associated nausea, diaphoresis
  • No relief with rest
  • "Takes blood pressure and cholesterol tablets"
Break Point 1Decision-Making

Triage and ATS Allocation

Facilitator Prompts

  • What ATS category? Rationale?
  • Waiting room, acute bed, or resus bay?
  • What SA Health Standing Medication Orders are you activating?
  • What does the triage nurse initiate before the MO is even aware?
  • The MO is in the doctors' quarters. Do you call them back now?

Expected Clinical Reasoning

  • ATS Category 2 — all chest pain suggestive of ACS = mandatory ATS 2 (medical assessment within 10 minutes)
  • Location: Direct to resus bay — not the waiting room. Monitored bed. Defibrillator nearby.
  • SMO Activation: Aspirin 300mg chewed + GTN 400mcg SL (after checking SBP >100, HR 60–120, no PDE-5 inhibitors)
  • Call the MO: Yes — brief direct message: "58M, crushing chest pain, diaphoretic, going to resus, SMO activated. Need you back now."

Key Teaching Points

  • SA Health SMOs are the mechanism that bridges the gap when the MO is unavailable — they exist for exactly this situation
  • The 10-minute ECG target starts at first clinical contact (triage), not when the patient reaches a bed
  • ATS 2 drives urgency — it means "this patient needs a doctor within 10 minutes"
  • Get the patient horizontal, monitored, with a defibrillator in the room. This patient is at risk of VF arrest.

Real-Shift Translation

At 1845, the MO might be in the quarters, on the phone, or finishing a discharge letter. The triage nurse's confidence to activate SMOs and initiate care is what buys the 10 minutes until the MO arrives at bedside. That is the whole point of Standing Medication Orders.

Triage and Initial Nursing Assessment

Triage Nurse Actions (Minutes 0–5)

  • Allocate ATS Category 2 — document rationale
  • Activate SMO: Aspirin 300mg and GTN 400mcg SL
  • Minimise patient exertion — wheelchair or ED trolley to resus bay
  • Commence RDR observation chart — first set of obs
  • Brief verbal handover to bedside nurse
  • Notify MO — direct, brief communication
  • Return to triage or assist in resus
Break Point 2Discussion

First Impression and Acuity Recognition

Facilitator Prompts

  • What was your gut feeling when you first saw Graham in the car?
  • What visual cues indicate high acuity before you even touch him?
  • Does the HIRAID assessment proceed while the MO is being called back?

Expected Clinical Reasoning

  • General appearance: Pale, diaphoretic, clutching chest = acute cardiac event until proven otherwise
  • Circulation to skin: Pale and sweaty = sympathetic response — pain, fear, or haemodynamic compromise
  • Decision: HIRAID assessment proceeds immediately. Do not wait for the MO to start nursing assessment and SMO medications.

Key Teaching Points

  • The 5-second doorway assessment sets your urgency. Diaphoresis + chest pain = ACS until proven otherwise.
  • In a 3-person SA rural ED, parallel processing is essential — the nurse assesses and treats while the MO is notified and attends
  • Trust your clinical instinct, then use HIRAID to give it structure

Real-Shift Translation

On a real shift, if the patient looks sick from the car park, they are sick. Act on that. HIRAID gives you the framework to be systematic under pressure.

SA Health Standing Medication Orders

SMO Nurse-Initiated Interventions

  • Continuous cardiac monitoring — 3-lead attached, defibrillator pads on standby
  • IV access — 18G or larger, antecubital fossa. Flush with 0.9% NaCl.
  • Aspirin 300mg (SMO) — non-enteric-coated, chew and swallow
  • GTN 400mcg SL (SMO) — Nitrolingual spray. Confirm: SBP >100, HR 60–120, no PDE-5 inhibitor use
  • Repeat GTN every 5 min if pain persists and obs remain within SMO parameters
  • O2 only if SpO2 <94% — target >94% (or >88% if COPD). Do not routinely apply.
  • Position semi-recumbent (30–45 degrees), minimise exertion
  • Plot all obs on RDR chart
GTN Contraindications (SA Health SMO): SBP <100mmHg. HR <60 or >120. PDE-5 inhibitors within 24hr (sildenafil, vardenafil, avanafil) or 48hr (tadalafil). Suspected right ventricular infarction. No maximum dose limit while observations remain within parameters. Lay patient supine if hypotension occurs after GTN.
Inferior STEMI caution: GTN use requires extra vigilance in inferior STEMI — right ventricular involvement makes these patients preload-dependent. Watch for BP drop. Consider V4R (right-sided ECG) to assess RV involvement before repeat GTN dosing.

Investigations

  • 12-lead ECG — completed. Upload to iPOCCS/MUSE. Repeat at 15-minute intervals if pain persists.
  • RTropT (Point-of-care) — if iCCnet PoCT available. In confirmed STEMI, do not delay treatment for troponin.
  • Pathology: FBE, EUC, LFT, GLU, INR (if indicated), Troponin (send-away)
  • BGL: 7.2 (noted above)
  • CXR: Consider if differential includes dissection, pneumothorax, pulmonary oedema. Do not delay treatment for CXR in confirmed STEMI. Nurse-initiated X-ray if credentialed.
  • V4R (right-sided ECG): Perform in inferior STEMI to assess RV involvement — this changes GTN safety and fluid management.
iPOCCS / MUSE — SA Health ECG Transmission

Upload 12-lead ECG to iPOCCS/MUSE via the SA Health network for remote cardiology review. If the system is down or unavailable: photograph the ECG, call iCARnet (08 8378 1133), and provide a verbal description. Have a backup plan — systems fail at 1900 on a Tuesday.

Skills Practice — 12-Lead ECG Acquisition

Practise: Correct Lead Placement

  • V1: 4th ICS, right sternal border
  • V2: 4th ICS, left sternal border
  • V3: Midway between V2 and V4
  • V4: 5th ICS, mid-clavicular line
  • V5: Anterior axillary line, level with V4
  • V6: Mid-axillary line, level with V4/V5
  • V4R: Right 5th ICS, mid-clavicular line — for RV assessment in inferior STEMI
Correct placement matters. Misplaced V1/V2 can mimic or mask ST changes. In a diaphoretic patient, dry the skin first. Time target: ECG acquired, interpreted, and uploaded within 10 minutes of first clinical contact.

Key Teaching Points

  • HIRAID gives structure under pressure — it stops you forgetting the PDE-5 question or the V4R
  • The SA Health SMO for GTN has specific parameter checks that must be confirmed and documented each time
  • iPOCCS/MUSE is your ECG lifeline to remote cardiology — know how to use it and have a backup
  • The nurse's ISBAR to MO included a clear recommendation — "I need you now" — that is not overstepping, that is the job

Real-Shift Translation

The 12-lead ECG is the single most important investigation. In a rural SA ED, that ECG drives everything — the MedSTAR call, the thrombolysis decision, the transfer urgency. Get it right, get it fast, get it uploaded.

Medical Officer Review

MO Actions on Arrival at Bedside

  • Receives ISBAR from bedside nurse
  • Reviews 12-lead ECG — confirms acute inferior STEMI (ACOMI)
  • Focused history and examination (confirm nursing findings, assess for differentials)
  • Checks for contraindications to thrombolysis
  • Calls iCARnet (08 8378 1133) and MedSTAR (13 78 27) for a conference call

Working Diagnosis

Acute Inferior STEMI (ACOMI)
Time from symptom onset: ~60 minutes at this point.
Time-critical. Emergent reperfusion required.

MO Orders (in consultation with iCARnet / MedSTAR)

  • Morphine 2.5–5mg IV titrated (with metoclopramide 10mg IV)
  • Enoxaparin 1mg/kg SC
  • Prepare tenecteplase if PCI cannot be achieved within 120 min of first medical contact
  • Repeat ECG at 15-minute intervals while pain persists
  • Defibrillator pads applied — ready for immediate use
  • Repeat ECG at 60–90 min post-thrombolysis if administered
SA-Specific — Tenecteplase Supply

Due to the global tenecteplase shortage, SA Health prioritises supply to non-metropolitan and remote sites for STEMI thrombolysis. Confirm your site has stock and staff are familiar with weight-based dosing. If your site does not hold tenecteplase, the MedSTAR retrieval team may carry it — discuss during the conference call.

Break Point 4Decision-Making

MO Review, Diagnosis, and Reperfusion Decision

Facilitator Prompts

  • The MO confirms STEMI. What is the reperfusion decision tree?
  • Our nearest PCI facility is 55 min by road. MedSTAR helicopter ETA ~40 min. Can PCI be achieved within 120 minutes?
  • If not — what is the alternative? What is the time target?
  • Who makes the thrombolysis decision? Does the MO decide alone?
  • What are the absolute contraindications to thrombolysis?

Expected Clinical Reasoning

  • Reperfusion: Primary PCI if achievable within 120 min. If not, fibrinolysis within 30 min.
  • Time calculation: First medical contact ~10 min ago. Road transfer 55 min + cath lab activation = likely >120 min. Helicopter 40 min + activation — borderline. Discuss with iCARnet/MedSTAR in conference call.
  • Conference call model: The rural MO calls MedSTAR (13 78 27) and iCARnet (08 8378 1133) simultaneously. The decision is made collaboratively — cardiologist advises on reperfusion, MedSTAR coordinates retrieval.
  • Absolute contraindications to thrombolysis: Active internal bleeding, suspected aortic dissection, ischaemic stroke within 3 months, intracranial neoplasm, significant head trauma within 3 months.

Key Teaching Points

  • In rural SA, thrombolysis followed by transfer for catheterisation is the most common STEMI pathway
  • The iCARnet/MedSTAR conference call is the cornerstone of rural cardiac care in SA — the MO is not making this decision alone
  • Time is myocardium — every 30-minute delay in reperfusion increases mortality
  • NHFA/CSANZ 2025: PCI within 120 min or fibrinolysis within 30 min

Real-Shift Translation

On a real shift, you are dialling 13 78 27 and 08 8378 1133 while the nurse is managing the patient, the wife is in the corridor, and the ECG is uploading to iPOCCS. Rehearsing this call — knowing what to say, what to ask — is why we practise it now.

Break Point 5Practical Skill

Treatment Initiation and Medication Safety

Skills Practice — Walk Through Each Skill

Task 1: IV Cannulation

  • 18G, antecubital fossa (may need contrast later)
  • Flush 0.9% NaCl, secure, document

Task 2: GTN Administration under SMO

  • Confirm SMO parameters: SBP, HR, PDE-5 status
  • Shake Nitrolingual spray. Patient opens mouth. Spray under tongue. Close mouth.
  • Reassess BP and pain at 5 min
  • Document: time, dose, observations pre/post

Task 3: IV Morphine (Two-Person Check)

  • Check: right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, expiry
  • In a 3-person team, the MO may be the second checker
  • Administer 2.5mg slow IV push. Observe.
  • Document time, dose, route, effect

Task 4: Subcutaneous Enoxaparin

  • Confirm weight-based dose (1mg/kg)
  • Anterolateral abdominal wall, 90 degree angle
  • Do not expel air bubble. Inject slowly. Do not rub.

Task 5: RDR Chart Documentation

  • Plot all observations on SA Health RDR chart after each intervention
  • Identify: are any parameters in the yellow or red escalation zone?
  • Trend comparison: are values improving, stable, or deteriorating?

Facilitator Prompts

  • Who checks the morphine in a 3-person team when one nurse is at triage?
  • BP drops to 85 systolic after GTN. What is your response?
  • Graham's pain goes from 9/10 to 6/10 after first GTN. Do you give another dose?
  • Are you plotting each set on the RDR chart? What colour zone are the obs in?

Expected Clinical Reasoning

  • MO may need to be the second checker for high-risk medications in a 3-person team
  • BP drops post-GTN: cease GTN, lay flat, IV bolus 250mL NaCl, reassess. Consider RV involvement — perform V4R. Notify MO.
  • Pain improving, SBP still >100: can repeat GTN under SMO. SA Health SMO has no maximum dose limit provided parameters are met — but clinical judgement applies.
  • RDR chart: observations should be plotted after every intervention. The colour-coded zones provide an objective trigger for escalation.

Real-Shift Translation

You are drawing up morphine while the MO is on the phone to MedSTAR, the triage nurse is reassuring the wife, and the ECG machine is printing. Practise these skills under low pressure so the muscle memory is there when the pressure is high.

Break Point 6Decision-Making

Reassessment and Escalation Decision

Facilitator Prompts

  • Review the RDR chart: what is the trend? What has improved?
  • The cardiologist says to give tenecteplase now. Who does what?
  • What are you monitoring for post-thrombolysis?
  • If Graham's BP suddenly drops to 80/50 — what triggers on the RDR chart? What is your response?
  • Who speaks to the wife?

Expected Clinical Reasoning

  • Trend: BP 148 to 128 (improving), HR 98 to 88 (improving), pain 9 to 5 (improving). Treatment is working. But STEMI persists until reperfusion is achieved.
  • Post-thrombolysis: Repeat 12-lead ECG at 60–90 min. Look for 50% or greater ST resolution and pain resolution. Watch for reperfusion arrhythmias. Have defibrillator ready.
  • RDR escalation: If BP 80/50 — this falls into the red zone on the RDR chart. Response: flat positioning, rapid IV bolus, reassess rhythm, call MedSTAR for update. Consider cardiogenic shock vs. bleeding complication.
  • Family: Brief, honest update. Usually the MO or triage nurse if available. Do not leave the wife without information.

Key Teaching Points

  • Trends matter more than single numbers — compare each set to the last on the RDR chart
  • RDR colour-coded zones provide an objective trigger — use them, even in resus
  • Post-thrombolysis is a high-risk period — the patient can improve or deteriorate rapidly
  • MedSTAR is your partner — the phone call is collaborative, not supervisory

Real-Shift Translation

The 40-minute wait for the MedSTAR helicopter is not idle time. You are monitoring, reassessing, managing complications, preparing documentation, and supporting the family. This is where rural SA clinical skill is tested.

Disposition and Transfer Planning

Disposition

Transfer to PCI-capable facility via MedSTAR aeromedical retrieval.

Preparation for MedSTAR Handover

  • All documentation current — RDR chart, medication times, ECGs
  • Repeat 12-lead ECG for retrieval team comparison
  • IV access patent and secure
  • Written medication summary: drug, dose, route, time for every medication
  • All ECG copies — originals travel with patient
  • Portable monitor for transfer to helipad
  • Defibrillator accompanies patient at all times
  • Family updated — destination hospital, how to follow, contact numbers
  • Cardiac rehabilitation referral flagged (CATCH Central Referral Service)
Break Point 7Discussion

Disposition and Transfer Planning

Facilitator Prompts

  • What documentation does the MedSTAR team need from us?
  • The helipad is 200 metres from the ED. How do you move Graham safely?
  • If the helicopter is delayed and pain recurs with re-elevation — what do you do?
  • What if MedSTAR sends a road ambulance instead? How does the plan change?

Expected Clinical Reasoning

  • MedSTAR needs: all ECGs, medication summary with times, RDR chart, allergy status, weight, thrombolysis time (if given), bloods sent/results
  • Transfer to helipad: stretcher, monitor, defib, O2, team escort. Plan for arrest during transfer.
  • Pain recurs post-thrombolysis with re-elevation: possible failed reperfusion — notify MedSTAR immediately, this is now rescue PCI indication
  • Road transfer: longer transit, ongoing monitoring and treatment during transport, SAAS crew handover becomes critical

Real-Shift Translation

Documents get lost, the monitor battery is flat, nobody told the wife where the helicopter is heading. Checklists and structured preparation prevent these real-world failures. Prepare for handover as if you have 5 minutes — because when MedSTAR walks in, you do.

ISBAR Handover

Facilitator Prompts

  • Have each participant practise the ISBAR aloud — MO to MedSTAR, nurse to nurse
  • What is the single most critical piece of information?
  • Was there closed-loop confirmation?

Expected Clinical Reasoning

  • Most critical: symptom onset time, ECG findings, reperfusion given and when, current haemodynamic status
  • Closed-loop: "Do you have any questions? Is there anything else you need from us?"
  • Nurse-to-nurse: observations, IV access, medications due, monitoring, patient/family concerns

Key Teaching Points

  • ISBAR is a clinical skill — practise until automatic
  • A poor handover can undo excellent clinical care
  • Include thrombolysis time and response — this drives cath lab urgency
  • ECGs are the most important physical document — they travel with the patient

Real-Shift Translation

When the MedSTAR team walks in, you have 5 minutes for handover before they want to move. Make it count.

Take-Home Messages

  • ECG within 10 minutes — acquire, interpret, upload to iPOCCS/MUSE. This is the non-negotiable standard.
  • SA Health SMOs authorise nurses to give aspirin and GTN for suspected cardiac chest pain without a prescription. Use them.
  • In rural SA STEMI: call iCARnet (08 8378 1133) and MedSTAR (13 78 27) early — they are your decision-making partners for reperfusion.
  • GTN caution in inferior STEMI — right ventricular involvement makes these patients preload-dependent. Consider V4R.
  • Plot every set of obs on the RDR chart. Trends matter more than single numbers.
  • PCI within 120 minutes or thrombolysis within 30 minutes — time is myocardium.
  • ISBAR handover is a clinical skill. Practise it until it is automatic.

Knowledge Check

Q1: Under the SA Health SMO for chest pain, what must the triage nurse confirm BEFORE administering GTN?

Correct. The SA Health SMO for GTN requires: SBP greater than 100mmHg, HR 60-120, and confirmation of no PDE-5 inhibitor use. These checks must be confirmed and documented before each dose.
Not quite. The SMO requires specific parameter checks: SBP greater than 100, HR 60-120, and no PDE-5 inhibitor use within 24hrs (sildenafil/vardenafil/avanafil) or 48hrs (tadalafil). The nurse does not need MO review or ECG before initiating GTN under SMO.

Q2: You identify an inferior STEMI on the 12-lead ECG. What SA Health systems should be activated?

Correct. In SA, STEMI triggers a coordinated response: ECG uploaded to iPOCCS/MUSE for remote review, iCARnet contacted for cardiologist input, and MedSTAR for retrieval coordination. The conference call model ensures collaborative decision-making.
Not quite. SA Health's cardiac pathway for rural STEMI involves three actions: (1) upload ECG to iPOCCS/MUSE, (2) call iCARnet for cardiology advice, and (3) call MedSTAR for retrieval. Both 08 8378 1133 and 13 78 27 should be on speed dial.

Q3: SA Health uses which system to detect and escalate clinical deterioration?

Correct. SA Health uses RDR (Rapid Detection and Response) observation charts with colour-coded escalation zones. Between the Flags is the NSW equivalent. Both serve the same purpose — standardised deterioration recognition — but in SA, RDR is the system.
Not quite. SA Health uses Rapid Detection and Response (RDR) observation charts. Between the Flags is NSW, ECAT is NSW, and NEWS2 is predominantly UK. Know your state's system.
