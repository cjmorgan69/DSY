Femoral Traction & C-Spine Immobilisation
Walk-Through Scenario — Volunteer AO

Scenario Context

A 75-year-old female has been struck by a golf cart at the Coffin Bay Golf Club. She has sustained a suspected mid-shaft femur fracture and possible cervical spine injury. Two Volunteer AO crews are responding.

CPPRO-028 STS Gen 2 CPPRO-021 C-Spine Pain Mx — AO ~90–120 min session
Resourcing: Primary crew (AO1 + AO2) with a second crew (AO3 + AO4) as backup. Paramedic backup 45 minutes away if requested. Nearest hospital: Port Lincoln Hospital (~30 min transport).

Learning Objectives

  1. Identify indications and contraindications for the Slishman Traction Splint Gen 2 (STS Gen 2)
  2. Correctly apply the STS Gen 2 to a simulated patient with a mid-shaft femur fracture
  3. Apply a modified towel roll within a vacuum mattress for cervical spine immobilisation
  4. Manage pain within AO scope of practice using Methoxyflurane and Paracetamol
  5. Coordinate a four-person team for multi-trauma patient assessment, treatment, and extrication
  6. Deliver a structured ISBAR handover including traction documentation and C-spine status

Required Equipment

  • STS Gen 2 traction splint (complete with pressure wrap and carry bag)
  • Vacuum mattress with pump

Break Point 3 — Initial Observations & Corpuls3 Setup
Type: Discussion / Practical Skill

Facilitator Prompts

  • Talk me through the primary survey findings — what concerns you most?
  • What do the observations tell you about this patient’s current state?
  • Is this patient time-critical? Why or why not?
  • What additional assessments do you need before making a plan?
  • AO2 — walk me through your Corpuls3 setup and where you’re documenting.

Expected AO Reasoning

  • Tachycardia + mild hypotension in a 75-year-old is concerning for haemorrhage. Femoral fractures can cause up to 2 L blood loss.
  • In elderly patients, “normal” BP may actually represent hypotension — they often run higher at baseline.
  • Elevated RR likely a combination of pain and early compensatory shock.
  • GCS 15 is reassuring but needs ongoing monitoring.
  • Need to assess distal perfusion of the right foot — pedal pulse, CRT, sensation.
  • Cervical spine tenderness plus significant mechanism plus distracting injury = cannot clinically clear the C-spine.

Key Teaching Points

  • First observations set the baseline — every subsequent set is compared to these.
  • Set Corpuls3 to repeat NIBP every 5 minutes. Trend monitoring catches deterioration early.
  • A painful distracting injury (femur fracture) means the patient cannot reliably report neck symptoms — default to immobilisation.

Real-Job Translation

  • If the patient is tachycardic and mildly hypotensive, they need close monitoring and timely transport.
  • Get repeat observations early and often — the trend tells you more than any single reading.

Clinical Decision Point

Working Diagnosis

  1. Suspected mid-shaft fracture, right femur
  2. Possible cervical spine injury (mechanism + cervical tenderness + distracting injury)

Applicable Protocols

  • CPPRO-028 Femoral Traction Splint — STS Gen 2
  • CPPRO-021 Cervical Spine Immobilisation — Modified towel roll within vacuum mattress
  • CPP-018-AO Spinal Injury — Ambulance Officer
  • Pain Mx AO Methoxyflurane + Paracetamol
Contraindications Check — STS Gen 2: NOT indicated for: neck of femur (NOF) fracture, avulsion with bone separation. This patient has mid-shaft deformity with shortening and rotation — consistent with diaphyseal fracture. STS Gen 2 is indicated.
C-Spine Decision: Cannot clinically clear. Significant mechanism (struck by vehicle) + cervical tenderness + distracting injury (femur fracture). Immobilisation required. Plan: modified towel roll within vacuum mattress. No semi-rigid collar required in this scenario.
Break Point 4 — Screening Tools, Protocols & Working Diagnosis
Type: Decision-Making

Facilitator Prompts

  • What is your working diagnosis?
  • Which protocols apply and why?
  • Is the STS Gen 2 indicated? What contraindications have you ruled out?
  • Can we clear the C-spine? Walk me through your reasoning.
  • Why vacuum mattress rather than just a towel roll on the stretcher?
  • What analgesia options are available within your scope?
  • Should we call the EOC Clinician? What are we asking for?

Expected AO Reasoning

  • Mid-shaft femur → STS Gen 2 is indicated. It is NOT a NOF (pain and deformity are mid-thigh, not groin/hip).
  • C-spine cannot be cleared — three factors: mechanism, tenderness, distracting injury.
  • Vacuum mattress immobilises the entire spine — indicated for significant mechanism with suspected spinal injury.
  • Modified towel roll inside the vacuum mattress provides C-spine immobilisation without needing a semi-rigid collar.
  • Analgesia: Methoxyflurane first (rapid onset), then Paracetamol PO (longer duration, slower onset).
  • EOC Clinician: Notify of significant trauma, confirm plan, request paramedic backup for IV analgesia.

Key Teaching Points

  • STS Gen 2 contraindications: NOF fracture, avulsion with bone separation. Know the difference between NOF and mid-shaft.
  • Modified towel roll in vacuum mattress: saline bags are NOT used — the mattress provides lateral support instead.
  • Vacuum mattress is the gold standard for suspected spinal cord injury during transport.
  • Always check medication contraindications before administration, even when under pressure.

Real-Job Translation

  • Your working diagnosis drives your treatment plan — get it right.
  • Know your protocols before you need them. On scene is not the time to read them for the first time.
  • Early EOC notification for significant trauma ensures backup is moving.

Treatment Phase

Indications

Suspected or obvious fracture of the femur diaphysis (mid-shaft). The diaphysis starts below the lesser trochanter and ends above the medial and lateral epicondyle.

Contraindications

  • Neck of femur (NOF) fracture
  • Avulsion with bone separation

Precautions

  • Suspected pelvic fracture → apply Pelvic Sling before traction device.
  • Regularly check traction is maintained — force can reduce by 58% within 30 minutes.
  • Consider padding the ischial (groin) strap — pressure on skin increases once traction applied.
  • Paediatrics: excessive traction may damage growth plates.

Traction Targets

  • Leg lengthens (equal to uninjured leg)
  • Improved patient comfort
  • Adequate distal perfusion (pedal pulse, CRT)
  • Neurological integrity maintained

Modified Towel Roll Within Vacuum Mattress

  • Saline bags are NOT required when using the vacuum mattress.
  • Option A: Modified towel roll (remove saline bags) with folded towels on both sides of the head, mattress moulded around them.
  • Option B: Place folded towels on both sides of the patient’s head and form the vacuum mattress around them.
  • A folded towel (~2 cm) under the patient’s head maintains the natural alignment of the spine.

‘Cervical Spine NOT Cleared’ Sticker

  • Apply in a prominent position as early as possible.
  • Avoid adhering directly to the patient’s skin.
  • This sticker alerts all people involved in care to maintain spinal precautions during handling.

Semi-Rigid Collar Contraindications (for reference)

  • Compromised airway
  • Spinal deformities
  • Concerns about raised intra-cranial pressure

Methoxyflurane (Penthrox)

  • 3 mL into inhaler device. Patient self-administers by inhaling through mouthpiece.
  • Diluter hole open = lower concentration (start here for elderly patients).
  • Diluter hole covered = stronger effect.
  • Maximum: 6 mL (2 bottles) per session. Duration: approximately 12–15 minutes per bottle.
  • Contraindications: renal impairment, hepatic disease, malignant hyperthermia susceptibility, concurrent nephrotoxic medications.
  • Caution in elderly: monitor level of consciousness for excessive sedation.

Paracetamol (Oral)

  • >50 kg: 1 g PO. ≤50 kg: 500 mg PO.
  • Maximum 4 g in 24 hours. Ask when the patient last took Paracetamol.
  • Contraindications: liver disease, known allergy.
  • Onset: 30–60 minutes (not immediate relief).

Non-Pharmacological

  • Splinting and traction provide significant mechanical pain relief.
  • Positional comfort, reassurance, temperature management.

Step 1 — Initial Stabilisation & Analgesia

  • AO1: Reassure Margaret. “Margaret, we’re going to look after you. Try to keep your head and neck still for me.”
  • AO2: Contact EOC Clinician — notify of significant trauma, confirm plan, request paramedic backup.
  • AO1: Prepare and administer Methoxyflurane.

Methoxyflurane Administration

Contraindication check: “Margaret, do you have any kidney or liver problems? Any family history of bad reactions to anaesthetics?” — No to all.
Open Methoxyflurane bottle. Pour 3 mL into inhaler chamber.
Coach patient: “Breathe in through this mouthpiece when you need pain relief. You control it — breathe in as you need to.”
Start with diluter hole OPEN (lower concentration for elderly patient).
Document time of administration on ePCR.
Monitor level of consciousness — watch for excessive drowsiness.

Paracetamol Administration

Check: “Margaret, when did you last take Paracetamol?” — None today. No liver disease. NKDA.
Estimated weight ~58 kg (>50 kg) → dose: 1 g (two 500 mg tablets).
Administer with a small sip of water. Document time and dose.

AO3Maintains manual C-spine stabilisation at the head throughout.

STS Gen 2 — Step-by-Step Application (CPPRO-028)

Hand hygiene and gloves — all clinicians involved.
Expose the injured limb — skin to splint. Use shears if needed. Place a towel over the groin for dignity. Consider temperature control.
AO4 begins manual traction. Apply gentle longitudinal traction from the ankle. Pull slowly — muscle spasm needs time to relax (this takes minutes, not seconds). Maintain traction until mechanical traction is locked.
Extend the telescopic pole. Pull the silver middle tube from the black outer tube until the spring button engages. Choose length based on patient’s leg.
Apply the BLACK ankle strap FIRST.
  • Neoprene flush against the skin.
  • Pole on the OUTER side of the strap.
  • Place the longer side of the strap UNDER the ankle.
  • Wrap around the limb firmly.
Apply the RED groin (ischial) strap.
  • Neoprene buckle cover flush against the skin.
  • Pole on the OUTER side of the ischial strap.
  • Carefully slide the long end under the patient’s leg.
  • Tighten the clasp firmly against the anterior thigh.
  • Take care not to impinge genitalia.
Pad the red groin strap with a combine dressing. Traction will exert pressure on the skin of the groin.
Pull the traction cord via the RED TOGGLE located at hip level. Pull the toggle towards the patient’s head. Pull slowly until the desired effect is achieved:
  • Leg lengthens (equal to uninjured leg)
  • Improved patient comfort
  • Bone realignment
Lock the traction. While maintaining tension, pull the cord towards the patient’s foot. Place the cord into the V-NOTCH at the top of the red inner tube.
Secure excess cord by wrapping it once around each cleat wing.
AO4 releases manual traction. Mechanical traction is now locked and maintained by the device.
Apply the Slishman Pressure Wrap:
  • Lasso one foot loosely in the loop.
  • Wrap the tail around the other foot so both feet sit adjacent (prevents rotation).
  • Velcro secures as you wrap — continue until no excess tail.
Reassess distal perfusion immediately:
  • Pedal pulse
  • Capillary refill time
  • Sensation (light touch)
  • Patient comfort and pain score
Document: Time traction applied. Pre- and post-traction perfusion findings. Any changes (e.g., improved pulse, reduced pain, leg length corrected).
Caution: Traction force can reduce by 58% within 30 minutes, with the fastest decline in the first 5 minutes. Check traction regularly during transport. Relock if loosened.

Expected AO Reasoning

  • Analgesia before splinting reduces distress and allows better application.
  • Manual traction applied slowly — forcing against spasm causes more pain and potential injury.
  • Black strap first, red strap second — always follow this order.
  • Neoprene against skin, pole on outer side — consistent for both straps.
  • Pull red toggle toward HEAD to apply traction. Pull cord toward FOOT to lock in V-notch.
  • Pressure wrap prevents rotation of the injured leg during transport.
  • Distal perfusion checks before AND after — document the change.

Key Teaching Points

  • Application sequence: Ankle → Groin → Pad → Traction → Lock → Cleat → Wrap → Check.
  • Traction provides significant pain relief, haemorrhage control, and prevents further neurovascular damage.
  • An incorrectly applied device can cause harm — take time to get it right.
  • In an unconscious patient, use the uninjured leg as a guide for traction length.

Real-Job Translation

  • Practice with the actual STS Gen 2 until the sequence is automatic.
  • In a real job, this is done on the ground, potentially in the dark, under pressure.
  • Manual traction by AO4 is essential — this is very difficult with only two people.
  • Check traction after every patient movement.

Step 4 — Vacuum Mattress & Modified Towel Roll

While AO1 applied the STS Gen 2, AO4 has prepared the vacuum mattress alongside the patient (deflated, flat on the ground).

Patient Transfer onto Vacuum Mattress

AO1 gives a coordination call. All four AOs know their position and task before any movement begins.
Positions:
  • AO3: At head — maintains manual C-spine stabilisation throughout.
  • AO1: At the right leg — supports and controls the injured leg with STS Gen 2 in situ.
  • AO2 + AO4: At trunk and hips.
On a coordinated count, carefully lift the patient as a unit and place onto the vacuum mattress. Maintain spinal alignment at all times. AO3 gives the command: “Ready… steady… lift.”

Modified Towel Roll Application (Within Vacuum Mattress)

Place a folded towel (~2 cm) under the patient’s head to maintain neutral spinal alignment.
Place folded towels on both sides of the patient’s head, snug against the ears/temples.
Mould the vacuum mattress around the patient’s entire body, including around the towels at the head and around the STS Gen 2.
AO3 adjusts hand position as needed to allow the mattress to be formed around the head and neck, then carefully withdraws hands once the mattress is supporting the head.
Deflate the vacuum mattress using the pump until rigid. Ensure firm support around the head, neck, trunk, pelvis, and legs.
Secure the vacuum mattress straps across the patient (chest, hips, legs).
Apply ‘Cervical Spine NOT Cleared’ sticker in a prominent position. Avoid adhering directly to the patient’s skin — place on clothing or the vacuum mattress itself.
Important: Within the vacuum mattress, saline bags are NOT used for the towel roll. The moulded mattress provides lateral head support. Use folded towels only.

Break Point 6 — Reassessment & Escalation Decision
Type: Decision-Making

Facilitator Prompts

  • Compare the initial and post-treatment observations. What has changed?
  • Has Margaret improved, stayed the same, or deteriorated?
  • Is pain management adequate at 5/10? What else can we do within our scope?
  • Do we still need paramedic backup? What for?
  • What would make you escalate further right now?
  • When should we get moving?

Expected AO Reasoning

  • Observations improving: HR down, BP up, RR down — positive trend.
  • Pain reduced from 8/10 to 5/10 — traction and analgesia are working.
  • Distal perfusion improved — traction is achieving alignment and reducing vascular compromise.
  • Methoxyflurane will wear off (12–15 min) — need a plan for transport analgesia. Paracetamol is working but slow onset.
  • Paramedic backup still warranted for IV analgesia during transport, but do not delay transport waiting.
  • Consider paramedic rendezvous en route.

Key Teaching Points

  • Trend is more important than any single observation — look for the direction of change.
  • Improved distal perfusion post-traction confirms the splint is achieving its purpose.
  • Escalation triggers: increasing tachycardia, dropping BP, altered GCS, worsening pain despite treatment, loss of distal perfusion.
  • Do not delay transport to wait for paramedic — time to definitive care matters in elderly trauma patients.

Real-Job Translation

  • Regular reassessment catches deterioration early — set yourself a rhythm.
  • Paramedic rendezvous en route is a practical rural option — meet on the main road.
  • Balance on-scene treatment time against transport time. Move when the patient is stabilised.

Extrication & Transport

Extrication Plan

  1. Transfer vacuum mattress (with patient, STS Gen 2 in situ) onto stretcher.
  2. Secure vacuum mattress to stretcher with straps.
  3. Transport ~200 m across fairway to ambulance (use club buggy to assist if available).
  4. Load into ambulance.
  5. AO1 rides in back with patient. AO2 drives.
  6. Second crew (AO3 + AO4) follows in their vehicle or returns to station.
After every movement: Check STS Gen 2 traction is maintained. Check distal perfusion. Check vacuum mattress integrity and C-spine immobilisation. Document any changes.

  • Rural transport times are significant — the patient needs to be stable before departure.

Real-Job Translation

  • In a real job, this is where things often go wrong — rough ground, equipment shifting, traction loosening.
  • Practice the full transfer sequence: ground → vacuum mattress → stretcher → ambulance.
  • Clear communication during extrication is non-negotiable. One voice, one count.

ISBAR Handover (AO1 Delivers)

I — Identify

“I’m [Name], Ambulance Officer from [Station]. This is Margaret Thompson, 75 years old.”

S — Situation

“Margaret was struck by a golf cart at Coffin Bay Golf Club approximately two hours ago. She has a suspected mid-shaft fracture of the right femur and a possible cervical spine injury. Cervical spine is NOT cleared.”

B — Background

“She was walking across the fairway when a golf cart lost control on a slope and struck her from behind. The cart wheel ran over her right thigh. She has a history of hypertension, osteoarthritis, osteoporosis, and diet-controlled Type 2 diabetes. Medications: Amlodipine 5 mg daily, Glucosamine, Calcium and Vitamin D. No known allergies. She had lunch about one hour before the incident.”

A — Assessment

“On arrival: GCS 15, HR 108, BP 102 over 58, RR 22, SpO2 96%, pain 8 out of 10. Obvious mid-thigh deformity, shortening, and external rotation right leg. Cervical tenderness without neurological deficit. Post-treatment: HR 92, BP 110 over 65, RR 18, SpO2 97%, pain 5 out of 10. Distal perfusion improved — pedal pulse now palpable, CRT 2 seconds, sensation intact.”

R — Recommendation

“She has an STS Gen 2 traction splint in situ, applied at [time]. Immobilised in a vacuum mattress with modified towel rolls for C-spine. Cervical spine is NOT cleared. She received Methoxyflurane 3 mL inhaled at [time] and Paracetamol 1 g oral at [time]. She will require imaging of the right femur and cervical spine, ongoing pain management, and likely orthopaedic review. Here is the ePCR.”

Critical Handover Flags:
  • ‘Cervical Spine NOT Cleared’ — sticker visible, stated verbally.
  • Traction splint time of application — record and state.
  • Medication times and doses — record and state.
  • Pre- and post-traction perfusion changes — documented on ePCR.

Key Teaching Points

  • ISBAR is structured and concise — do not ramble. Practise until it is automatic.
  • The receiving team needs to know about the traction splint — they will manage removal.
  • ‘Cervical Spine NOT Cleared’ is a critical safety flag — ensure it is heard and seen.
  • Time documentation matters: when was traction applied, when were medications given.

Real-Job Translation

  • A clear handover is the final link in the care chain. A poor handover can undo good work.
  • Hand over the documentation with the patient — ePCR complete before arrival.

Debrief (10–15 minutes)

Reflective Questions

  1. How did our en-route planning affect what happened on scene?
  2. What was the most challenging part of this scenario and why?
  3. How well did the team coordinate when the second crew arrived? What would we change?
  4. Were there any points where you felt uncertain about what to do next?
  5. How did the STS Gen 2 application go? What would you do differently next time?
  6. Did we manage Margaret’s pain effectively within our scope? Could we have done more?
  7. How confident are you with the modified towel roll technique inside the vacuum mattress?

Take-Home Messages

  1. Femoral shaft fractures are high-risk injuries. Up to 2 L blood loss is possible, even from seemingly moderate mechanisms in elderly patients with osteoporosis.
  2. STS Gen 2 follows a specific sequence. Ankle strap → Groin strap → Pad → Traction → V-notch lock → Cleat wings → Pressure wrap → Perfusion check.
  3. Traction provides real clinical benefit. Pain relief, haemorrhage control, perfusion improvement, and prevention of further neurovascular damage.
  4. C-spine cannot be cleared when there is a significant mechanism, cervical tenderness, OR a distracting injury. Default to immobilisation.
  5. Modified towel roll in the vacuum mattress does not use saline bags. The moulded mattress provides lateral head support. Folded towels only.
  6. Coordinate your team early. Brief arriving crews clearly, assign roles, and communicate throughout every patient movement.
  7. Document traction time, perfusion changes, and medication times. This information is critical for hospital handover and ongoing patient care.

Optional Scenario Variations

Variation 1 — Open (Compound) Fracture Same mechanism, but bone is visible through a wound in the thigh. Requires wound irrigation with at least 1 L sodium chloride 0.9% before traction. Cover with a damp combine dressing. After traction, the bone may retract back into the thigh — document this and hand over clearly. Re-cover with a fresh damp combine and crepe bandage. High infection risk.
Variation 2 — Patient Deterioration En Route During transport, Margaret becomes confused (GCS 13). HR increases to 122, BP drops to 85/50. Signs of significant haemorrhagic shock. Escalation: call EOC Clinician, upgrade to time-critical transport, request urgent paramedic rendezvous. Reassess traction. Consider positioning (elevate legs if vacuum mattress allows). Pre-alert Port Lincoln Hospital.
Variation 3 — Bilateral Femur Fractures Both legs are injured. Two STS Gen 2 devices required (one per leg). Significantly higher blood loss risk. Use thigh deformity, pain score, perfusion, and neurovascular function as traction guides for each leg independently. More complex extrication — requires careful coordination.
Variation 4 — Isolated C-Spine Concern (No Femur Fracture) Margaret has neck pain and upper limb tingling after being knocked down, but no femur fracture. Focus shifts entirely to C-spine assessment, immobilisation decisions, and vacuum mattress application. Discuss when a semi-rigid collar IS indicated and the contraindications (compromised airway, spinal deformities, raised ICP concerns).
