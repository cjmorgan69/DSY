Prostate Cancer Care Plan Review

Setting: General practice clinic, scheduled 45-minute care plan review appointment

Your role: Practice nurse conducting structured interview with patient who has been diagnosed with prostate cancer and is under active surveillance or post-treatment follow-up

Appointment type: GPCCMP review (MBS item 967 — face-to-face review, $156.55)

GP's role: Reviews your findings, finalises care plan, writes referrals to allied health services

Learning Objectives

  1. Conduct a structured prostate cancer care plan review aligned with GPCCMP framework and 2025 Australian guidelines
  2. Assess current disease status, treatment side effects, and psychological wellbeing
  3. Screen for complications and comorbidities relevant to prostate cancer survivors
  4. Identify appropriate allied health referrals (physiotherapy, psychology, sexual health support)
  5. Document findings clearly for GP review and plan finalisation
  6. Support shared decision-making around ongoing surveillance, treatment options, and quality of life

Knowledge Foundation

What is a GPCCMP? ▼

GPCCMP = GP Chronic Condition Management Plan

Effective from 1 July 2025, the GPCCMP replaces the old GPMP (GP Management Plan) and TCA (Team Care Arrangement).

Key Features:

  • Eligibility: Patient has a chronic or terminal condition present (or likely) for 6+ months. Prostate cancer qualifies.
  • Preparation: Once per 12 months (if necessary) — MBS item 965, $156.55
  • Review: Every 3 months — MBS item 967, $156.55
  • Allied health entitlements: Up to 5 individual visits per calendar year (10 for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander patients)
  • Referral process: Direct GP letter to allied health provider (no EPC form required from 1 July 2025)
  • Referral validity: 18 months from first session
The Practice Nurse's Role in Prostate Cancer Care ▼

What You Do:

  • Conduct structured care plan interview with patient
  • Review recent pathology results (PSA, other relevant tests)
  • Assess current symptoms, treatment side effects, and functional status
  • Screen for complications (erectile dysfunction, urinary symptoms, bowel changes)
  • Assess psychological wellbeing and cancer-related distress
  • Identify lifestyle factors and barriers to self-management
  • Recommend allied health referrals (for GP to finalise)
  • Document findings in structured format for GP review

Scope Boundaries:

  • You do NOT make final clinical decisions — the GP does
  • You do NOT diagnose or change treatment — that's the GP/urologist's role
  • You DO gather comprehensive information and flag concerns for GP review
  • You DO support shared decision-making and patient education
Allied Health Under a GPCCMP ▼

Entitlements:

  • Individual visits: Up to 5 per calendar year (10 for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander patients)
  • Group sessions: Not applicable for prostate cancer (group sessions are T2DM-specific)
  • Referral validity: 18 months from first session

Available Providers for Prostate Cancer Survivors:

  • Physiotherapist: Pelvic floor rehabilitation, continence support, exercise programs
  • Psychologist: Cancer-related distress, anxiety, depression, sexual health counselling
  • Occupational therapist: Functional assessment, activity modification, return-to-work support
  • Exercise physiologist: Tailored exercise programs to improve fitness and quality of life
  • Dietitian: Nutritional support, weight management, prostate-healthy diet
  • Speech pathologist: If swallowing or communication issues arise

Referral Process:

GP writes direct referral letter to allied health provider. Patient takes letter to provider. No EPC form required.

The Care Plan Interview: 10 Structured Steps

1
Opening & Building Rapport
What's happening: You're setting the tone for the appointment, explaining the purpose of the care plan review, and establishing trust. This is especially important for cancer patients who may be anxious or have concerns they haven't shared.
Suggested Approach:
  • Greet warmly and use patient's preferred name
  • Acknowledge the diagnosis: "I know you were diagnosed with prostate cancer 6 months ago. How have you been managing?"
  • Explain the appointment: "Today I want to check in on how you're doing physically and emotionally, review your recent test results, and make sure we have a plan that works for you."
  • Set expectations: "This will take about 45 minutes. I'll ask you some questions, and then Dr [GP name] will review everything and finalise your care plan."
  • Invite questions: "Is there anything you'd like to talk about today?"
Patient Response:

"Thanks. Yeah, it's been a bit of a shock, to be honest. I've been trying not to worry too much, but I do think about it. My wife has been great, but I'm not sure what happens next. The urologist said everything looks stable, but I want to make sure I'm doing everything right."

Key Point:

Cancer diagnosis triggers anxiety and uncertainty. Acknowledging this and providing structure reassures the patient. Active surveillance is a valid, evidence-based approach for low-risk prostate cancer, but patients need clear communication about what to expect.

Documentation Note:

Record: Patient's emotional state (anxious but coping), wife's support, understanding of diagnosis and management plan, any specific concerns raised.

2
General Wellbeing Check
What's happening: You're assessing overall health, energy, sleep, and any new symptoms that might indicate disease progression or treatment side effects.
Suggested Approach:
  • "How's your energy been? Any fatigue or tiredness?"
  • "How's your sleep? Any changes?"
  • "Any new aches, pains, or symptoms you've noticed?"
  • "How's your appetite?"
  • "Any weight changes?"
  • Listen for: Unexplained weight loss, bone pain, fatigue (could suggest progression)
Patient Response:

"Energy's been okay, actually. I'm sleeping reasonably well, though I do wake up a couple of times a night to go to the toilet. That's been happening for a while, though — not new. No pain or anything. Weight's about the same. I've been trying to eat healthier since the diagnosis."

Key Point:

Nocturia (waking to urinate) is common in prostate cancer patients and can be related to the cancer itself, BPH, or age. In this case, it's not new, so it's likely baseline. No red flags for metastatic disease (no bone pain, weight loss, or fatigue). Patient is taking positive steps (diet).

Documentation Note:

Record: Energy level (good), sleep (fair, with nocturia x2/night — not new), appetite (good), weight (stable), no new symptoms.

3
Prostate Cancer-Specific Assessment
What's happening: You're assessing disease-specific symptoms, understanding the patient's current management (active surveillance), and exploring any concerns about progression or treatment options.
Suggested Approach:
  • "Tell me about your understanding of your cancer diagnosis. What did the urologist tell you?"
  • "You're on active surveillance — do you understand what that means?"
  • "How do you feel about the plan to monitor rather than treat right now?"
  • "Any concerns about the cancer progressing?"
  • "Have you had any urinary symptoms — difficulty passing urine, weak stream, urgency?"
  • "Any bowel symptoms — diarrhoea, constipation, blood in stool?"
  • "Any blood in urine or semen?"
  • Review PSA trend: "Your PSA was 5.2 six months ago and is now 5.4. That's a very slight rise, which is normal variation. The urologist is happy with this."
Patient Response:

"The urologist explained it's a slow-growing cancer and that watching it is safer than surgery or radiation at this stage. I get that, but sometimes I worry — what if it's growing and we miss it? I've been reading things online, and some people say you should treat it straight away. No urinary symptoms, though. Everything's normal down there. No blood or anything. I just want to make sure we're not missing something."

Key Point:

Active surveillance is evidence-based for low-risk prostate cancer (Gleason ≤6, PSA <10, clinical stage T1-T2a). It reduces overtreatment and treatment side effects while maintaining cure rates. However, patient anxiety about "missing" progression is common. Reassurance based on PSA trend, imaging, and urologist review is important. Online information can increase anxiety — address this.

Documentation Note:

Record: Patient understands active surveillance, some anxiety about progression, no urinary/bowel/sexual symptoms, PSA trend stable (slight rise within normal variation), urologist review reassuring.

4
Medication Review & Adherence
What's happening: You're checking that the patient is taking medications as prescribed and assessing for side effects. For prostate cancer patients on active surveillance, this focuses on comorbidity management (BP, cholesterol).
Suggested Approach:
  • "Let's go through your medications. You're on amlodipine for blood pressure and atorvastatin for cholesterol. Are you taking these regularly?"
  • "Any side effects from the amlodipine — headaches, swelling in the ankles, dizziness?"
  • "Any muscle aches or pains from the atorvastatin?"
  • "Are you taking any supplements or herbal products?"
  • "Any over-the-counter medications?"
  • Check BP at home if patient monitors: "How's your blood pressure been at home?"
Patient Response:

"Yeah, I take them both every morning. No side effects that I can tell. BP's been pretty good — I've been checking it at home, and it's usually around 135-140 over 80-85. I'm not taking anything else. I did think about taking some supplements — I saw something about saw palmetto for prostate health — but I didn't want to interfere with anything, so I asked the urologist first. He said it wasn't necessary."

Key Point:

Good adherence and no side effects. Patient is appropriately cautious about supplements. Saw palmetto has limited evidence for prostate cancer prevention/management and is not recommended. BP control is good (target <140/90 for this patient). Cardiovascular risk management is important in prostate cancer survivors.

Documentation Note:

Record: Amlodipine 5mg daily (adherent, no side effects), Atorvastatin 20mg daily (adherent, no side effects), home BP monitoring (135-140/80-85), no supplements or OTC medications.

5
Complication Screening #1: Urinary & Bowel Function
What's happening: You're screening for urinary incontinence, urgency, frequency, and bowel symptoms that may be related to prostate cancer or its treatment. These are common concerns for prostate cancer survivors.
Suggested Approach:
  • "Let's talk about urinary symptoms. Do you have any leakage or incontinence?"
  • "How often are you passing urine during the day? Any urgency?"
  • "At night — you mentioned waking to pass urine. How many times? Is that affecting your sleep?"
  • "Any difficulty starting the stream or weak stream?"
  • "Any bowel symptoms — constipation, diarrhoea, urgency, incontinence?"
  • "Any blood in urine or stool?"
  • If on active surveillance: "These symptoms can sometimes be related to the prostate cancer itself or to BPH. We'll monitor them."
Patient Response:

"No leakage. I pass urine maybe 7-8 times during the day, which seems normal. At night, I wake up twice, usually between midnight and 6am. It's not really affecting my sleep — I fall back asleep pretty easily. No difficulty starting or weak stream. Bowels are fine — regular, no issues."

Key Point:

Nocturia x2 is common in men this age and may be related to age, BPH, or prostate cancer. Daytime frequency (7-8 times) is normal. No incontinence, no bowel symptoms. No red flags. If symptoms worsen, pelvic floor physiotherapy can help. Urinary symptoms should be monitored as part of active surveillance.

Documentation Note:

Record: Daytime frequency 7-8 times/day (normal), nocturia x2/night (not new, not affecting sleep), no incontinence, no difficulty with stream, no bowel symptoms.

6
Complication Screening #2: Sexual & Erectile Function
What's happening: You're assessing sexual function and erectile dysfunction, which are important quality-of-life issues for prostate cancer patients. This requires sensitivity and normalisation.
Suggested Approach:
  • Normalise: "Many men with prostate cancer have concerns about sexual function. It's important to talk about this."
  • "How's your sexual function been? Any changes since the diagnosis?"
  • "Are you able to get and maintain erections?"
  • "Any changes in libido or interest in sex?"
  • "Any pain or discomfort during sex?"
  • "How is your partner coping with the diagnosis? Has it affected your relationship?"
  • If ED present: "This is common and treatable. We can discuss options with the GP."
Patient Response:

"Um, yeah, that's been a bit of a concern, actually. I haven't had any problems with erections, but I've noticed my libido isn't quite what it was. I think it's more anxiety about the cancer than anything physical. My wife and I have talked about it — she's been very understanding. We're still intimate, just maybe not as often. I'm hoping it'll improve once I feel less anxious about the diagnosis."

Key Point:

Reduced libido is common after cancer diagnosis and is often psychological (anxiety, fear) rather than physical. Patient has good relationship communication and partner support — protective factors. No erectile dysfunction. Reassurance and psychological support may help. If ED develops, PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil) are options. Consider psychology referral if anxiety persists.

Documentation Note:

Record: Erectile function intact, reduced libido (likely anxiety-related), good partner communication and support, no pain with intercourse, patient hopeful about improvement with reduced anxiety.

7
Organ System Assessment: Cardiovascular & Metabolic
What's happening: You're assessing cardiovascular health and metabolic factors. Prostate cancer survivors have increased cardiovascular risk, especially if on hormone therapy (not applicable here, but important context). You're also reviewing comorbidities.
Suggested Approach:
  • Review BP: "Your BP has been improving — 138/84 today, down from 142/86 last time."
  • "Any chest pain, shortness of breath, or palpitations?"
  • "Any leg swelling or calf pain?"
  • Review lipids: "Your cholesterol is well-controlled on atorvastatin."
  • "Any family history of heart disease?"
  • Assess exercise: "How much physical activity are you doing?"
  • Assess diet: "Tell me about your diet — any changes since the diagnosis?"
Patient Response:

"No chest pain or anything like that. I've been trying to exercise more — I go for a walk most days, maybe 30 minutes. I'm not doing heavy exercise, but I feel good. Diet-wise, I've cut back on red meat and processed foods. My wife's been helping me cook healthier meals. No family history of heart disease that I know of."

Key Point:

Good cardiovascular risk profile. BP improving, lipids on target, no cardiac symptoms. Exercise (30 min walking most days) is excellent and aligns with prostate cancer survivorship guidelines (150 min moderate activity/week). Diet changes are positive. No red flags. Encourage continued exercise and healthy diet.

Documentation Note:

Record: BP 138/84 (improving), no cardiac symptoms, exercise 30 min walking most days, diet improved (reduced red meat/processed foods), lipids on target, no family history of CVD.

8
Lifestyle Assessment: Diet, Activity, Smoking, Alcohol
What's happening: You're assessing modifiable lifestyle factors that can influence prostate cancer outcomes and overall health. Exercise and diet are particularly important.
Suggested Approach:
  • Exercise: "What type of exercise are you doing? How often? Any barriers?"
  • Diet: "Tell me about a typical day of eating. Any specific dietary changes since diagnosis?"
  • Smoking: "Do you smoke or have you ever smoked?"
  • Alcohol: "How much alcohol do you drink per week?"
  • Weight: "How do you feel about your weight? Any goals?"
  • Encourage: "Exercise and a healthy diet can help with energy, mood, and overall health during cancer treatment and recovery."
Patient Response:

"I walk most days — it's easy because I can do it from home. I'd like to do more, maybe some strength training, but I'm not sure where to start. Diet's pretty good now — lots of vegetables, fish, whole grains. I used to eat more red meat, but I've cut back. I don't smoke — never have. Alcohol, maybe a couple of beers on the weekend. Weight-wise, I'd like to lose a few kilos, but it's not urgent. I'm more focused on feeling good and staying healthy."

Key Point:

Excellent lifestyle habits. Walking is good; strength training would add benefit (improves bone health, muscle, mood). Diet is prostate-healthy (vegetables, fish, whole grains, reduced red meat). No smoking, minimal alcohol. Weight loss of 5-10% would be beneficial but not urgent. Patient is motivated by health rather than weight — positive mindset. Consider exercise physiologist referral to develop structured strength training program.

Documentation Note:

Record: Exercise — walking 30 min most days, interested in strength training; Diet — improved (vegetables, fish, whole grains, reduced red meat); Smoking — never; Alcohol — 2 beers/weekend; Weight — BMI 27.3, interested in modest loss; Motivation — high.

9
Mental Health & Psychological Wellbeing
What's happening: You're assessing cancer-related distress, anxiety, depression, and coping. This is crucial for prostate cancer patients, who often experience significant psychological impact.
Suggested Approach:
  • "How have you been coping emotionally since the diagnosis?"
  • "Any anxiety or worry about the cancer?"
  • "Any low mood or depression?"
  • "How's your sleep? Any nightmares or intrusive thoughts?"
  • "Do you have support — family, friends, support groups?"
  • "Have you thought about talking to a counsellor or psychologist?"
  • Screen for depression: "Do you feel hopeful about the future?"
  • Screen for anxiety: "Do you worry a lot about the cancer coming back?"
Patient Response:

"Emotionally, it's been a bit of a rollercoaster. When I first got the diagnosis, I was pretty scared — I thought 'cancer' meant I was going to die. But the urologist explained it's low-risk and slow-growing, and that helped. I still have moments where I worry — especially before appointments or when I think about the PSA result. My wife's been amazing, and I've talked to a couple of friends who've had cancer. I feel hopeful, but there's definitely some anxiety. I haven't seen a psychologist, but I'm open to it if it would help."

Key Point:

Cancer-related anxiety is normal and understandable. Patient has good insight, strong support system, and hopeful outlook — protective factors. Anxiety is manageable but present. Psychology referral would be beneficial for anxiety management, coping strategies, and reassurance. Prostate cancer support groups (online or in-person) can also help. No signs of major depression.

Documentation Note:

Record: Emotional response — initial fear, now more hopeful; Anxiety — present but manageable, especially around appointments; Coping — good (wife support, friends, positive reframing); Sleep — fair; Support — strong family and social support; Openness to psychology — yes. Recommend psychology referral for anxiety management.

10
Referrals, Goals & Wrapping Up
What's happening: You're synthesising all the information, identifying appropriate allied health referrals, setting goals with the patient, and preparing the summary for the GP to review and finalise.
Suggested Approach:
  • "Let's talk about what might help you over the next few months."
  • "Based on what you've told me, I think a psychologist could help with anxiety management. Would that be helpful?"
  • "An exercise physiologist could help you develop a strength training program. Interested?"
  • "What are your main goals for the next 3 months?"
  • "Any questions or concerns we haven't covered?"
  • "I'll summarise everything and give it to Dr [GP name]. He'll review it and finalise your care plan. We'll also arrange any referrals he thinks are appropriate."
  • "When's your next urologist appointment?"
Patient Response:

"Yeah, I think talking to a psychologist would be good. I want to get better at managing the anxiety. And I'm definitely interested in learning how to do strength training properly — I don't want to hurt myself. My goals are to feel more confident about my health, lose a few kilos, and get stronger. My next urologist appointment is in 3 months."

Key Point:

Patient is engaged and motivated. Recommended referrals: (1) Psychologist for anxiety management and cancer-related distress (priority); (2) Exercise physiologist for structured strength training program (high priority). Both are within GPCCMP entitlements (5 individual visits/year). Goals are realistic and health-focused. Urologist follow-up is appropriate (3-monthly for active surveillance).

Documentation Note:

Record: Recommended referrals — (1) Psychologist (anxiety management, cancer-related distress); (2) Exercise physiologist (strength training program). Patient goals — manage anxiety, lose 5-10 kg, develop strength training, feel more confident. Next urologist appointment — 3 months. Care plan summary prepared for GP review and finalisation.

Quick Reference: Prostate Cancer Care Planning

Active Surveillance Monitoring Protocol ▼

For Low-Risk Prostate Cancer (Gleason ≤6, PSA <10, T1-T2a):

  • PSA testing: Every 3-6 months initially, then every 6-12 months if stable
  • Digital rectal examination: Periodically (not routine in primary care)
  • Repeat biopsy: At 12 months (at clinician's discretion), then only if PSA changes or clinical concern
  • Repeat MRI: May be offered at 12-24 months or if clinically indicated
  • Triggers for treatment: Pathological progression on biopsy, patient preference, or clinical concern
Red Flags & Escalation Triggers ▼

Alert GP Urgently If:

  • Rapid PSA rise: PSA doubling time <3 years (may indicate progression)
  • Bone pain: Could indicate metastatic disease
  • Unexplained weight loss: May suggest disease progression
  • Severe urinary retention: May require catheterisation
  • Haematuria or haemospermia: Needs investigation
  • Severe psychological distress: Risk of self-harm or non-adherence
  • Acute urinary symptoms: May indicate infection or obstruction
Prostate Cancer Survivorship: Key Domains ▼

Physical Health:

  • Disease monitoring (PSA, imaging, biopsy as per protocol)
  • Treatment side effects (urinary, bowel, sexual)
  • Comorbidity management (BP, lipids, diabetes)
  • Exercise and physical activity (150 min/week moderate intensity)
  • Nutrition and weight management

Psychological Health:

  • Cancer-related anxiety and fear of recurrence
  • Depression and mood disturbance
  • Coping strategies and resilience
  • Support systems (family, friends, support groups)

Sexual & Intimate Health:

  • Erectile function and sexual satisfaction
  • Libido and interest in sex
  • Relationship communication and intimacy
  • Access to sexual health support (psychology, urology)

Social & Functional:

  • Return to work and daily activities
  • Social engagement and relationships
  • Practical support and resources
Allied Health Referral Indications ▼

  • Cancer-related anxiety or fear of recurrence
  • Depression or mood disturbance
  • Sexual dysfunction (psychological component)
  • Coping difficulties or adjustment issues

Exercise Physiologist:

  • Desire for structured exercise program
  • Strength training for bone health and muscle
  • Cardiovascular fitness improvement
  • Weight management support

Physiotherapist:

  • Pelvic floor dysfunction or incontinence
  • Urinary symptoms affecting quality of life
  • Post-treatment rehabilitation

Dietitian:

  • Weight management (loss or gain)
  • Nutritional support for energy and health
  • Prostate-healthy diet education
