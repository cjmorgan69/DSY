Asthma Care Plan Review

Welcome to Your Practice Module

You're about to conduct a structured 45-minute asthma care plan review appointment with a patient in your general practice clinic. Your role is to gather comprehensive information about their asthma control, medication use, and lifestyle factors—then document findings clearly for the GP to review and finalise the care plan.

Setting: General practice clinic (urban or rural Australia) | Appointment: Scheduled 45-minute care plan review | Your role: Practice nurse conducting structured interview

Learning Objectives

  1. Conduct a structured asthma review interview aligned to GPCCMP framework (effective 1 July 2025)
  2. Assess asthma control using validated tools (ACQ-5, ACT) and identify symptom patterns
  3. Review medication adherence, inhaler technique, and trigger identification
  4. Screen for asthma complications and comorbidities (allergic rhinitis, GORD, anxiety)
  5. Identify gaps in preventive care (spirometry, asthma action plan, written plan)
  6. Recommend appropriate allied health referrals (respiratory physiotherapist, psychologist) and document for GP finalisation

Knowledge Foundation

What is a GPCCMP? ▼

GPCCMP = GP Chronic Condition Management Plan

Effective 1 July 2025, the GPCCMP replaces the old GPMP (229, 721) and TCA (230, 723).

Key Features:

  • Eligibility: Patient has at least one chronic condition present (or likely) for 6+ months, or terminal condition. No specific condition list—GP's clinical judgement.
  • MBS Items: 965 (prepare face-to-face), 967 (review face-to-face), 92029 (prepare video), 92030 (review video)
  • Fee: $156.55 per preparation or review
  • Frequency: Preparation once per 12 months (if necessary); reviews every 3 months
  • Your role: Conduct structured interview, gather information, document findings for GP to review and finalise
The Practice Nurse's Role in Asthma Review ▼

What You Do:

  • Conduct a structured, patient-centred interview (45 minutes)
  • Assess asthma control, symptoms, and triggers
  • Review medications, adherence, and inhaler technique
  • Screen for complications (exacerbations, hospitalisations, comorbidities)
  • Explore lifestyle factors (smoking, exercise, allergen exposure)
  • Assess mental health and psychosocial barriers
  • Identify gaps in preventive care (spirometry, asthma action plan)
  • Recommend allied health referrals (for GP to finalise)
  • Document findings in structured format for GP review

Scope Boundaries:

  • You gather information and make recommendations
  • GP makes final clinical decisions, signs the plan, and writes referrals
  • If you identify red flags (severe exacerbation, acute distress), alert GP immediately
  • Do not diagnose or change medications without GP approval
Allied Health Under a GPCCMP ▼

Individual Visits Entitlements:

  • Up to 5 per calendar year (10 for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander patients)
  • Referral validity: 18 months from first session
  • Referral process: Direct GP letter (no EPC form required from 1 July 2025)

Available Allied Health Providers for Asthma:

  • Respiratory Physiotherapist: Breathing exercises, inhaler technique, airway clearance
  • Psychologist: Asthma-related anxiety, depression, coping strategies
  • Dietitian: Nutritional support, weight management, food triggers
  • Exercise Physiologist: Safe exercise prescription, fitness improvement
  • Occupational Therapist: Environmental modifications, activity pacing
  • Physiotherapist, speech pathologist, audiologist, chiropractor, osteopath, Aboriginal health practitioner (if clinically indicated)

Maximising Medicare Allocation:

With 5 individual visits per year, prioritise referrals based on patient's greatest needs: e.g., respiratory physio for technique, psychologist for anxiety, dietitian for weight/triggers.

The Asthma Care Plan Interview: 10 Structured Steps

Follow these steps to conduct a comprehensive, patient-centred asthma review. Each step includes context, suggested approach, patient response, and key clinical points.

Step 1: Opening & Building Rapport
Setting the tone, explaining the appointment, addressing concerns
1

Context

Sarah arrives for her scheduled appointment. She's familiar with the practice but may feel anxious about her recent symptoms. Your first task is to build trust, explain what you'll be doing, and set expectations for the 45-minute interview.

Suggested Opening:

"Hi Sarah, thanks for coming in today. I'm going to spend some time with you reviewing how your asthma has been going. We'll talk about your symptoms, your medications, and any triggers you've noticed. I'll also check your inhaler technique and ask about your overall wellbeing. After we're done, I'll write up my notes for Dr [GP name] to review and finalise your care plan. Does that sound okay? Do you have any concerns or questions before we start?"

What to Listen For:
  • Patient's understanding of asthma and their condition
  • Anxiety or concerns about symptoms
  • Readiness to engage in the review
  • Any immediate issues they want to raise

"Thanks. I'm a bit worried actually. I've been getting more wheezy lately, especially at work. I thought my asthma was under control, but it doesn't feel like it. I'm using my blue inhaler more often than I used to. And I'm not sure I'm using my other inhaler correctly—I've been using it for years, so I just assumed I was doing it right."

Clinical Reasoning:

Sarah has identified two important issues: (1) increased symptoms and reliever use (suggesting loss of control), and (2) uncertainty about inhaler technique. These are common problems in asthma management. Reassure her that this is exactly what the review is for, and that many people need technique checks. This sets a collaborative tone and validates her concerns.

Record in Structured Notes:
  • Patient's chief concern: "Increased wheeze at work, increased reliever use"
  • Patient's understanding: "Thought asthma was controlled; now concerned"
  • Readiness to engage: "Yes, wants to understand technique"

Step 2: General Wellbeing Check
Holistic health, mood, stress, sleep, quality of life
2

Context

Before diving into asthma-specific questions, assess Sarah's overall wellbeing. Stress, poor sleep, and mood disorders can worsen asthma control. As a teacher, she may be under significant pressure, especially during term time.

Suggested Questions:
  • "How have you been feeling in general—mood, energy, stress levels?"
  • "How's work been? Any particular stressors?"
  • "How's your sleep? Are you waking up at night?"
  • "How is your asthma affecting your daily life—work, exercise, family activities?"
  • "Have you noticed any patterns—does your asthma get worse at certain times or with certain activities?"
Assessment Techniques:
  • Observe body language and tone (signs of anxiety or distress)
  • Listen for mentions of sleep disruption, fatigue, or mood changes
  • Explore work-related triggers (classroom dust, chalk, stress)

"Work has been really busy—it's term time, and we've had a lot of illness going around the school. I've had a couple of colds myself. I'm sleeping okay, but I do wake up sometimes feeling a bit wheezy. My partner says I'm more stressed than usual. The asthma is definitely affecting things—I can't run with the kids like I used to, and I get tired more easily. I'm also a bit frustrated because I feel like I should have this under control by now."

Clinical Reasoning:

Sarah has identified several important factors: (1) work-related triggers (viral exposure, stress), (2) nocturnal symptoms (waking wheezy—sign of poor control), (3) stress and frustration (psychological factors), and (4) reduced activity tolerance (quality of life impact). These are all relevant to asthma management and should be addressed in the care plan. Nocturnal symptoms are a red flag for inadequate control.

Record:
  • Mood: Stressed, frustrated; no depression screening needed at this stage but monitor
  • Sleep: Waking with wheeze (nocturnal symptoms—sign of poor control)
  • Work triggers: Viral exposure, classroom environment, stress
  • Quality of life: Reduced exercise tolerance, impact on family activities
Steps explored: 2 / 10
Step 3: Asthma Control Assessment
Symptoms, triggers, exacerbations, control status
3

Context

Now assess Sarah's asthma control in detail. Use validated tools (ACT, ACQ-5) and explore symptom patterns, triggers, and exacerbation history. Her recent ACT score of 18/25 suggests suboptimal control—this needs to be explored and addressed.

Suggested Questions & Assessment:
  • "Over the past 4 weeks, how many days have you had asthma symptoms (wheeze, cough, shortness of breath)?"
  • "How many nights per week do you wake up with asthma symptoms?"
  • "How often do you use your blue reliever inhaler? Is it more than twice a week?"
  • "What triggers your asthma? (e.g., exercise, cold air, allergens, stress, infections)"
  • "Have you had any asthma attacks or exacerbations in the past year? How many?"
  • "Have you ever been to hospital or ED for asthma?"
  • "Do you have an asthma action plan? Do you know what to do if your symptoms get worse?"
Validated Tool:
  • Administer Asthma Control Test (ACT) if not done recently (score ≥20 = well controlled)
  • Or use Asthma Control Questionnaire (ACQ-5) (score <1.5 = well controlled)

"I'd say I have symptoms maybe 3 or 4 days a week—mostly when I'm at work or after exercise. I wake up wheezy maybe twice a week. I'm using my blue inhaler probably 4 or 5 times a week, sometimes more. My triggers are definitely exercise, cold air, and when I've had a cold. I had two bad episodes last winter where I felt really tight and had to take a lot of puffs. I didn't go to hospital, but I was pretty worried. I do have an asthma action plan somewhere, but it's old—I think it's from when I was first diagnosed. I'm not really sure what to do if things get worse."

Clinical Reasoning:

Sarah's asthma is NOT well controlled. Red flags include: (1) symptoms 3–4 days/week, (2) nocturnal symptoms twice/week, (3) reliever use 4–5 times/week (well above the "twice a week" threshold for well-controlled asthma), (4) exercise-induced symptoms, (5) two exacerbations in past year, and (6) outdated action plan. According to the 2025 Australian Asthma Handbook, this patient needs step-up therapy and a current written action plan. This is a key finding to communicate to the GP.

Record:
  • Asthma control status: NOT well controlled
  • Symptom frequency: 3–4 days/week
  • Nocturnal symptoms: 2 nights/week
  • Reliever use: 4–5 times/week (excessive)
  • Triggers: Exercise, cold air, viral infections, work stress
  • Exacerbations: 2 in past 12 months (winter)
  • Action plan: Outdated (3 years); needs update
  • Recommendation for GP: Consider step-up therapy (e.g., increase ICS dose or add LTRA); provide current written action plan
Steps explored: 3 / 10
Step 4: Medication Review & Adherence
Current medications, adherence, technique, side effects
4

Context

Review Sarah's current medications in detail. Poor adherence and incorrect inhaler technique are common reasons for apparent treatment failure. This is a critical step for identifying modifiable factors.

Suggested Questions:
  • "Tell me about your maintenance inhaler (the one you use every day). How often do you use it?"
  • "Do you use it exactly as prescribed, or do you sometimes skip doses?"
  • "What time of day do you use it? Do you have a routine?"
  • "Have you noticed any side effects—thrush, hoarseness, cough?"
  • "Can you show me how you use your inhaler? Let's check your technique together."
  • "Are there any barriers to using your inhaler regularly—cost, forgetting, not feeling like you need it?"
Inhaler Technique Assessment:
  • Ask patient to demonstrate their technique with a placebo or their own inhaler
  • Check for common errors: not shaking, not breathing out first, not holding breath, poor coordination
  • Assess whether they rinse mouth after use (to prevent thrush)

"I'm supposed to use it twice a day, but honestly, I probably only use it once a day, sometimes less. When I'm feeling okay, I think 'why do I need it?' and I skip it. I don't have a routine—I just use it when I remember. I haven't noticed side effects. Let me show you how I use it... [demonstrates]. I think I'm doing it right, but I'm not sure. I don't always rinse my mouth after. I haven't had thrush or anything, so I thought it was okay."

Clinical Reasoning:

Sarah has identified a major adherence problem: she's using her maintenance inhaler (ICS/LABA) only once daily instead of twice, and skipping doses when she feels well. This is a common misconception—patients often think they only need maintenance therapy when symptomatic. This is a KEY MODIFIABLE FACTOR that likely explains her poor control. Additionally, she's not rinsing her mouth, which increases thrush risk. Her technique may also have errors that reduce drug delivery. This needs to be addressed with education and a clear explanation of why maintenance therapy is essential even when feeling well.

Record:
  • Maintenance inhaler: Fluticasone/Salmeterol 125/25, prescribed BD
  • Actual use: Once daily or less (poor adherence)
  • Reason for non-adherence: "Feels well, thinks doesn't need it"
  • Technique: Demonstrated; appears reasonable but needs reinforcement
  • Mouth rinsing: Not done consistently
  • Side effects: None reported
  • Key finding: Poor adherence is likely major contributor to poor control
  • Recommendation for GP: Patient education on importance of maintenance therapy; consider referral to respiratory physiotherapist for technique check and adherence support

Step 5: Complication Screening #1 – Exacerbation Risk & Severity
History of severe exacerbations, hospitalisations, near-fatal attacks
5

Context

Assess Sarah's risk of severe exacerbations. Patients with a history of severe attacks, hospitalisations, or near-fatal asthma are at higher risk and need closer monitoring and more aggressive management.

Suggested Questions:
  • "Have you ever had a really severe asthma attack—where you couldn't catch your breath or felt very frightened?"
  • "Have you ever been to hospital or the emergency department for asthma?"
  • "Have you ever needed oral steroids (prednisone) for asthma?"
  • "Have you ever been intubated or admitted to ICU for asthma?"
  • "What was the worst attack you've had? What happened?"
  • "Do you have any warning signs that an attack is coming?"
Risk Stratification:
  • History of ICU admission, intubation, or near-fatal asthma = HIGH RISK
  • ≥2 exacerbations requiring oral steroids in past year = HIGH RISK
  • ≥1 hospitalisation in past year = MODERATE-HIGH RISK

"I've had two bad episodes in the past year, especially in winter. I felt really tight and wheezy, and I was using my blue inhaler a lot. I didn't go to hospital, but I was worried I might need to. I haven't had oral steroids or anything like that. I've never been to hospital for asthma. The worst attack I remember was years ago when I was first diagnosed—I was really scared and couldn't breathe properly. I know when an attack is coming because I get a tight feeling in my chest and I start coughing more."

Clinical Reasoning:

Sarah has had 2 exacerbations in the past year but has not required hospitalisation or oral steroids. This puts her at MODERATE risk. However, the fact that she's having frequent exacerbations (2 in 12 months) despite being on maintenance therapy suggests her current regimen is inadequate. She has good awareness of warning signs (chest tightness, increased cough), which is positive. The goal is to prevent future exacerbations through better control and a clear action plan.

Record:
  • Exacerbations in past 12 months: 2 (winter)
  • Hospitalisations: None
  • Oral steroids: None in past year
  • ICU/intubation: Never
  • Risk stratification: MODERATE (2 exacerbations/year)
  • Warning signs: Chest tightness, increased cough
  • Recommendation for GP: Step-up therapy; ensure current action plan; consider referral to respiratory physio for education and monitoring

Step 6: Complication Screening #2 – Comorbidities & Triggers
Allergic rhinitis, GORD, anxiety, obesity, smoking exposure
6

Context

Assess comorbidities that can worsen asthma control. Sarah already has allergic rhinitis and GORD documented, but we need to explore their impact and identify any other contributing factors.

Suggested Questions:
  • "How is your hay fever or allergies? Are they well controlled?"
  • "Do you have heartburn or reflux? Does it affect your asthma?"
  • "Do you have anxiety or worry about your asthma? Does stress make it worse?"
  • "Are you exposed to smoke at home or work?"
  • "Do you have pets? Any allergies to animals?"
  • "What about dust, mould, or damp in your home?"
  • "Do you exercise regularly? What type?"
Assessment Focus:
  • Allergic rhinitis: Often coexists with asthma; poor control worsens asthma
  • GORD: Reflux can trigger asthma, especially at night
  • Anxiety: Can worsen symptoms and reduce adherence
  • Environmental triggers: Smoke, allergens, occupational exposures
  • Weight: Obesity can worsen asthma control

"My hay fever is pretty bad in spring and summer. I take antihistamines, but I still get sneezy and stuffy. I think it makes my asthma worse during those months. My reflux is usually okay, but I do get heartburn sometimes, especially at night. I've noticed that when I have reflux, my asthma feels worse the next day. I'm a bit anxious about my asthma—I worry about having an attack at work where I can't get help quickly. My partner smokes, and I've asked him to smoke outside, but he sometimes smokes in the house. We have a cat, but I've had her for years and don't think I'm allergic. I try to exercise, but I get wheezy when I run, so I've been doing less. I know I've put on weight—I'm heavier than I'd like to be."

Clinical Reasoning:

Sarah has identified several modifiable comorbidities and triggers: (1) uncontrolled allergic rhinitis (worsens asthma seasonally), (2) GORD with nocturnal symptoms (triggers asthma), (3) anxiety about asthma (can worsen symptoms and reduce adherence), (4) passive smoking exposure (major trigger), (5) exercise-induced symptoms (limiting activity), and (6) overweight status (BMI 28.5—obesity worsens asthma). These are all addressable factors that should be included in the care plan. The anxiety is particularly important—she may benefit from psychological support or education to reduce fear and improve self-management.

Record:
  • Allergic rhinitis: Seasonal (spring/summer), suboptimal control, worsens asthma
  • GORD: Nocturnal symptoms, triggers asthma
  • Anxiety: Worried about asthma attacks at work; may benefit from support
  • Environmental triggers: Passive smoking (partner), cat (long-standing, no allergy)
  • Exercise: Limited due to wheeze (exercise-induced bronchoconstriction)
  • Weight: BMI 28.5 (overweight); patient aware, willing to address
  • Recommendations for GP: Optimise rhinitis control; review GORD management; consider psychology referral for anxiety; smoking cessation counselling for partner; weight management support; exercise physiology referral for safe exercise prescription

Step 7: Lung Function & Preventive Screening
Spirometry, peak flow, asthma action plan, immunisations
7

Context

Review Sarah's spirometry results and assess whether preventive measures are in place. Her declining FEV1 (78% vs 85% previously) is concerning and needs investigation.

Suggested Questions & Assessment:
  • "Have you had spirometry done recently? Do you know your results?"
  • "Do you check your peak flow at home? How often?"
  • "Do you have an asthma action plan? When was it last updated?"
  • "Do you know what to do if your symptoms get worse?"
  • "Have you had your flu vaccine this year? Pneumococcal vaccine?"
Interpretation of Spirometry:
  • FEV1 78% predicted (down from 85%)—declining, concerning
  • FEV1/FVC 0.72—normal, suggests asthma (not COPD)
  • Peak flow 380 L/min (below predicted 400–500)—suboptimal
  • Interpretation: Asthma with suboptimal control; possible fixed airway obstruction if not reversible

"I had spirometry done last year, and the doctor said my numbers were okay. I don't check my peak flow at home—I didn't know I should. I have an asthma action plan somewhere, but like I said, it's really old. I'm not sure what to do if things get worse—I guess I'd just use my blue inhaler more and call the doctor if it didn't help. I had my flu vaccine last year, but I'm not sure about pneumococcal. I should probably get that done."

Clinical Reasoning:

Sarah's spirometry shows a concerning decline in FEV1 (78% vs 85% last year). This could indicate: (1) worsening asthma control, (2) development of fixed airway obstruction, or (3) poor technique during testing. This needs to be repeated and investigated. She doesn't monitor peak flow at home, which would help her track control. Her action plan is outdated and she doesn't know what to do in an exacerbation—this is a safety gap. She's not up-to-date with preventive vaccines. All of these are addressable.

Record:
  • Spirometry: FEV1 78% predicted (declining from 85%); FEV1/FVC 0.72 (normal)
  • Peak flow: 380 L/min (below predicted); not monitored at home
  • Asthma action plan: Outdated (3 years); needs urgent update
  • Patient knowledge: Doesn't know what to do in exacerbation (safety gap)
  • Vaccinations: Flu vaccine up-to-date; pneumococcal status unknown
  • Red flag: Declining FEV1—needs investigation; possible fixed obstruction or poor control
  • Recommendations for GP: Repeat spirometry; provide written action plan; patient education on peak flow monitoring; ensure pneumococcal vaccine; consider respiratory physio referral for education and monitoring

Step 9: Mental Health & Psychosocial Wellbeing
Anxiety, depression, coping, support, asthma-related distress
9

Context

Assess Sarah's mental health and psychological wellbeing. Anxiety about asthma can worsen symptoms and reduce adherence. Depression can reduce motivation for self-management.

Suggested Questions:
  • "How are you feeling emotionally? Any anxiety or worry?"
  • "Do you feel anxious about your asthma? What worries you most?"
  • "Have you felt down or depressed lately?"
  • "How is your asthma affecting your mood or quality of life?"
  • "Do you have good support from family and friends?"

Screening Tools (if indicated):
  • PHQ-9 (depression screening)
  • GAD-7 (anxiety screening)
  • Asthma-related anxiety scale

"I do feel anxious about my asthma, especially at work. I worry about having an attack and not being able to get help. I've had moments where I feel a bit tight and I panic, which makes it worse. She would likely benefit from psychological support to address anxiety and improve coping strategies. This could include cognitive-behavioural therapy (CBT) or asthma-specific counselling. Improving asthma control (through better adherence and step-up therapy) will also help reduce anxiety.

Record:
  • Mood: Not depressed; frustrated about asthma limitations

  • Support: Good family support; partner supportive
  • Coping: Recognises anxiety worsens symptoms; open to psychological support
  • Recommendation for GP: Psychology referral for asthma-related anxiety and coping strategies; may benefit from CBT or asthma-specific counselling

Step 10: Referrals, Goals & Wrapping Up
Summarise findings, recommend referrals, set goals, document for GP
10

Context

Conclude the interview by summarising key findings, recommending allied health referrals, and setting goals with Sarah. Document everything clearly for the GP to review and finalise the care plan.

Suggested Closing:

"Sarah, thank you for sharing all of this information with me. I can see that your asthma hasn't been as well controlled as you'd like, and we've identified some things that can help. Here's what I'm hearing: your maintenance inhaler isn't being used as prescribed, which is a big factor. You're also dealing with allergies, reflux, and anxiety, which all affect asthma. And you'd like to exercise more and lose weight, which would really help. I'm going to write all of this up for Dr [GP name] to review. I'd like to recommend a few things: (1) a respiratory physiotherapist to help with your inhaler technique and give you strategies to manage your asthma better, (2) a psychologist to help with the anxiety and give you coping tools, and (3) an exercise physiologist to help you exercise safely. We can also think about a dietitian if you'd like support with weight management. Dr [GP name] will review all of this and decide what's best for you. Do you have any questions? What's one goal you'd like to work on first?"

"That makes sense. I think the biggest thing for me is getting my asthma under control so I can exercise again and not worry so much. I'm definitely interested in seeing a physiotherapist to check my technique and a psychologist to help with the anxiety. I'm not sure about the dietitian yet, but maybe. I want to try to use my inhaler properly and regularly first, and see if that helps. When will I hear from the doctor?"

Clinical Reasoning:

Sarah has identified her priorities: asthma control, exercise, and anxiety management. She's engaged and motivated. She's agreed to respiratory physio and psychology referrals, and is open to dietitian support. She understands that improving inhaler adherence is a key first step. This is a good outcome—she's collaborative and realistic. The GP will review all findings and finalise the care plan, including medication adjustments, referrals, and a written action plan.

Summary for GP Review:
  • Key Findings: Asthma not well controlled (ACT 18/25); poor adherence to maintenance therapy; 2 exacerbations in past year; declining FEV1; nocturnal symptoms; exercise-induced symptoms; asthma-related anxiety
  • Modifiable Factors: Poor adherence (using maintenance inhaler once daily instead of twice); suboptimal allergic rhinitis control; GORD; anxiety; overweight (BMI 28.5); passive smoking exposure; exercise limitation
  • Patient Goals: Improve asthma control, exercise safely, manage anxiety
  • Recommended Referrals: (1) Respiratory physiotherapist (inhaler technique, education, monitoring), (2) Psychologist (asthma-related anxiety, coping strategies), (3) Exercise physiologist (safe exercise prescription, EIB management), (4) Dietitian (weight management—optional, patient to decide)

Red Flags & Escalation Triggers ▼

Alert GP Immediately If:

  • Severe dyspnoea or chest tightness at rest
  • Inability to speak full sentences
  • Cyanosis or altered consciousness
  • Peak flow <50% of personal best
  • No improvement with reliever inhaler
  • Recurrent exacerbations (≥2 in 12 months)
  • Declining lung function on spirometry
  • Severe anxiety or panic related to asthma
  • Non-adherence with high-risk features
Asthma Action Plan Template ▼

Green Zone (Well Controlled)

  • No symptoms or symptoms ≤2 days/week
  • Use maintenance inhaler as prescribed (e.g., 2 puffs BD)
  • Use reliever as needed (≤2 days/week)

Yellow Zone (Caution)

  • Increased symptoms, cough, wheeze, or shortness of breath
  • Waking at night with asthma
  • Using reliever >2 days/week
  • Peak flow 50–80% of personal best
  • Action: Increase maintenance therapy; use reliever every 4–6 hours; contact GP if no improvement in 24 hours

Red Zone (Medical Emergency)

  • Severe shortness of breath at rest
  • Cannot speak full sentences
  • Severe chest tightness or pain
  • Peak flow <50% of personal best
  • No improvement with reliever inhaler
  • Action: Call 000 (ambulance); use reliever every 5–15 minutes while waiting
Inhaler Technique Checklist ▼

Metered Dose Inhaler (MDI) with Spacer

  1. Shake the inhaler well for 10 seconds
  2. Attach spacer to inhaler
  3. Breathe out fully (away from spacer)
  4. Place spacer mouthpiece in mouth (seal lips around it)
  5. Press inhaler once to release one puff into spacer
  6. Breathe in and out slowly and deeply 5–6 times (or until mist disappears)
  7. Wait 30–60 seconds between puffs
  8. Repeat steps 1–7 for second puff (if prescribed)
  9. Rinse mouth with water after use (especially with ICS)

Common Errors to Correct

  • Not shaking the inhaler
  • Not breathing out first
  • Breathing in too quickly
  • Not holding breath or breathing out immediately
  • Not waiting between puffs
  • Not rinsing mouth after ICS use
GPCCMP Referral Process (from 1 July 2025) ▼

Key Changes

  • No EPC form required: Direct GP letter to allied health provider
  • Referral validity: 18 months from first session
  • Individual visits: Up to 5 per calendar year (10 for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander patients)
  • Group sessions: Up to 8 additional per year (T2DM only)

Referral Letter Should Include

  • Patient name, DOB, Medicare number
  • Diagnosis and clinical indication for referral
  • Relevant pathology or investigation results
  • Current medications
  • Specific goals or areas to address
  • Any precautions or contraindications
