Headache Assessment and Management for Paramedics

A walk-through scenario for skills practice, clinical reasoning, and crew discussion.
Based on SA Ambulance Service Headache CPG-172-P.

2-Person Paramedic Crew 90 Minutes Regional / Rural Setting Paramedic Scope

Learning Objectives

On completion, participants will be able to:

  • Conduct a thorough headache-focused history and neurological assessment in the prehospital setting
  • Differentiate primary from secondary headache using systematic red flag screening
  • Apply ROSIER/ACT and gait assessment as part of the neurological examination
  • Manage a medically diagnosed migraine presentation per CPG-172-P (symptom control + transport decision-making)
  • Safely prepare and administer IV metoclopramide 10 mg via slow push over ≥ 3 minutes
  • Use IV NaCl 0.9% rehydration in 250 mL aliquots up to 20 mL/kg when oral fluids aren’t tolerated
  • Identify when escalation, early transport, and pre-notification are required
  • Deliver a structured ISBAR handover for a headache presentation

  • Primary headaches are common and usually benign (migraine, tension-type, cluster)
  • Secondary headaches are less common but can be life-threatening (e.g., SAH, meningitis, raised ICP)
  • Paramedics are not aiming to diagnose headache type prehospital — the operational priority is red flag identification, symptom management, and transport for further assessment
  • Symptomatic relief from analgesia is not evidence of a benign cause
  • Complete a pain assessment and a neurological assessment, including ROSIER/ACT and gait where feasible

Break Point 1 Discussion
En-route planning

Facilitator prompts

  • What’s your differential before arrival?
  • What red flags will you actively screen for?
  • What kit goes in first?

Expected reasoning

  • “Known migraine” informs context but does not exclude secondary headache
  • Plan roles: one leads history/neuro; one gets baseline obs, BGL, prepares IV/meds

Key teaching points

  • Don’t anchor on “migraine” from the MDT
  • Long transport = early symptom care + deliberate reassessment plan

Real-job translation

On the way in, agree on your working plan and what would trigger escalation/time-critical transport.

On Arrival – Scene Management

Porch light on. Partner meets you. Bedroom darkened, patient lying still with emesis bowl beside bed.

  • Scene safe; quiet environment maintained
  • Minimise light/noise to support assessment and reduce distress
  • Plan a low-stimulus patient movement strategy early (carry chair/stretcher)
Break Point 2 Discussion
Doorway / first impression

Facilitator prompts

  • What does the environment and patient posture suggest?
  • What’s reassuring, and what is not reassuring?

Expected reasoning

  • Photophobia and lying still fits migraine behaviour but is not diagnostic
  • Still must complete red flag screen and neuro exam

Key teaching points

  • Environmental management is clinical care
  • History and neuro exam determine risk, not “migraine look”

Real-job translation

First impressions can help you pace the job and manage patient comfort, but they must not replace structured assessment.

Break Point 3 Practical / discussion
Initial observations & Corpuls3 setup

Facilitator prompts

  • What is your “baseline set” and what will you trend?
  • What is pain-mediated hypertension and how do you prove it?

Expected reasoning

  • Baseline obs before meds so treatment response is measurable
  • BP should be reassessed after symptom control

Key teaching points

  • Vitals don’t rule in/out secondary headache; they add context
  • Trend matters more than a single number

Real-job translation

Split tasks: one clinician talks; one gets objective data. It makes the job faster and safer.

Clinical Decision Point

Focused neuro exam

  • ROSIER/ACT: negative
  • Speech: clear
  • Face/arm: no drift or weakness
  • Pupils: equal/reactive
  • Gait: steady (slow due to nausea)
  • No neck stiffness; afebrile; no rash
Red flags that mandate transport (screen + document) Thunderclap/uncharacteristic onset; triggered by cough/exertion/sexual activity; systemic illness signs (ALOC, neck stiffness, fever, rash, vomiting + confusion); neuro signs/ALOC; first severe headache >50; cancer/HIV; worsening over hours–days; pregnancy; head trauma; features of raised ICP.
Break Point 4 Decision-making
Screening tools, protocols & working diagnosis

Facilitator prompts

  • Which red flags did you actively rule out, and how?
  • When does this become a Stroke pathway job?

Expected reasoning

  • Negative ROSIER/ACT + normal neuro supports migraine pathway, but does not “clear” serious causes without full red-flag screen
  • Any neuro deficit = manage per Stroke protocol; transport to CSU/STS

Key teaching points

  • Symptom relief is not proof of benign cause
  • Document negatives: “no neck stiffness”, “afebrile”, “no focal neuro”

Real-job translation

If you get challenged at handover, clear documentation of what you looked for and didn’t find matters.

Treatment Phase (CPG-172-P)

Operational aim Symptom management + safe transport decision-making after a structured red flag screen and neuro exam.
  1. Low-stimulus care: dark, quiet, minimal movement.
  2. IV access: establish and secure IV line for antiemetic/fluids if indicated.
  3. Antiemetic: consider metoclopramide 10 mg IM/IV (IV slow push ≥ 3 minutes).
  4. Rehydration: encourage oral fluids if tolerated. If not, NaCl 0.9% 250 mL aliquots up to 20 mL/kg with reassessment after each aliquot.
  5. Analgesia: per Pain Management protocol; reassess and trend.
  6. Reassess: pain, nausea/vomiting, neuro status, vitals.
Skill focus This is a medication safety drill: indication, cross-check, timing, monitoring, documentation.
  1. Confirm indication: adult with medically diagnosed migraine, current episode consistent with prior pattern.
  2. Check contraindications / risks: confirm as per local medication reference; document patient’s own meds already taken.
  3. Prepare dose: correct drug, concentration, expiry. Label syringe.
  4. Independent cross-check: drug, dose, route, patient, indication, expiry.
  5. Administer: IV slow push over ≥ 3 minutes (use a timer).
  6. Monitor: watch for dystonic/extrapyramidal effects; manage supportively and escalate if they occur.
  7. Document: time, dose, route, rate, response, adverse effects.
Break Point 5 Practical / discussion
Treatment initiation & medication safety

Facilitator prompts

  • Show the cross-check. What exactly are you verifying?
  • Time the slow push. No guessing.
  • What adverse effects are you watching for?

Expected reasoning

  • Medication safety = preparation + cross-check + administration rate + monitoring + documentation
  • Slow push reduces adverse-effect risk and is a protocol requirement

Key teaching points

  • Use a timer for ≥ 3 minutes
  • Be explicit about what the patient already took (e.g., triptan)

Real-job translation

Busy scenes and low light are where errors happen. Slow down for high-risk steps.

Break Point 6 Decision-making
Reassessment & escalation decision

Facilitator prompts

  • She’s improved — does she still need transport?
  • What would make you upgrade to time-critical?

Expected reasoning

  • Improvement helps, but does not prove benign cause
  • Failed home management + severity + dehydration = transport is clinically appropriate

Key teaching points

  • Document capacity and safety netting if refusing transport
  • Trends + neuro stability guide risk assessment

Real-job translation

If there’s doubt, transport. You can’t undo a missed SAH.

Extrication & Transport

  • Carry chair to stretcher to minimise exertion and light exposure
  • Dim cabin lights; reduce noise; smooth driving
  • Emesis bag accessible; position of comfort (head elevated/lateral)
  • Obs and neuro reassessment every 15 minutes during 45-minute transport
  • Prepare ISBAR en route
Break Point 7 Discussion / practical
Extrication & transport planning

Facilitator prompts

  • How do you keep this job low-stimulus from bedroom to ED?
  • What are your in-transit priorities on a long run?

Expected reasoning

  • Transport is active care: reassess, trend, anticipate deterioration
  • Low-stimulus environment reduces nausea and improves patient tolerance

Key teaching points

  • Have clear triggers for escalation during transport

Real-job translation

Long transports are where subtle changes can be missed. Commit to scheduled reassessments.

Handover

ISBAR — Headache / Migraine
I: “I’m [name], paramedic. This is Sarah, 42.”
S: “Severe headache with vomiting x3, known medically diagnosed migraines. Current episode consistent with prior pattern but more severe; home triptan ineffective.”
B: “10-year migraine history. NKDA. No head trauma. Not pregnant. No cancer/HIV history disclosed.”
A: “GCS 15, ROSIER/ACT negative, no focal neuro, afebrile, no neck stiffness/rash. Baseline BP 148/88, HR 92.”
R: “Given metoclopramide 10 mg IV slow push ≥3 min, and IV NaCl 0.9% (first 250 mL aliquot). Now pain 4/10, nausea settling, vitals normalising. IV in situ, patent.”
Break Point 8 Practical skill
ISBAR handover

Facilitator prompts

  • Deliver ISBAR in 45–60 seconds.
  • Include your negative red-flag findings.

Expected reasoning

  • ED needs the story + what you ruled out + what you treated + response

Key teaching points

  • Clear negatives show structured assessment
  • Include patient’s own meds taken (e.g., sumatriptan)

Real-job translation

A structured handover improves continuity of care and reduces rework in ED.

Debrief (10–15 minutes)

Reflective questions

  1. What were your top 3 red flags to rule out and why?
  2. How did ROSIER/ACT and gait change your confidence in the working impression?
  3. What medication safety steps did you use for IV metoclopramide?
  4. How did you decide transport urgency?
  5. What would make you change pathway mid-transport?

Key takeaways

1) History is your most powerful tool. Headache risk is determined by onset, associated features, and patient context — not by how “migraine-like” it looks.
2) Symptom relief does not equal benign cause. Pain improvement after treatment is useful data, but it does not exclude serious pathology.
3) Red flags must be screened and documented every time. Document positives and negatives (e.g., no neck stiffness, afebrile, no focal neuro).
4) Metoclopramide IV must be slow (≥ 3 minutes). Use a timer. Monitor for dystonic/extrapyramidal reactions.
5) When in doubt — transport. Particularly with thunderclap onset, neuro signs, systemic illness features, pregnancy, or atypical pattern.

Optional Scenario Variations

Variation 1 — Thunderclap headache (suspected SAH)

Sudden-onset “worst headache” during exertion, peaks in seconds, neck stiffness and/or ALOC. Treat as time-critical and transport for urgent workup.

Variation 2 — Headache with focal neurology (Stroke pathway)

Any focal deficit or positive ROSIER/ACT: manage per Stroke protocol and transport to CSU/STS as indicated.

Variation 3 — Pregnancy + severe headache (possible pre-eclampsia)

Severe headache in pregnancy with/without hypertension: treat as high risk, escalate early, pre-notify, time-critical transport.

Variation 4 — Fever, neck stiffness, rash (meningitis/meningococcal)

Systemic illness features: manage per Sepsis pathway and treat as time-critical with early pre-notification.
