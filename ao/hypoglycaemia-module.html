📹 Introduction

An overview of hypoglycaemia recognition and the Ambulance Officer treatment pathway, including BGL measurement and glucagon administration.

🧠 Why This Matters

Hypoglycaemia — abnormally low blood glucose — is one of the most time-critical and treatable emergencies you will encounter. The brain depends almost entirely on glucose for fuel. When levels fall below 3.5 mmol/L, cognitive function deteriorates rapidly.

Without prompt treatment, hypoglycaemia can progress from confusion and behavioural changes to unconsciousness, seizures, and permanent brain injury.

The good news: intervention is straightforward. Oral glucose is first-line for conscious patients who can swallow. When that's not possible, intramuscular glucagon is your key treatment at AO scope. Knowing how to recognise the signs, measure BGL accurately, and prepare glucagon from a powder vial can make the difference between a routine call and a critical one.

Glucagon must be reconstituted from a dry powder before administration — a skill that requires familiarity with the drawing up procedure (CPPRO-010) to perform safely under pressure.

⚠️ Critical Reminder

Always measure BGL on any patient with altered conscious state, altered behaviour, trauma, or seizures — especially children. A presentation that looks like intoxication, stroke, or behavioural disturbance may be hypoglycaemia.

📚 Key Knowledge

Mild–Moderate (BGL typically 2.5–3.5 mmol/L):

  • Confusion, altered behaviour, irritability
  • Diaphoresis (sweating) — often profuse
  • Tremor, palpitations, tachycardia
  • Hunger, nausea
  • Pallor, weakness, dizziness
  • Slurred speech, poor coordination

Severe (BGL < 2.5 mmol/L or symptomatic decline):

  • Decreased GCS / unconsciousness
  • Seizures
  • Focal neurological signs (may mimic stroke)
  • Coma (if prolonged and untreated)
💡 Clinical Tip

In children, hypoglycaemia often presents primarily as behavioural change, lethargy, or irritability rather than classic adult symptoms. Maintain a high index of suspicion and check BGL early.

  • Perform hand hygiene
  • Ensure meter is calibrated (lot number matches test strip foil packets)
  • Clean sampling site with alcohol swab — allow to dry
  • Use lancet to obtain capillary blood sample (fingertip)
  • Insert blue test strip, apply blood to white test area
  • Read result after 5-second countdown

Normal Ranges:

  • Adults & children: > 3.5 mmol/L
  • Neonates: > 2.5 mmol/L

Paediatric Note: In small infants and newborns, heel prick testing may be the most appropriate sampling location.

What is Glucagon? A hormone that stimulates the liver to convert stored glycogen into glucose, raising blood glucose levels.

AO Scope — Indication:

  • Hypoglycaemia where oral treatment is ineffective or inappropriate — IM route only

Dosage:

  • ≥ 6 years (or ≥ 25 kg): 1 mg IM
  • ≥ 2 to < 6 years (or < 25 kg): 0.5 mg IM
  • < 2 years: Consult EOC clinician — may advise 0.5 mg IM

Key Pharmacology:

  • Onset: Response should occur within 10 minutes
  • Duration: 1–2 hours
  • If no response within 10 mins → IV glucose needed — escalate

Important Considerations:

  • IV glucose is preferred where available (not standard AO scope)
  • Hypoglycaemia from gliclazide (Diamicron® MR, Glyade® MR) is typically protracted — vigilant BGL monitoring required
  • Following reversal, encourage complex carbohydrates
  • Glucagon may be less effective in patients with depleted glycogen stores (starvation, chronic alcohol use, liver disease)

Presentation: Glucagon is supplied as a glass vial containing 1 mg dry powder. It must be reconstituted with 1 mL of water for injection (0.9% saline is acceptable).

⚠️ Key Principle

Always draw up the diluent first, then inject it into the medication vial. Prepare in a clean area with minimal distractions.

Equipment Required:

  • Glucagon vial (1 mg dry powder)
  • Water for injection — 1 mL plastic ampoule (or 0.9% saline)
  • Blunt fill needle (18g)
  • Appropriately sized syringe
  • IM needle (23–25g)
  • 70% isopropyl alcohol swab
  • Sharps container

Step-by-Step Method:

  1. Complete all pre-preparation medication checks
  2. Perform hand hygiene
  3. Prepare a clean work area (use a blue sheet if needed)
  4. Open syringe, blunt fill needle, and IM needle packages — peel from corner, leave in plastic cradles
  5. Draw up diluent first: Twist open the plastic ampoule of water for injection. Attach syringe directly, withdraw 1 mL
  6. Separate ampoule from syringe
  7. Attach the blunt fill needle to the syringe
  8. Remove protective cap from the glucagon vial
  9. Scrub the rubber stopper with alcohol swab — allow to dry
  10. Insert needle through the rubber stopper
  11. Inject the 1 mL diluent into the vial
  12. Agitate the vial (needle/syringe still in situ) until powder is fully dissolved
  13. Invert the vial and withdraw the required dose
  14. Withdraw needle and syringe from the vial
  15. Discard blunt fill needle into sharps container
  16. Attach IM needle (23–25g)
  17. Expel air and prime syringe to the required dose with IM needle attached

Dose Calculation:

Desired dose (mg) ÷ Total prepared dose (mg) × Total volume (mL) = Volume to give
• 1 mg dose: 1 ÷ 1 × 1 mL = 1 mL (full vial)
• 0.5 mg dose: 0.5 ÷ 1 × 1 mL = 0.5 mL

Administration: IM injection into the lateral thigh or deltoid.

If in doubt about the preparation at any point — discard and prepare a new syringe.

When to Label:

  • Any medication not being immediately administered in full must be labelled
  • Prepare and label one medicine at a time

Syringe Labels:

  • Use small coloured labels conforming to AS/NZS 4375
  • If no specific label available, use a blank (white) label with: generic name, total dose, total volume (mL)

Important Rules:

  • Labels must not obscure graduation marks
  • If no labels available — write drug name and dose on syringe with permanent marker
  • Discard any medication not clearly labelled or where there is any doubt

Infection Control: All equipment is single use. Report any sharps injury via Incident Report.

📝 Knowledge Check

🎯 Key Takeaways

📊
Always check the BGL. Any patient with altered consciousness, confusion, behavioural changes, trauma, or seizures needs a BGL — it takes seconds and changes management entirely.
🥤
Least invasive first. Oral glucose up to 15 g is first-line if the patient can swallow safely. IM glucagon is second-line — consult EOC, reconstitute from powder with 1 mL WFI, and know your dose by age/weight.
⏱️
Monitor, prevent recurrence, escalate early. Expect glucagon response within 10 minutes (duration 1–2 hours only). Recheck BGL, offer complex carbohydrates, and be especially vigilant with gliclazide-induced hypoglycaemia.
