Sepsis — Volunteer AO Scenario

Walk-through scenario exercise with integrated break points for skills practice, clinical discussion, and operational decision-making.

SAAS Sepsis — AO (CPP-062-AO) Pre-Hospital / Rural 2 x Vol AO ~90 minutes

Title & Context

A 71-year-old male at a rural farmhouse has been unwell for two days with increasing confusion, fever, and reduced urine output. His wife calls when he becomes difficult to rouse and feels “burning hot.” A 2-person Volunteer AO crew responds from the local station.

Source Protocol: SAAS Sepsis — AO Protocol (CPP-062-AO). NEWS2 Sepsis Scoring, EOC Clinician escalation, oxygen therapy, IM Ceftriaxone for suspected meningococcal, priority transport, and pre-notification.
Setting: Thursday evening, 2115 hours, late autumn. Rural farmhouse 12 km north-east of Tumby Bay, SA. Nearest hospital: Tumby Bay Hospital (~15 min drive). Limited backup. EOC Clinician available by phone. Nearest MedSTAR retrieval: Port Lincoln (~45 min road).

Learning Objectives

On completion of this scenario walk-through, participants will be able to:

  • Recognise the signs and symptoms of sepsis in an adult patient
  • Apply the NEWS2 Sepsis Scoring Calculator in conjunction with the NEWS2 Adult Sepsis Screening Tool (CTL 062)
  • Differentiate between suspected sepsis (SBP >100 mmHg) and suspected septic shock (SBP ≤100 mmHg)
  • Administer high-flow oxygen appropriately and identify when EOC consultation is required for chronic lung disease patients
  • Describe the indications, preparation, and administration of IM Ceftriaxone for suspected meningococcal septicaemia
  • Escalate to the EOC Clinician using structured communication
  • Prioritise extrication and transport — treatment should not delay transport
  • Deliver an ISBAR handover stating “suspected sepsis” or “suspected septic shock”

Pre-Brief (~10 minutes)

Review the following protocol content before commencing the scenario. Use the toggle sections below to expand each topic. Allocate crew roles: Clinician (patient contact, history, medications, EOC calls) and Driver (observations, Corpuls3, documentation, equipment preparation).

IV antibiotics, IV fluids, and vasopressors are NOT within Volunteer AO scope of practice.
These interventions require paramedic or medical practitioner level care. The AO role is early recognition, basic care, oxygen, EOC escalation, IM Ceftriaxone (if directed for meningococcal), and rapid transport.

Sepsis is a life-threatening condition that arises when the body’s response to an infection damages its own tissues and organs. Early recognition and urgent treatment can save lives and prevent disability. Sepsis can be difficult to predict and diagnose.

Signs of sepsis can include one or more of the following:
  • Fast breathing or breathlessness
  • Fever and chills
  • Low body temperature
  • Low or no urine output
  • Fast or slow heartbeat
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Diarrhoea
  • Fatigue, confusion, or sleepiness
  • A lot of pain or “feeling worse than ever”
  • Headache
  • A new rash, discoloured or clammy sweaty skin

Almost any type of infection can lead to sepsis, including infections of the lungs, abdomen, urinary tract, skin, or other parts of the body.

The NEWS2 Sepsis Scoring Calculator in conjunction with the NEWS2 Adult Sepsis Screening Tool (CTL 062) is designed to assist clinicians in determining illness severity and support consistent clinical decision-making.

Key principle: Treatment should take priority over sepsis screening in those patients with clearly apparent infection and hypotension (SBP ≤100 mmHg). Do not delay treatment to complete a screening tool.

Use the NEWS2 tool for all patients with a suspected infection. The score guides escalation and transport priority.

Pathway 1: Suspected Sepsis — SBP >100 mmHg

  • Administer high-flow oxygen if SpO2 <94%
  • If chronic lung disease — consult EOC Clinician immediately regarding oxygen
  • Priority transport to appropriate facility
  • Consult for ongoing management
  • Notify stating “suspected sepsis”

Pathway 2: Suspected Sepsis — SBP ≤100 mmHg (Septic Shock)

  • Request early clinical support, stating “suspected septic shock”
  • Treatment should NOT delay extrication or transport
  • RV with clinical support en route if required
  • Administer high-flow oxygen
  • Priority transport to appropriate receiving facility
  • Notify stating “suspected septic shock”
Septic Shock (SBP ≤100 mmHg): This is time-critical. Request early clinical support immediately. Do not delay extrication or transport for on-scene treatment. Rendezvous with clinical support en route if required.

Meningococcal disease is an acute bacterial infection that can cause death within hours. Early administration of antibiotic therapy has been associated with reduced mortality.

Meningococcal rash progression: Starts as a simple spot → red pin-prick spots (petechiae) → widespread purple bruise-like appearance (purpura). The rash is a late sign. Absence of a rash does NOT exclude meningococcal septicaemia.
When to consider Ceftriaxone: If a patient presents febrile with an altered conscious state, with or without a petechial or purpuric rash — consult the EOC Clinician regarding the possibility of administering Ceftriaxone.

Clinical judgement is the best tool for early recognition of sepsis in children. The child with sepsis may be recognised by history or signs of infection, along with any of:

  • Fever ≥38°C or hypothermia <36°C
  • Altered conscious state
  • Marked or persistent tachycardia
  • Signs of toxicity: decreased alertness/arousal/activity, pale or mottled with cool peripheries, weak cry or grunting, rigors, bounding or weak pulses
  • Non-blanching rash
  • Hypotension
  • Unexplained generalised pain

AND any of: ANY purple zone observation on RDR chart, two or more red zone observations, additional response criteria on RDR chart, or serious clinical concern.

Paediatric Risk Factors

  • Age <3 months
  • Indwelling medical device
  • Unimmunised
  • Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander
  • Immunocompromised
  • Chronic disease or congenital disorder
  • Recent trauma, surgery, invasive procedure, or wound
  • Known malignancy

Practical Skill — IM Ceftriaxone Preparation (Demonstration and Discussion)

Note: In this scenario, the EOC Clinician has NOT directed Ceftriaxone because there are no meningococcal features. However, this is the ideal time to practise the preparation and administration technique for when it IS indicated. Use training stock or empty vials for this demonstration.

Indication for IM Ceftriaxone: Patient presents febrile with altered conscious state, WITH or WITHOUT petechial or purpuric rash, AND the EOC Clinician directs administration for suspected meningococcal septicaemia.

  1. Confirm EOC Clinician direction — Ceftriaxone is only administered on EOC Clinician instruction. Confirm: dose, any allergy concerns, and document the authorisation.
  2. Check for cephalosporin allergy — Ask the patient (or family): “Are you allergic to any antibiotics, particularly cephalosporins or penicillin?” If known cephalosporin hypersensitivity or immediate/severe allergy — do NOT administer. Consult EOC for SAAS Medical Practitioner advice for an alternative.
  3. Calculate the dose — 50 mg/kg up to a single maximum dose of 2 g. For Robert (estimated 85 kg): 50 x 85 = 4250 mg, capped at 2000 mg (2 g). Dose = 2 g.
  4. Prepare the medication — Take two 1 g vials of Ceftriaxone. For each vial: draw up 3.5 mL of sodium chloride 0.9% using a drawing-up needle and syringe. Inject the 3.5 mL into the Ceftriaxone vial. This makes 4 mL of solution at a concentration of 250 mg/mL.
  5. Reconstitute — Gently roll or swirl the vial until the powder is completely dissolved. Do NOT shake vigorously. The solution should be clear and pale yellow.
  6. Draw up the dose — Draw up 4 mL (1 g) from each reconstituted vial into separate syringes. You now have two syringes, each containing 1 g (4 mL).
  7. Select injection sites — Maximum 1 g (4 mL) per IM injection site. For a 2 g dose, you need TWO separate injection sites. Use the vastus lateralis (lateral thigh) or ventrogluteal site. One injection in each thigh, or one in each ventrogluteal site.
  8. Administer — Clean each site with an alcohol swab. Using a 21G 38 mm IM needle, insert at 90 degrees to the skin. Aspirate briefly. Inject slowly and steadily. Withdraw, apply pressure with gauze.
  9. Dispose and document — Dispose of sharps immediately into the sharps container. Document: drug name, dose (total and per site), time, route, sites used, EOC authorisation, and patient response.
Critical safety points:
  • NEVER administer without EOC Clinician direction
  • ALWAYS check for cephalosporin allergy before preparation
  • Maximum 1 g (4 mL) per injection site — a 2 g dose requires TWO sites
  • Use sodium chloride 0.9% for dilution (NOT water for injection for IM route)
  • Reconstituted solution should be clear — do not use if cloudy or contains particles

Break Point 6

Reassessment & Escalation Decision

Type: Decision-Making / Discussion

Facilitator Prompts
  • Compare the initial and reassessment observations — what trends do you see?
  • The BP has dropped from 92 to 88 — what does that tell you?
  • GCS has dropped from 9 to 8 — what is the clinical significance of GCS 8?
  • Mottling is appearing on the peripheries — what does this indicate?
  • What can you do on scene to change this patient’s trajectory? What can’t you do?
  • How does this change your transport urgency?
Expected AO Reasoning
  • Trends show deterioration: falling BP, rising HR, dropping GCS, mottling — septic shock is progressing
  • BP 88/54 confirms ongoing hypoperfusion — organs are not getting adequate blood flow
  • GCS 8 is the threshold for considering advanced airway management — at AO level, this means: position airway, suction if needed, OPA if tolerated, maintain oxygen, and prepare for potential further deterioration
  • Mottling = poor peripheral perfusion — a late sign of shock
  • On scene: oxygen (already on), positioning, monitoring. Cannot give: IV fluids, IV antibiotics, vasopressors — these require hospital
  • Transport urgency is maximum — load and go. Every minute on scene is a minute without definitive treatment
Key Teaching Points
  • Trends matter more than single numbers — a falling BP is more concerning than a single low reading
  • GCS 8 is a critical threshold — at AO level, focus on airway protection, positioning, and suction readiness
  • Mottling is a late sign of shock — if you see it, the patient is critically unwell
  • Recognise what you CAN do (oxygen, monitoring, transport) and what you CANNOT do (IV therapy) — this drives your decision to move
  • Early escalation saves lives — the EOC has already been notified, MedSTAR is aware
Real-Job Translation

On a real job, this is the moment where you stop assessing and start moving. The patient is deteriorating and nothing you can do on scene will reverse it. Your value is in getting this patient to definitive care as quickly and safely as possible. Call the Driver: “We need to move now.” Update the EOC if the patient has deteriorated since your last call. Every minute counts.

Extrication & Transport

Robert is deteriorating. The priority is rapid, safe extrication and transport to Tumby Bay Hospital (~15 min drive). The hospital has been pre-notified stating “suspected septic shock.”

Extrication Plan

  • Use the green carry chair to move Robert from the bedroom to the stretcher at the ambulance — he cannot walk (GCS 8, hypotensive)
  • Maintain oxygen via non-rebreather mask during transfer — do not disconnect
  • Two-person lift into carry chair — communicate clearly: “On three, we lift”
  • Jean (wife) to follow in her own vehicle or arrange transport — she cannot travel in the ambulance if the patient deteriorates and you need space to work
  • Secure on stretcher supine — do NOT elevate the head (hypotensive patient)
  • Continue Corpuls3 monitoring during transport — continuous SpO2, NIBP every 5 minutes
  • Have suction and OPA within arm’s reach — GCS 8, airway compromise risk
  • Have BVM assembled and ready — if GCS drops further or RR becomes inadequate
  • Blanket over the patient — maintain warmth, prevent heat loss during transfer to vehicle

Real-Job Translation

On a real job, this extrication takes 3–5 minutes. You and your partner lift Robert into the carry chair, wheel him to the ambulance, transfer to the stretcher. Oxygen stays on. Corpuls3 stays connected. You are in the back monitoring while the Driver navigates 4 km of dirt road before hitting sealed road. You are watching the monitor, watching the patient’s chest rise, and mentally preparing your handover. This is the sharp end of rural ambulance work.

Handover — ISBAR

On arrival at Tumby Bay Hospital, deliver a structured ISBAR handover to the receiving nurse or medical officer. The hospital has been pre-notified stating “suspected septic shock.”

—— ISBAR HANDOVER ——
I — Identify: “I’m [name], Volunteer AO from Tumby Bay. This is Robert Patterson, 71 years old.”
S — Situation: “Robert has suspected septic shock secondary to urosepsis. He has been unwell for two days with urinary symptoms progressing to fever, confusion, and haemodynamic compromise. His wife called when he became unrousable this evening.”
B — Background: “He has Type 2 Diabetes on Metformin and Gliclazide, CKD Stage 3, BPH on Tamsulosin, and Aspirin 100 mg daily. He has had three UTIs in the past 12 months. No known allergies. Has not eaten since yesterday and minimal fluid intake.”
A — Assessment: “On our arrival at 2125, GCS was 9, HR 118, BP 92/58, SpO2 91% on room air, RR 26, Temp 39.4, BGL 14.2. We applied high-flow oxygen via non-rebreather at 15 litres — SpO2 improved to 96%. We contacted the EOC Clinician who confirmed suspected septic shock pathway. No meningococcal features identified. On reassessment, he deteriorated: GCS dropped to 8, BP fell to 86/52, mottling appeared on peripheries. Current obs en route: HR 124, BP 86/52, SpO2 95% on high-flow, GCS 8.”
R — Recommendation: “He needs urgent IV access, IV antibiotics, and fluid resuscitation. BGL is elevated at 13.8 but likely stress-related. MedSTAR has been notified by the EOC for potential retrieval. His airway is currently maintained but GCS is 8 — he may need advanced airway management if he deteriorates further.”

Type: Practical Skill

Facilitator Prompts

  • What specific words must you use when handing over this patient?
  • Why is mentioning the trend in observations important?
  • What recommendation are you making and why?
  • What happens if the receiving team asks you questions you cannot answer?
Expected AO Reasoning
  • Structured ISBAR covering all elements concisely — practise delivering it in under 90 seconds
  • Must use the words “suspected septic shock” — this triggers the hospital’s sepsis pathway
  • Trends show deterioration (BP falling, GCS dropping) — this tells the receiving team the patient is getting worse, not stable
  • Recommendation: IV access, antibiotics, fluids, potential retrieval — shows you understand what the patient needs next
  • If asked something you cannot answer: “I don’t have that information, but the EOC Clinician was consulted at [time] and may have further details”
Key Teaching Points
  • ISBAR is a framework — hit every letter but keep it concise
  • The words “suspected septic shock” are a trigger phrase — use them clearly
  • Include trends, not just current numbers — “BP was 92, now 86” tells a story
  • Your recommendation shows clinical reasoning — it is not overstepping your scope to say what the patient needs
  • Hand over the ePCR or paperwork. Confirm the handover is acknowledged before you leave
Real-Job Translation

On a real job, you deliver this handover at the hospital bed or resuscitation bay. The receiving team may already be preparing IV access and antibiotics based on your pre-notification. Your handover confirms what they are expecting and adds the detail they need. Be concise, be clear, and do not leave until the handover is acknowledged. Then complete your ePCR documentation.

Debrief (10–15 minutes)

Gather the crew and work through the following reflective questions. There are no wrong answers — the goal is to consolidate learning and identify areas for further practice.

Reflective Questions

What were the key clinical findings that made you suspect sepsis? At what point did you identify this as septic shock rather than sepsis?
How did you prioritise your actions on scene? What did you do first and why?
The protocol says treatment should not delay extrication or transport in septic shock — how did that principle guide your decision-making?
Walk through the NEWS2 Sepsis Screening process. When is it appropriate to complete the tool, and when should you act before completing it?
How did you structure your EOC Clinician phone call? What information did you have ready? How could you improve it?
Describe the Ceftriaxone preparation process. Even though it was not indicated in this scenario, how confident are you to prepare and administer it if directed?
How did the Clinician and Driver roles work during this job? Was the division of tasks clear? What would you change?

Take-Home Messages

1. Sepsis can be subtle early — fever and confusion in an elderly patient with a known infection source should trigger immediate suspicion.
2. Hypotension + infection = time-critical. SBP ≤100 mmHg with suspected infection is septic shock until proven otherwise.
3. Trends matter more than single numbers — a falling BP and dropping GCS tell you the patient is deteriorating, even if individual readings seem borderline.
4. Early escalation saves lives — calling the EOC Clinician early and using the words “suspected septic shock” triggers the right response chain.
5. Treatment should not delay transport in septic shock — oxygen on, EOC called, then move. The definitive treatment (IV antibiotics, fluids, vasopressors) is at hospital, not on scene.
6. IM Ceftriaxone for suspected meningococcal is an EOC-directed skill — know the preparation, dose calculation, dilution, and injection technique so you are ready when directed.
7. Team coordination and clear communication is essential — the Clinician and Driver must work simultaneously, not sequentially, to minimise scene time.

Optional Scenario Variations

Use these variations to extend the session, increase complexity, or tailor to specific learning needs.

Variation 1 — Suspected Meningococcal (Young Adult)

Change the patient to a 19-year-old university student presenting with sudden onset fever, severe headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, and a rapidly spreading petechial rash on the trunk and limbs. GCS 12 and falling. The EOC Clinician directs IM Ceftriaxone administration. This variation activates the full Ceftriaxone preparation and administration skill. Calculate dose for a 75 kg patient (50 mg/kg = 3750 mg, capped at 2 g). Practise the two-site injection technique. Discuss the significance of the rash as a late sign and the urgency of antibiotic administration.

Variation 2 — Paediatric Sepsis (3-Year-Old)

Change the patient to a 3-year-old child with a 24-hour history of fever, irritability, and reduced oral intake. Now lethargic with mottled skin, weak cry, and tachycardia. Use the Paediatric Assessment Triangle: Appearance (lethargic, weak cry), Work of Breathing (grunting), Circulation to Skin (mottled, cool peripheries). Discuss paediatric sepsis recognition criteria, RDR chart observations, risk factors (age, immunisation status), and the differences in assessment approach. If meningococcal features present, calculate paediatric Ceftriaxone dose: 50 mg/kg for a 15 kg child = 750 mg IM (from one reconstituted vial, draw up 3 mL).

Variation 3 — Sepsis with SBP >100 mmHg (Non-Shock Pathway)

Modify Robert’s observations so that BP is 108/68 (SBP >100 mmHg). This places the patient on the “suspected sepsis” pathway rather than “suspected septic shock.” The management changes: still priority transport and EOC consultation, but the urgency language changes to “suspected sepsis” rather than “suspected septic shock.” Discuss: what if the BP drops below 100 during transport? How does your pathway change mid-job? This variation reinforces the importance of the SBP threshold and ongoing reassessment.

Variation 4 — Deterioration to Cardiac Arrest En Route

During transport, Robert’s GCS drops to 3, he becomes apnoeic, and the Corpuls3 shows PEA (pulseless electrical activity). The crew must now manage a cardiac arrest secondary to septic shock. Transition to the SAAS Cardiac Arrest (Adult) — AO protocol. Focus on: recognition of PEA arrest, high-quality CPR, AED analysis (non-shockable rhythm), airway management (OPA + BVM), and communication with EOC. Discuss reversible causes relevant to sepsis (hypovolaemia, hypoxia, hydrogen ions). This variation tests the crew’s ability to transition from medical management to resuscitation under pressure.
