COPD Care Plan Review

GP Clinic Practice Module

Welcome to Your COPD Care Plan Review Module

Setting: General practice clinic, scheduled 45-minute care plan review appointment

Your Role: As a practice nurse, you'll conduct a structured interview with a patient with established COPD. You'll review their respiratory status, medication adherence, exacerbation history, screening status, and identify gaps in care. Your findings will be documented for the GP to review and finalise the GPCCMP.

Learning Objectives

  1. Conduct a systematic COPD assessment including spirometry review, symptom severity, and exacerbation history
  2. Identify and screen for common COPD complications (cardiovascular disease, osteoporosis, depression, anxiety)
  3. Assess medication adherence and inhaler technique
  4. Evaluate lifestyle factors (smoking, physical activity, nutrition) and self-management capacity
  5. Recommend appropriate allied health referrals (pulmonary rehabilitation, physiotherapy, psychology) within GPCCMP entitlements
  6. Document findings in structured format for GP review and GPCCMP finalisation

Knowledge Foundation

What is a GPCCMP?

▼

GPCCMP = GP Chronic Condition Management Plan

Effective 1 July 2025, the GPCCMP replaces the previous GPMP (GP Management Plan) and TCA (Team Care Arrangement).

Key Features:

  • Eligibility: Patient has at least one chronic condition present (or likely) for 6+ months, or terminal condition
  • Preparation: Once per 12 months (if necessary) — MBS item 965 (face-to-face) or 92029 (video)
  • Review: Every 3 months — MBS item 967 (face-to-face) or 92030 (video)
  • Fee: $156.55 per preparation or review (paid to practice)
  • GP's Role: Reviews nurse's findings, makes clinical decisions, signs plan, writes referrals

Note: COPD is a chronic condition eligible for GPCCMP. No specific condition list exists — GP's clinical judgement applies.

The Practice Nurse's Role in COPD Care Plan Review

▼

What You Do:

  • Conduct structured interview with patient (45 minutes)
  • Review spirometry results and confirm COPD diagnosis (FEV1/FVC < 0.7)
  • Assess respiratory symptoms using validated tools (COPD Assessment Test — CAT)
  • Review exacerbation history (frequency, triggers, management)
  • Screen for complications (cardiovascular disease, osteoporosis, depression, anxiety)
  • Assess medication adherence and inhaler technique
  • Evaluate lifestyle factors (smoking, physical activity, nutrition)
  • Identify screening gaps (vaccinations, bone health, mental health)
  • Recommend allied health referrals (for GP to finalise)
  • Document findings in structured format for GP review

Scope Boundaries:

  • You gather information and make recommendations
  • GP makes final clinical decisions and signs the plan
  • GP writes all referral letters to allied health providers
  • Escalate red flags (severe dyspnoea, hypoxia, acute exacerbation) to GP immediately

Allied Health Under a GPCCMP

▼

Individual Visits Entitlements:

  • Up to 5 individual visits per calendar year
  • Up to 10 individual visits per calendar year for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander patients
  • Referral validity: 18 months from first session

Available Allied Health Providers:

  • For COPD specifically: Physiotherapist (pulmonary rehabilitation, exercise), Psychologist (anxiety, depression), Occupational Therapist (activities of daily living, energy conservation)
  • General: Podiatrist, dietitian, diabetes educator, exercise physiologist, speech pathologist, audiologist, chiropractor, osteopath, Aboriginal health practitioner

Referral Process:

  • GP writes direct letter to allied health provider (no EPC form required from 1 July 2025)
  • Patient takes letter to provider
  • Provider claims Medicare rebate directly

Maximising Entitlements:

  • Prioritise referrals based on patient's identified needs and goals
  • Consider phased approach (e.g., physiotherapy first, psychology if anxiety emerges)
  • Document clinical reasoning for each referral in care plan

The COPD Care Plan Review Interview

You have 45 minutes. Work through these 10 structured steps to gather comprehensive information, identify risks, and recommend appropriate support.

Steps explored: 0 / 10
1

Opening & Building Rapport

Context: You're starting the appointment. Bob is familiar with the practice but may be anxious about his declining lung function. Set a collaborative tone, explain the purpose of the review, and establish what he hopes to achieve.

Suggested Approach

Opening statement:

  • "Hi Bob, good to see you today. We're doing your regular COPD care plan review — this is a chance to check in on how you're managing your breathing, your medications, and any concerns you might have. We'll also look at your recent test results and talk about what support might help you. How have you been getting on since we last saw you?"

Listen for:

  • Overall sense of wellbeing and control
  • Any new symptoms or concerns
  • Barriers to self-management (cost, motivation, understanding)
  • Emotional state (anxiety, frustration, acceptance)

Patient Response

"Yeah, I'm alright. A bit worried though — my wife says I'm not as active as I used to be. I get puffed out just walking to the shops. I know my last test showed my breathing got a bit worse. I'm worried it's going to keep getting worse."

Key Point

Bob is expressing anxiety about disease progression and functional decline. This is common in COPD and often linked to inactivity (deconditioning worsens breathlessness). Note his concern — it's a cue to explore anxiety formally later and discuss pulmonary rehabilitation as a potential intervention.

Documentation Note

Record: "Patient reports declining activity tolerance, concerned about disease progression. Wife notes reduced activity. Expresses anxiety about worsening lung function."

2

General Wellbeing Check

Context: Before diving into COPD-specific symptoms, assess Bob's overall health, mood, energy, sleep, and any other concerns. This sets the stage for identifying comorbidities and mental health issues.

Suggested Approach

Questions to ask:

  • "How's your energy been? Are you sleeping okay?"
  • "Any new aches, pains, or health concerns since we last met?"
  • "How's your mood? Any stress or worry that's been on your mind?"
  • "Are you managing your diabetes and blood pressure okay?"

What to listen for:

  • Sleep disturbance (common in COPD due to nocturnal hypoxia, anxiety, or sleep apnoea)
  • Fatigue (may indicate poor disease control, depression, or comorbidity)
  • Mood changes (depression, anxiety)
  • Medication side effects or adherence issues

Patient Response

"Sleep's been okay, though I wake up sometimes feeling a bit short of breath. Energy's not great — I get tired easily. Mood's fine, I think. Just frustrated I can't do the things I used to. My diabetes is fine, I take my tablets every day."

Key Point

Bob reports nocturnal dyspnoea (waking short of breath) — this warrants further assessment. Could indicate: (1) inadequate disease control, (2) sleep apnoea, (3) nocturnal hypoxia, or (4) anxiety. His fatigue and frustration may reflect both physical deconditioning and low mood. Diabetes adherence is good — note this as a strength.

Documentation Note

Record: "Sleep generally okay but reports nocturnal dyspnoea (wakes short of breath). Fatigue and reduced energy. Mood stable but frustrated by functional decline. Diabetes adherence good. No new health concerns reported."

3

COPD-Specific Symptom Assessment

Context: Assess current respiratory symptoms, disease severity, and functional impact. Use the COPD Assessment Test (CAT) or similar validated tool to quantify symptom burden. Review spirometry results and compare to previous.

Suggested Approach

Key questions:

  • "Tell me about your cough — is it worse than usual? Any sputum?"
  • "How's your breathing day-to-day? Can you walk up stairs, or do you get short of breath?"
  • "Do you have a written action plan for when your breathing gets worse?"
  • "Have you had any chest infections or exacerbations since we last met?"

Administer COPD Assessment Test (CAT): 8-item questionnaire (0–40 scale). Score ≥10 indicates significant symptoms.

Review spirometry: Compare FEV1 to previous results. Decline >5% may indicate worsening disease or need for treatment adjustment.

Patient Response

"Cough's pretty much the same — bit of phlegm in the morning. Breathing's the main thing. I can walk maybe 100 metres before I get puffed out. Stairs are hard. I have the action plan, yeah. No infections since last time, but I'm worried about winter coming up."

CAT Score: 16/40 (indicates moderate symptom burden)

Key Point

Bob's FEV1 has declined from 60% to 58% — a small but measurable decline. His CAT score of 16 indicates moderate symptoms affecting daily life. His functional limitation (100m walk before dyspnoea) is significant and suggests he would benefit from pulmonary rehabilitation to improve exercise capacity and confidence. His concern about winter is appropriate — exacerbations are more common in cold months.

Documentation Note

Record: "CAT score 16/40 (moderate symptoms). Morning cough with phlegm, unchanged. Significant dyspnoea on exertion (100m walk tolerance). FEV1 58% predicted (down from 60% 3 months ago). No recent exacerbations. Concerned about winter. Action plan in place and understood."

4

Medication Review & Adherence

Context: Review current COPD medications, assess adherence, check inhaler technique, and identify any side effects or barriers to compliance. This is critical — poor technique or non-adherence significantly impacts outcomes.

Suggested Approach

Questions to ask:

  • "Tell me how you use your inhalers. Can you show me your technique?"
  • "Do you use your tiotropium every day, even when you feel well?"
  • "How often do you need your blue reliever inhaler?"
  • "Any side effects from your medications?"
  • "Any difficulty affording or accessing your medications?"

Assess inhaler technique: Watch patient demonstrate. Common errors: not shaking MDI, not holding breath, poor seal on spacer.

Assess adherence: Ask about frequency of use, barriers (cost, forgetfulness, side effects).

Patient Response

"I use the tiotropium every morning — it's part of my routine. The blue one I use maybe 2–3 times a week. Let me show you..." [Demonstrates inhaler technique — adequate but not perfect; doesn't hold breath long enough after inhalation]. "No side effects. I get them on my PBS script, so cost is fine."

Key Point

Bob's adherence to maintenance therapy is good (daily tiotropium). His reliever use is moderate (2–3 times/week) — suggests his current maintenance therapy is reasonably effective, though there's room for improvement. His inhaler technique needs refinement — not holding breath long enough reduces drug deposition. This is a teaching opportunity. His use of reliever >2 days/week could indicate need for ICS, but his low eosinophil count argues against this per GOLD 2025 guidelines.

Documentation Note

Record: "Good adherence to tiotropium (daily). Reliever use 2–3 times/week. Inhaler technique adequate but suboptimal (breath-hold duration <10 seconds). No side effects. PBS access adequate. Counselled on technique improvement."

5

Complication Screening #1: Cardiovascular & Metabolic

Context: COPD patients have high cardiovascular risk. Screen for hypertension, coronary artery disease, heart failure, and metabolic complications. Review recent BP, lipids, and glucose control.

Suggested Approach

Questions to ask:

  • "Any chest pain or pressure, especially with exertion?"
  • "Any swelling in your legs or ankles?"
  • "Any palpitations or irregular heartbeats?"
  • "How's your blood pressure been at home, if you check it?"

Observations:

  • Measure BP today (target <140/90)
  • Review recent lipid panel (if available)
  • Assess for peripheral oedema, signs of heart failure

Note: Bob's BP is 136/80 (controlled). Last lipid panel was 2 years ago — may need updating.

Patient Response

"No chest pain. No swelling. Heart feels fine. I don't check my BP at home, but the doctor said it was okay last time. I take my amlodipine every day."

Today's BP: 135/79 mmHg (well controlled)

Key Point

Bob's cardiovascular assessment is reassuring — no symptoms of angina, heart failure, or arrhythmia. BP is well controlled on amlodipine. However, his last lipid panel was 2 years ago — given his age, diabetes, and COPD (all cardiovascular risk factors), a recent lipid panel would be prudent to guide statin therapy if needed. This is a screening gap to flag for the GP.

Documentation Note

Record: "No symptoms of angina, heart failure, or arrhythmia. BP 135/79 (controlled on amlodipine). No peripheral oedema. Last lipid panel 2 years ago — recommend update. Cardiovascular risk factors: age, diabetes, COPD, sedentary lifestyle."

6

Complication Screening #2: Bone Health & Osteoporosis

Context: COPD patients have increased osteoporosis risk due to: (1) systemic inflammation, (2) reduced physical activity, (3) possible prior corticosteroid use, (4) male gender and age. Screen for bone health and fracture risk.

Suggested Approach

Questions to ask:

  • "Have you ever had a bone density scan (DEXA scan)?"
  • "Any history of fractures or falls?"
  • "How much calcium and vitamin D do you get in your diet?"
  • "Any back pain or height loss?"

Risk assessment: Bob has multiple risk factors: age 68, male, COPD, sedentary, possible prior corticosteroid use (check history).

Recommendation: Consider DEXA scan referral if not done recently.

Patient Response

"Never had a bone scan. No fractures. I don't really think about my diet much — probably not enough calcium. No back pain. I'm the same height I've always been, I think."

Key Point

Bob has never been screened for bone health — this is a significant gap. His risk factors (age, male, COPD, sedentary) warrant a DEXA scan. His dietary calcium intake is likely inadequate. This is an opportunity to: (1) recommend DEXA scan, (2) counsel on calcium/vitamin D intake, (3) encourage weight-bearing exercise (which also helps COPD). Osteoporosis screening and management should be part of his comprehensive COPD care plan.

Documentation Note

Record: "Never had DEXA scan. No fracture history. Dietary calcium intake likely inadequate. No back pain or height loss. Risk factors for osteoporosis: age, male, COPD, sedentary. Recommend DEXA scan and calcium/vitamin D supplementation. Counsel on weight-bearing exercise."

7

Complication Screening #3: Mental Health & Anxiety

Context: Depression and anxiety are common in COPD and significantly impact outcomes. Bob has already expressed anxiety about disease progression. Formally assess mood and anxiety using validated tools.

Suggested Approach

Questions to ask:

  • "Over the past 2 weeks, have you felt down, depressed, or hopeless?"
  • "Have you lost interest in things you normally enjoy?"
  • "Do you worry a lot about your breathing or health?"
  • "Does anxiety affect your daily activities?"

Use validated screening tool: PHQ-9 (depression) or GAD-7 (anxiety) — quick, evidence-based.

Listen for: Anxiety about exacerbations, fear of breathlessness, avoidance of activity due to anxiety.

Patient Response

"I'm not depressed, but yeah, I do worry about my breathing. I worry that if I go out and get short of breath, I won't be able to get home. So sometimes I just stay home instead. My wife says I'm more anxious than I used to be."

GAD-7 Score: 11/21 (mild-to-moderate anxiety)

Key Point

Bob has mild-to-moderate anxiety (GAD-7 = 11) related to his COPD. His anxiety is functionally limiting — he's avoiding activities due to fear of breathlessness. This is a classic pattern: anxiety → avoidance → deconditioning → worse breathlessness → more anxiety (vicious cycle). Pulmonary rehabilitation (which includes exercise and education) and psychology/CBT are evidence-based treatments for COPD-related anxiety. This is a strong indication for referral.

Documentation Note

Record: "GAD-7 score 11/21 (mild-to-moderate anxiety). Anxiety related to COPD and breathlessness. Avoidance of activities due to fear of exacerbation. Wife reports increased anxiety. No depression (PHQ-9 not administered as patient denies depressive symptoms). Recommend psychology referral for anxiety management and pulmonary rehabilitation."

8

Lifestyle Assessment: Smoking, Activity, Nutrition

Context: Lifestyle factors significantly impact COPD outcomes. Assess smoking status (relapse risk), physical activity (deconditioning), nutrition (weight management), and self-management capacity.

Suggested Approach

Smoking:

  • "Are you still not smoking? How long has it been?"
  • "Any temptation or stress that makes you want to smoke?"
  • "Do you live with anyone who smokes?" (secondhand smoke exposure)

Physical Activity:

  • "What activities do you do in a typical week?"
  • "How much walking or exercise do you manage?"
  • "What stops you from being more active?" (breathlessness, anxiety, motivation)

Nutrition:

  • "How's your appetite?"
  • "Any difficulty eating or swallowing?"
  • "Are you maintaining a healthy weight?"

Patient Response

"Quit smoking 10 years ago — best thing I did. No temptation now. Wife doesn't smoke either. As for activity, I don't do much. Walk to the shops maybe once a week, but that's about it. I get too puffed out. I know I should do more. Appetite's okay, weight's stable."

Key Point

Bob's smoking cessation is excellent (10 years quit) — a major strength. However, his physical activity is very low — this is a key modifiable risk factor. His inactivity is driven by breathlessness and anxiety (as identified earlier). Pulmonary rehabilitation is the gold-standard intervention: it combines supervised exercise, education, and psychological support to break the deconditioning cycle. His weight is stable (BMI 26.5) — acceptable. Nutrition appears adequate.

Documentation Note

Record: "Ex-smoker, quit 10 years ago, no relapse risk. No secondhand smoke exposure. Physical activity very low (minimal walking, <1 hour/week). Inactivity driven by breathlessness and anxiety. Weight stable (BMI 26.5). Appetite and nutrition adequate. Strong candidate for pulmonary rehabilitation."

9

Vaccination & Preventive Health

Context: Vaccinations are critical for COPD patients to prevent exacerbations. Review influenza, pneumococcal, and COVID-19 vaccination status. Identify gaps and plan updates.

Suggested Approach

Questions to ask:

  • "When did you last have your flu vaccine?"
  • "Have you had pneumococcal vaccines? Do you know which ones?"
  • "Are you up to date with COVID-19 vaccines?"

Current recommendations for COPD patients:

  • Influenza: Annual (due April–May in Australia)
  • Pneumococcal: PPSV23 (23-valent) + newer vaccines (PCV13, PCV15, PCV20) — consult current guidelines
  • COVID-19: As per current recommendations (typically annual booster)

Patient Response

"Flu vaccine last year. Pneumococcal... I think I had one a few years ago, but I'm not sure which one. COVID vaccine is up to date."

Key Point

Bob's influenza vaccine is due (last given 12 months ago; annual vaccination recommended). His pneumococcal vaccine status is unclear — he received PPSV23 3 years ago, but newer pneumococcal vaccines (PCV13, PCV15, PCV20) may be indicated depending on current guidelines and his age. This is a screening gap to clarify and address. COVID-19 status is current. Vaccination is a key preventive strategy to reduce exacerbation risk.

Documentation Note

Record: "Influenza vaccine due (last given 12 months ago). Pneumococcal vaccine status unclear (PPSV23 given 3 years ago; newer vaccines may be indicated). COVID-19 up to date. Recommend: (1) influenza vaccine today or soon, (2) clarify pneumococcal vaccine status and update per current guidelines."

10

Referrals, Goals & Wrapping Up

Context: Synthesise your findings. Identify priority referrals based on Bob's needs. Agree on goals. Document recommendations for GP to review and finalise. Ensure Bob understands the plan and feels supported.

Suggested Approach

Summarise findings: "Bob, let me recap what we've talked about today..."

  • COPD is stable but FEV1 declining slowly
  • Symptoms moderate (CAT 16); functional limitation significant
  • Anxiety about breathlessness is limiting activity
  • Bone health never screened
  • Vaccinations need updating

Propose referrals: "I'd like to recommend a few things to help you..."

  • Pulmonary rehabilitation: Supervised exercise + education to improve fitness and confidence
  • Psychology: Help with anxiety management and building confidence
  • DEXA scan: Check bone health (GP will arrange)

Agree on goals: "What would you like to achieve over the next 3 months?"

Wrap up: "The GP will review everything we've discussed and write referral letters. You'll take those to the providers. Any questions?"

Patient Response

"That makes sense. I'd like to be able to walk further without getting so puffed out. And I'd like to feel less worried about my breathing. Pulmonary rehab sounds good — will it really help? How long does it take?"

Key Point

Bob is receptive to referrals and has clear, functional goals. Pulmonary rehabilitation is evidence-based for COPD and improves exercise capacity, symptoms, and quality of life. Typical programs are 8–12 weeks, 2–3 sessions/week. Combined with psychology for anxiety, this is a strong, integrated approach. Document your recommendations clearly for the GP to action.

Documentation Note

Record: "Patient receptive to referrals. Goals: improve exercise tolerance, reduce anxiety about breathlessness. Recommended: (1) Pulmonary rehabilitation (8–12 weeks), (2) Psychology for anxiety management, (3) DEXA scan for bone health screening, (4) Influenza vaccine today, (5) Clarify pneumococcal vaccine status. Patient education provided on pulmonary rehab benefits. GP to review and finalise referrals."

Red Flags & Escalation Triggers

▼

Alert GP immediately if:

  • SpO2 <90% on room air (hypoxia)
  • Severe dyspnoea at rest or with minimal exertion
  • Signs of acute exacerbation (increased cough, sputum, dyspnoea)
  • Chest pain or signs of heart failure (oedema, orthopnoea)
  • Severe anxiety or depression with suicidal ideation
  • Suspected pneumonia (fever, purulent sputum, focal crackles)
  • Haemoptysis (coughing blood)

Bob's status: No red flags identified. Stable COPD with manageable anxiety.

Inhaler Technique Checklist

▼

For Metered-Dose Inhaler (MDI) with Spacer:

  • Shake inhaler vigorously (10 shakes)
  • Attach spacer to MDI
  • Breathe out fully (away from spacer)
  • Place spacer mouthpiece in mouth or use face mask
  • Press MDI to release one dose into spacer
  • Breathe in and out slowly and deeply 5–6 times (or until spacer stops whistling)
  • Wait 30–60 seconds before next dose (if multiple doses)

Common errors: Not shaking, not holding breath, breathing too fast, not waiting between doses.

Bob's technique: Adequate but needs to hold breath longer (≥10 seconds) to maximise drug deposition.
