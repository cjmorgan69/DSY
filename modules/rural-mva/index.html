Core Principle

Why Rural High-Speed MVAs Demand a Different Approach

Managing high-speed MVAs in rural settings requires a comprehensive understanding of trauma care principles adapted to remote environments. Extended response times, limited backup, and prolonged patient care make early recognition and prevention of the lethal triad the single most critical factor in patient survival.

⚠ The Lethal Triad
Successful resuscitation of patients with severe haemorrhage requires understanding the lethal triad — a combination of coagulopathy, hypothermia, and acidosis. Each component independently and collectively potentiates bleeding. Once established, the triad creates a self-reinforcing cycle that is extremely difficult to reverse.
🕑 Time Is Critical
In rural settings, transport times to definitive care can exceed 60–120 minutes. Every intervention at the scene must focus on stopping haemorrhage, maintaining warmth, and preventing acidosis through appropriate resuscitation — this is the foundation of Damage Control Resuscitation (DCR).
📞 Early Notification
Contact RFDS / MedSTAR as early as possible. Rural retrieval services require lead time for crew mobilisation and flight planning. Early notification can save 30+ minutes in the retrieval chain.

Knowledge Sections

Rural high-speed MVAs present unique challenges that require adaptive thinking and resource management:

  • Extended response and transport times — definitive care may be 1–2+ hours away
  • Limited immediate backup resources — you may be the only crew on scene
  • Complex extrication scenarios — CFS/SES may have extended response times
  • Weather and environmental factors — heat, cold, dust, and limited lighting
  • Communication difficulties — radio and mobile blackspots
  • Need for prolonged patient care — ongoing management during extended transport
  • Wildlife and road hazards — scene safety considerations unique to rural settings

The lethal triad describes three interrelated physiological derangements that, once established, create a self-perpetuating cycle leading to death:

🩸
Coagulopathy
Impaired clotting from loss of clotting factors, accelerated consumption, and excessive clot breakdown (hyperfibrinolysis)
❄️
Hypothermia
Reduces platelet function and clotting cascade enzyme activity — even mild hypothermia (<36°C) impairs coagulation
⚠️
Acidosis
Causes multiple negative impacts on the clotting cascade and coagulation; driven by tissue hypoperfusion and anaerobic metabolism

How They Interact

Haemorrhage leads to hypothermia (reduced metabolic heat production) and acidosis (tissue hypoperfusion). Hypothermia worsens coagulopathy. Acidosis further impairs clotting enzymes. Each worsens the others, accelerating haemorrhage and shock.

Scene Assessment

  • Scene safety evaluation (fuel spills, unstable vehicles, traffic hazards)
  • Mechanism of injury assessment (speed, vehicle damage, intrusion)
  • Number of patients and triage requirements
  • Environmental factors (weather, terrain, lighting)
  • Available resources and estimated arrival of additional support

Patient Assessment

  • Signs of severe haemorrhage (internal and external)
  • Indicators of the lethal triad developing
  • Need for immediate life-saving interventions
  • Potential for deterioration during extended transport

Resource Assessment

  • Available equipment and medications
  • Transport options and estimated times
  • Communication capabilities (radio, satellite phone)
  • Need for additional resources (RFDS, MedSTAR, CFS, SES)

Damage Control Resuscitation (DCR) focuses on preventing and reversing the lethal triad while maintaining minimal acceptable tissue perfusion.

⚛ Scope of Practice Notice
Items marked with a PARAMEDIC badge are Paramedic scope only. These interventions may only be performed with direct clinical support from a Paramedic, or once the patient arrives at a hospital facility. They are included here for informational awareness so all team members understand the full treatment pathway.

Key DCR Principles

  • Haemorrhage control — direct pressure, tourniquets, haemostatic agents, pelvic binders
  • IV/IO access & fluid resuscitation PARAMEDIC — warm IV fluids; avoid large volumes of cold crystalloid
  • Permissive hypotension PARAMEDIC — target SBP 80–90 mmHg (in the absence of TBI)
  • Tranexamic acid (TXA) PARAMEDIC — administer 1g IV within 3 hours of injury for suspected significant haemorrhage
  • Hypothermia prevention — active and passive warming, minimise exposure
  • Rapid transport to definitive care — minimise scene time; treat en route

Analgesia — Scope of Practice

📋 AO Scope — Refer to SAAS CPG: Acute Pain Management
Ambulance Officers may administer methoxyflurane (Penthrox) and oral paracetamol in accordance with the SAAS Clinical Practice Guideline for Acute Pain Management. Always refer to the current CPG for dosing, contraindications, and reassessment requirements.
  • Methoxyflurane (Penthrox) — inhaled; AO scope as per SAAS Acute Pain Management CPG
  • Oral paracetamol — AO scope as per SAAS Acute Pain Management CPG
  • IV/IM ketamine PARAMEDIC — as per SAAS CPG
  • IV/IN fentanyl PARAMEDIC — as per SAAS CPG
  • IV/IM morphine PARAMEDIC — as per SAAS CPG

RFDS / MedSTAR Coordination

  • Notify early — do not wait until you are overwhelmed
  • Provide structured handover (IMIST-AMBO / MIST)
  • Confirm landing site / rendezvous location
  • Prepare patient for aeromedical retrieval (secure lines, prepare documentation)

Field Checklist

Rural High-Speed MVA Management

1Scene Safety & Primary Survey
  • Assess scene safety — fuel spills, traffic, unstable vehicles, fire risk
  • Determine mechanism of injury — estimated speed, vehicle damage, intrusion
  • Triage — count patients, apply triage categories
  • Primary survey (DRsABCD) — address life threats immediately
  • Baseline vitals — HR, BP, RR, SpO2, GCS, temperature
2Focused History & Assessment
  • SAMPLE history (if obtainable)
  • Identify signs of severe haemorrhage (internal & external)
  • Assess for indicators of lethal triad development
  • Head-to-toe secondary survey (if time permits)
3Treatment & DCR Interventions
  • Control haemorrhage — direct pressure, tourniquets, haemostatic agents, pelvic binder
  • IV/IO access — warm fluids, permissive hypotension (SBP 80–90 if no TBI) PARAMEDIC
  • Administer TXA 1g IV if <3 hours from injury PARAMEDIC
  • Active & passive warming — blankets, warm fluids, minimise exposure
  • Analgesia — methoxyflurane / paracetamol (AO scope per SAAS CPG)
  • IV analgesia — ketamine / fentanyl / morphine as per SAAS CPG PARAMEDIC
4Reassessment, Escalation & Transport
  • Reassess vitals & response to treatment every 5 min
  • Calculate Shock Index (HR ÷ SBP) — monitor trends
  • Contact RFDS / MedSTAR early — MIST handover, confirm rendezvous point
  • Transport decision — load & go vs. rendezvous; prepare for aeromedical retrieval

Key Takeaways
1
Prevent the Lethal Triad
Control haemorrhage aggressively, maintain warmth, and avoid large-volume cold crystalloid. Every action should target coagulopathy, hypothermia, and acidosis.
2
Call for Help Early
Notify RFDS / MedSTAR as early as possible. Rural retrieval requires significant lead time. Provide a structured MIST handover and confirm your rendezvous point.
3
Load & Go
Perform only life-saving interventions on scene. Continue treatment en route. Time to definitive care is the most powerful predictor of survival in major trauma.
